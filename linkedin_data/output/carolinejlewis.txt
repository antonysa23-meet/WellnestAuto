Reshared by Caroline Lewis


Last month I shared a first of a series of posts about our "crisis cycle" and why investing in women is key to accelerating us out of this cycle. This is about the patterns in crisis cycles: 

Every crisis cycle has a pattern.
 Not in the details—those are always new.
 But in what the moment demands of a society.
Across history, when institutions lose trust and the future feels brittle, crisis eras tend to call for a few big things:
1) Shared meaning.
 People stop tolerating fragmented stories. They want a “why” big enough to organize sacrifice and risk. If leaders can’t provide it, movements will.
2) Functional institutions.
 Not perfect ones—working ones. Crisis cycles strip away tolerance for performative systems. They force reform, consolidation, and sometimes replacement.
3) Local resilience.
 When the macro feels unstable, communities rebuild the micro: mutual aid, regional supply chains, care networks, new civic glue, and local community focus. The center can’t hold unless the edges are strong.
4) A re-ranking of values.
 What looked like “nice-to-haves” becomes non-negotiable. What looked inevitable becomes optional. Crisis eras redraw the social contract.

We’re living through stacked shocks—economic uncertainty while in an AI bubble, institutional mistrust, demographic shifts, climate volatility, AI acceleration, and geopolitical stress. When shocks stack, societies don’t just want solutions.
 They want stability.
 And stability requires a different kind of build.
Here’s the part that keeps landing for me:
In every crisis cycle, the “invisible infrastructure” becomes visible—care, health, education, community cohesion, workforce participation, family economics. 
They’re the load-bearing beams.
That’s why I believe investing in women isn’t just equity work.
 It’s security work.
 Women founders are building the products and systems that keep societies coherent through turbulence: maternal health, childcare, eldercare, financial resilience, safer workplaces, mental health, community platforms, tools for dignified labor, AI that represents the whole of our society, dual-use defense tech that serves communities and not just destruction.
Crisis cycles demand that we reinforce what makes life livable.
 We’re seeing those demands now because the old scaffolding is cracking.
The question is:
 What are we choosing to rebuild—and who are we choosing to back to rebuild it?
If you’re watching these patterns too, I’d love to hear:
 What do you think this cycle is asking us to create next?

En route to celebrate my mom’s 80th birthday! If you know me you know I love and admire my mama so much!

She was the only woman scientist out of 70 male colleagues. With a doctorate of pharmacology and first career in research she has over fifty patents to her name - leading the way on diagnostic research that was the foundation for pregnancy tests and HIV/AIDS tests. She also legally challenged a big corporation on their pay and leave policy. The outcome? They permanently changed their policy, paving the way for equal pay for future generations of women and men (all of this I didn’t even know until after college).

Yet when I think of her, I just think of her as mom - someone who has loved me, guided me, and helped me be the best version of myself. She selflessly and unconditionally has given to me and my siblings with everything she has. 

She’s also shown me what it means to be multidimensional, a whole person, and someone who has an endless curiosity and drive to learn and master.

Alongside a career that was breaking barriers and somehow being a present mom to three littles (she did this with an hour commute each way), she also did so much more! 

She competed in tennis, golf, horse riding, carriage driving, and was part of the fox hunt club. We lived on a small farm where our garden was half the size of a football field. Determined to show us how to “live off the land” we canned, preserved, learned how to hunt, and identify medicinal herbs (wormwood tea really does cure a cold). She didnt just knit and hand sew our clothes - she learned how to spin yarn! Our home was a collection of various projects from refinishing furniture, weaving, sewing, leather work, medicinal salves and teas, etc! 

She paved the way for my financial literacy - something I didn’t appreciate until I was much older. Birthday presents were contributions to a roth and analyzing a stock together - what had a dividend and good ratio (this was not fun as a teen - I just wanted the coolest clothes 🙄) In my twenties she taught me how to do my taxes and till this day around tax time we can talk for hours about tax and tax strategy. 

She has never been one thing. Scientist. Athlete. Healer. Gardener. Teacher. Financial and Business Expert. Horse Woman. Mentor. Mother. Wife. Spiritual Guide. Traveler (she went to China before it was “open” 😱). She has always been all of it, with an insatiable curiosity that I don't think will ever slow down.

I think about her constantly as I raise my own daughters. I hope I can give them even a fraction of what she gave me — the sense that they are loved unconditionally, and that there is no ceiling on what a full life can be.

My mom is so much more than my mom and yet as a 41 year old she still is just that - my mom - my go to in all things life. Happy 80th!… more

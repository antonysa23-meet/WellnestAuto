About
Technology is accelerating. Human potential should accelerate with it. For more than 25 years, I've worked at the intersection of human behavior, leadership, innovation and emerging technology from the NBA to entrepreneurship, investing, authorship and advising leaders navigating change.

Today, my work centers on a question that will shape the future of work, leadership and human performance:
How do we leverage AI to increase human potential rather than diminish it?

I believe the competitive advantage in an AI-accelerated world isn't artificial intelligence. It's amplified human intelligence.

As a keynote speaker, I help leaders and organizations use AI as a thought partner to increase capacity, reduce busywork, improve decision-making, accelerate execution and strengthen the uniquely human skills that matter most: leadership, creativity, communication, connection, judgment and adaptability.

My audiences don't just hear about AI. They experience it. Through The Human + AI Collaboration Lab™, I bring audiences into a live, interactive experience where we collaborate with Agentic AI in real time to solve challenges, uncover opportunities and demonstrate what human-machine collaboration looks like in practice.

The goal isn't simply to understand AI. It's to learn how to work alongside it.

The result isn't a room full of people who heard an interesting keynote. It's a room full of leaders who leave knowing how to do something they couldn't do before they walked in.

My perspective is informed by decades of experience as a New York Times bestselling author, founder and CEO, investor, board member, former NBA executive and host of the Why Not Now? podcast. 

But more than anything, it is grounded in a simple belief: AI should deepen humanity, not dehumanize it.

The Human Edge will become increasingly valuable in an accelerated era. The future doesn't belong to humans or machines, it belongs to humans who learn how to collaborate effectively with machines.

Post

Last week, I invited an AI Agent to join me live on stage. The room took over. The real-time collaboration with agentic AI became a new keynote format. We stopped talking about AI and started using it together. 

Instead of ending with a Q&A, I projected my laptop onto the ballroom screen and invited hundreds of hospitality industry leaders to work alongside AI and I, in real time, at Stay&Gather. 

Before the event, attendees completed a survey sharing their biggest challenges, opportunities, fears and "Why Not Now?" ideas.

Using the large volume of anonymized qualitative data, we explored the results live and started solving. Not hypothetical prompts, not canned examples. Their actual challenges. Every person contributed. 

AI isn't just for productivity and automation. It's a thought partner. A collaborator.

The most interesting part wasn't the technology. It was watching the room's relationship with the technology change. It became a companion vs. a competitor.

Many entered the conversation wondering whether AI might diminish creativity. They left seeing how it can help us think better, synthesize faster, surface connections, and create more space for the uniquely human work that matters most: Taste. Judgment. Original thinking. Connection.

One attendee's survey response captured it perfectly: "Those who automate the transactional will have more time for the relational."

That's exactly what we explored together. The future isn't humans versus AI. It's humans amplified by AI. Not dehumanizing. Deepening our humanity.

Thank you to the Stay & Gather visionaries, Stacy Shoemaker Rauen and Keisha Byrd, for being willing to experiment with me. Not to mention the incredible audience collaborators. Every single person in The Boca Raton ballroom participated alongside Claude Code and I via Visual Studio Code.  

Thank you to AAE Speakers Bureau’s Amanda Miller and Marissa Hill for their partnership! 

This won't be the last time I bring agentic AI collaboration onto a keynote stage.

More and more, executives are pulling me aside and privately admitting the same thing. "I know I need to use AI at work... but honestly, I don't really use it as much in my personal life."

This is a big miss. Learning AI is like learning a language. We need to practice it in every aspect of our lives to become fluent. This is how we'll spot future opportunities. 

The people who will lead AI at work won't necessarily be the ones taking the most training courses, they'll be the ones living with it. You need to be ALL in.

Just this past week, here are a few ways I've used AI:

• Diagnose my Tahoe's Cylinder 7 misfire while stranded in rural Nebraska and personally replacing the coil pack and spark plug. (Cross-checked recommendations and diagrams via ChatGPT and Claude) 

• Analyze hundreds of audience survey responses with Claude Code to identify recurring themes and compare them against my keynote to uncover exactly where I could make the talk more relevant using the audience's own language. 

• Review a contract, identify the three highest-leverage negotiation points and map my next ask—all in under 10 minutes. 

• Help brainstorm business names and generate a logo for my 7 year old's new business where he sells a popsicle, a joke and a 3D printed item. Introducing, GigglePop! (Name and logo were done in less than 20 minutes.)

This all comes from living with the technology. That's how we develop intuition and how we begin to anticipate what's possible. AI isn't just another workplace tool, it's a "figure-it-out" companion that gives us an edge at work and home.  

I'd love to hear ways you're using AI personally, please share.… more

I've followed Intercom since 2016, the year we started BotsCrew.

Back then they were already the company to watch - they'd just raised a $50M round and were defining what customer support software looked like. That little chat bubble in the corner of half the websites you visit? - Very often it's theirs.

We built the chatbots that answered the easier questions and handed the hard ones to humans inside tools like Intercom. So I watched them closely. When you're a young founder, you pick a few companies to look up to. They were one of mine.

Today Salesforce bought them - now under new name Fin - for ~$3.6B.

And they have a very interesting story.

Three years ago Intercom's growth was dying. It dropped near zero. Then the founder came back as CEO, they bet the whole company on an AI agent called Fin, and the line turned around. From zero for a new product to ~$400M total, and growing fast again. A 15 year old company speeding up. You almost never see that.

Now look at LivePerson. Same business, but public and 15 years older. Same moment in history. But they protected the old thing instead of killing it. Down ~99% in five years, now being sold for parts (~$43M) to SoundHound AI. It's a crazy low number, as LivePerson itself acquired companies for more. 

One company destroyed its past in time. One didn't.

That's the lesson. To survive AI, you sometimes have to kill the thing that makes you money today. You don't always have to go that far - but there look to be more and more cases where it's the only way out. And most companies just can't do it.

Look at Allbirds. The internet is laughing at them right now - the shoe brand that sold off its shoes to become an AI company. Easy to mock. But is it? Betting on a future that might work is braver than slowly dying in a business that won't. That's a swing to protect shareholders and jobs. The alternative is Kodak - ~$14B in revenue in 2005, ~$1B today. They basically invented the digital camera, then sat on it to protect film. Nobody's laughing at that.

I think this is why it takes a founder and uniquely strong team culture. A hired CEO protects this quarter. A founder can set fire to his own creation, but also needs a strong team that will believe this new bold vision. Look at Elon - nearly broke betting everything on Tesla and SpaceX, and this week SpaceX went public at ~$1.7 trillion. Extreme outliers, sure. But maybe only founders can really do this.

Fin's CEO(Eoghan) wrote a blog about all of this 3 months ago - "There is exactly one way that SaaS can be saved". I read it then. It hits very differently now. Link in the comments. It only got 20 likes and 3 reposts on their corporate blog, I recommend reading it, it deserves more attention. 

Huge respect and congrats to Eoghan McCabe, Des Traynor and the whole Fin team. A masterclass. Well earned.… more

1mo • 

I accidentally built myself a tiny AI surf town and now the citizens are catching revenue leaks before my second iced matcha hits.

Here's what's actually running:

What I mean by "agent": A Claude Cowork skill I gave a name and a job to.

Some run on a schedule. Some scan for signals. Some are on-call, ready when I ask.

My girl Alex is the glue.

The town has citizens:

• Alex is my chief of staff and runs the day 
• Scout watches revenue 
• Current reads performance marketing 
• Harbor watches wholesale 
• Reef sharpens site merchandising 
• Wake processes every meeting I attend
• a few more still earning residency

One example from last week: Scout (revenue) flagged a PDP running at 0.13% conversion with a 92% bounce rate after ~750 sessions. Almost certainly out of stock in the most-bought size.

Reef (site merch) validated it before the next email hit. We avoided routing traffic to a broken page.

That used to be a manual catch. Or more often, a missed catch.

Now the DTC daily brief lands by 8:40 AM every weekday, before the day gets rocking and rolling.

The interesting part isn't the agents. It's the infrastructure between them: handoffs, context, shared memory, eventually permissions.

How I got here:

Phase one was the out-of-the-box stack.

If you're a busy operator: Pick the three systems you live in most. Connect them to Claude. Then build one skill that watches one number you actually care about.

Phase two is the unlock we're building now: A unified data warehouse as the source of truth for the org.

The agents already reading from it stopped speculating. They started reading from the real ledger.

That shift changes the credibility of every output.

Next layer: reactive systems. Agents that don't just report, they wake up when something meaningful changes.

My personal Cowork stack will always stay local and operator-focused. Things like my morning brief and my EOD recaps that pull data from meetings, emails, chats, calendars, etc. But the goal: same foundational data and context layer across personal workflows and enterprise cloud-deployed agents.

Different environments. Same source of truth.

So I'm running both tracks side by side.

Every problem I hit solo becomes a brief for the enterprise team. Every discipline they bring to scaling sharpens what I'm building personally.

Two tracks, one bet:

The future of operating a brand isn't one giant super-app.

It's a small, specialized town of agents that knows your business cold, reads from the same source of truth, and stays awake when you don't.

The future isn't fully autonomous brands. It's humans making better decisions with better systems around them.

Still building. Still learning.

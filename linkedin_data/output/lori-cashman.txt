About
I'm the Founder & Managing Partner of Visible Ventures, an early-stage venture capital fund in Boston and New York backing founders rebuilding health, wealth, and work for Gen Z. I invest where patterns are broken, not where they repeat. 

For more than a decade, I've backed founders building solutions for overlooked and / or underserved consumer audiences. Today, I am focused on investing in the systems Gen Z will inherit. We invest pre-launch / pre-seed through Series A. 

What makes us different is the platform we've built around the thesis:
The first standing Gen Z Council in venture — operating founders who help source and support our founders and an ecosystem paired with an advisory board comprised of seasoned, highly engaged, humble operators and thought leaders.

I enjoy bringing a venture mindset to my engagement in the non-profit and education sector as well. I enjoy fundraising and philanthropy and embrace opportunities to reimagine how we can engage community members in ways that strengthen connection and unlock capacity for support.  

I'm a proud mom of five Gen Zers - my unfiltered, most honest focus group, and the daily reminder for why our thesis matters. It is an economic imperative that every member of Gen Z finds a path to a paycheck, builds financial security, and has the agency to lead a healthy, fulfilling, and purposeful life.

Gen Z grew up in chaos. A recession, a pandemic, and front-row seats to the mental health crisis that followed. They were the guinea pigs of social media. They watched institutions fail in real time.

So they did what made sense: they learned to minimize risk. To wait until they feel ready. To prepare, to polish, to perfect. Many have been building resumes since they knew what one was.

But this doesn't always lead where they want to go. Especially now, as AI disrupts entry-level work. Just 30% of college graduates landed a job in the field they hoped for last year. 

To better understand the forces shaping life for young adults today, Lori Wood Cashman and I spoke to dozens of them across high school, community college, and four-year public and private universities.

We explored what we're calling Gen Z's preparedness paradox: the idea that constant preparation is, ironically, holding this generation back. 

In careers, waiting until you feel ready is often the very thing keeping you from becoming ready. Confidence doesn't come before the leap. It comes after.

Truth is, the generation used to chaos and instability has everything it takes to thrive in it — if (and only if!) they stop waiting for certainty that might never come.

1mo • Edited • 


At a time when many companies are cutting back internship programs and freezing entry-level hiring, Cloudflare is going in the other direction.

Earlier this year, they announced "Intern-et," an initiative to hire 1,111 interns (several times more than in past years) to train the next generation of talent—and notably, the application itself isn't a résumé. It requires the candidate to build something.

That move resonated with me. In our own way at Visible Ventures, Alexander Harris and I wanted to broaden Gen Z exposure to both venture and AI, so we brought on seven interns for an AI spring sprint. 

This is Part 1 of 5 in a series sharing their work.

First up: Will Frank, a freshman at Dartmouth College. Will built a Qualitative Traction Radar (QTR)—an automated intelligence system that evaluates early-stage companies by reading the real-time conversation happening around them across the internet.

Here is how it works: QTR ingests company inputs, then systematically discovers and monitors data sources across Reddit, Twitter, Product Hunt, LinkedIn, and broader web content. It applies natural language processing to extract key signals, including emotional intensity, switching behavior, product satisfaction, and recurring pain points—and turns unstructured market chatter into structured, decision-ready insights.

The practical payoff for investors: identify high-conviction opportunities earlier, ask sharper diligence questions, and track evolving company trajectories with an always-on signal layer that traditional metrics can't capture.

Will built the workflow using Claude Code through n8n, with Apify Web Scraper and Claude Web Search powering data collection, Notion AI handling notes and tables, and Google's API platform filling in the rest.

Thanks to Axios (article linked in comments) for the shout-out on our newest report, out today (linked below). This one felt personal - intersecting with my experience as a mom of Gen Zers, a mentor, convener of dozens of undergrad interns annually, and an investor in health, wealth, and upward mobility.

Rachel Janfaza and I set out to understand how young people are thinking about their educational paths, the opportunity set in front of them, and how that will inform their career choices. We structured listening sessions across the country in pre-professional high schools, the Boys & Girls Club of Boston, community colleges, and four-year universities - expecting to hear a growing pull toward the trades.

Instead, we heard something different. Young people still hold deep conviction in the four-year path through undergraduate education. What has shifted is the weight they are carrying on it. 

Employers are now telling incoming interns and new hires that, on top of everything else applicants have done to earn their offers, they are now expected to know the most about AI upon arrival - and that they will be expected to teach the rest of their colleagues how to use it. The expectations we impose on young people keep rising, even as the rules of work are being rewritten in real time. It feels like we are tossing a grenade to the other side of the generational wall. 

So we went deeper on their views of AI, and worked alongside them to shape the prescriptions (more coming from our undergrad AI interns soon). 

One answer kept surfacing: the way to counter AI anxiety is to start. Not prepare more. Not wait to be taught. Start.

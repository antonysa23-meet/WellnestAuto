Excited to share my conversation with Ariel Winton-Jones on Investing in Startups.

We talked about how AI is reshaping what “software” even means, why the best B2B companies increasingly deliver outcomes instead of just tools, and how intentional, high-conviction investing can create a real edge.

Here’s the longer breakdown:

AI is changing what “software” means. Ariel’s core insight is that the most compelling new B2B companies do not just help customers do work; they increasingly do the work for them, with AI as the intelligence layer. Her “services as software” lens is about backing products that solve the problem, not just support the workflow.

That same flexibility shapes how she thinks about business models. Ariel rejects rigid rules around pricing. Instead of forcing companies into seat-based or usage-based frameworks, she starts with how customers experience value and how they prefer to buy. Good pricing, in her view, is market-matched rather than ideology-driven.

Her investing process also reflects that pragmatism. At the earliest stages, insight can matter more than speed because the best young companies often show signals before they show spreadsheets. Ariel looks for qualitative evidence such as customer behavior, founder learning velocity, market pull, and the first signs of product-market fit.

She also sees relationship-building as part of diligence. Meeting founders before a financing process creates a better read on how they think, learn, and handle pressure, and it helps investors win deals by showing value over time rather than reacting only once a round is live.

Ariel remains focused on B2B because she finds it unusually resilient: more durable, more capital-efficient, and guided by clearer operating playbooks than many other categories.

She is also willing to concentrate earlier than many investors, once qualitative signs of product-market fit appear. That connects to her skepticism of heavy reserve strategies. Follow-ons are not just a math problem; they are an emotional one, because objectivity gets harder once you are already partnered with the founder.

More broadly, Ariel draws useful distinctions: being founder-friendly does not mean saying yes to every follow-on; solo GP does not mean making decisions alone; and the best founder support is often bespoke, trust-based, and impossible to turn into a scalable platform function.

Finally, she treats a founder’s “why” as core diligence. Motivation, origin, and market intimacy are not soft details. They are context for everything else.

I've spoken with dozens of healthcare practice owners all over the country, and without fail, every one of them says their #1 priority is finding a way to keep track of and collect their hard-earned revenue from payers. The system is broken, and the cost of a human recovering this revenue would in many cases exceed the revenue itself. So they're stuck. 

What Eliana Berger and Warren Green have built at Joyful Health is fundamentally transforming these practices' businesses by identifying what they're owed by whom and recovering it for them with a services-as-software model. They're changing the economics of caregiving by collecting the $125B of lost revenue each year and giving practices their time and energy back to focus on patient care.

This team's journey has been nothing short of inspiring - from serving handfuls of practices as their outsourced CFO, to becoming certified billers, to building a tight-knit, human-centric community among customers. And it's just the beginning. 

Congratulations on this huge milestone!

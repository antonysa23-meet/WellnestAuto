🚨 Welcome back to VC Wednesdays. This week, we have communications pro-turned-investor Masha Bucher from Day One Ventures. Read on for more. 🚨

✒️ You have a unique background. How does that make you a better investor?
Business in general benefits from clear, concise, and precise communication. As investors, you pitch yourself to your LPs, to other VCs, and to founders — and convince the companies you want to invest in about the value-add from your fund. And in communications, you pitch your companies to reporters. The nature of the work is really similar. The one big difference is that VC is heavily weighted on due diligence. We look at around 1,000 companies every month to do one investment.

✒️ What’s the most important thing you look for when investing in a startup or a founder? 
Like every other firm, we look at the product, the market, and the team. But the one thing I’d put in a different category in terms of what we look at more closely is culture. We like to understand how founders treat people in the process of building.

Some of the best founders I’ve worked with were trained as scientists and ended up with business ideas. They've also had energy and imagination — what some people call vision. It’s the ability to create worlds in your head that don’t exist yet, which you then execute on.

✒️ Last year, you started an initiative offering laid-off tech workers $100,000 to start their own companies. How’s that panned out?
We invested in seven different companies, and out of those, five have raised more capital. Most of those five already have working prototypes, and a few have paying customers and revenue. It’s quite a broad spectrum of industries ranging from fintech to climate. One example is Odyssey, an AI copilot for community health and helps connect Medicaid members to the services they need. Another is Composure, a company building software for legal teams.

✒️ Are you prioritizing AI investments more?
My very first angel investment in 2016, before Day One, was in AI, so I've always been interested. We have about 35 companies in our portfolio right now that work in some capacity with AI. But we've not rushed in to look at more, even though the pipeline has increased for sure. And that’s because with this current splash, we are sadly seeing many more lower-quality companies. One that I’m quite excited about, however, is Superhuman, and its new AI feature that lets you draft responses in seconds. For a person like me who's not a native English speaker, it's a lifesaver.

✒️ What about Web3 and crypto?
Now is actually a good time to be in Web3 and crypto because all the bad players have left. Despite all its SEC and other troubles, crypto keeps bouncing back — Bitcoin has grown like 80% in the last six months. It’s more resilient than we expected. And our thesis has always been the same — that people will always want financial alternatives — and we invest founder-first.

The deeper we got, the more we saw it: health insurance runs on administrative systems that haven't meaningfully changed in decades. Fragmented vendors that don't talk to each other. Manual processes held together with spreadsheets and phone calls. A lack of infrastructure is the core reason smarter health plans don't exist.

Health insurance costs are rising at their fastest rate in over 15 years. Employers are desperate. Members are suffering. And the innovators who want to fix it are stuck fighting the plumbing.

So we rebuilt it from scratch.

We forged a new model of the age-old Third-Party Administrator: a behind-the-scenes engine that powers claims processing, payments, and general administration for health plans. We own every piece of software in-house. We’ve built a unified system of record with all the data and context of a health plan under one roof, built to make fair, fast, and explainable decisions. This is the foundation required to automate the most complex processes in healthcare and bring trust and transparency back into the system. 

We’ve never believed in being a software layer or a point-solution. We’ve always wanted to go where the problem is and build from first principles. This took courage - and the Yuzu team brings that every day. Working on something this challenging, in an industry this complex, requires a rare kind of person, and I’m constantly humbled by the team we’ve built.

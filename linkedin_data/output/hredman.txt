About
Heather is Co-Founder & Managing Partner at Flying Fish Partners, a venture firm investing in artificial intelligence, machine learning and robotics companies. She currently serves on two corporate boards: PPL Corp. (NYSE: PPL) and privately held Coldstream Holdings Inc. 

Heather's career has spanned product, finance, operations, strategy, and legal roles. She began her career as a lawyer at a top firm working in technology. She became general counsel to two internet companies, first Photodisc followed by Getty Images, which she took public at age 32. She then moved to her first business operations role, heading product development and acquisition for Atom until the company's sale. She then spent over a decade at Summit Power in various c-suite roles, working on wind, solar, and natural gas development, new energy technologies, and project finance. Heather made the leap back into tech with an operations role at Indix before co-founding a venture firm focused on seed investments in AI companies. She is experienced in disruptive technologies, corporate development and finance, business development and intellectual property strategy, and M&A. 

Heather's current industry and civic boards include the Washington Technology Industry Association (past Chair), the Greater Seattle Chamber (past Chair), Institute of Systems Biology, the Hawthorn Club, Technology Alliance, Ruckelshaus Center, and Washington State University. 

Heather holds a JD (with distinction) from Stanford and her BA from Reed. She was named director of the Year in 2019 and to the Power 100 in 2020, 2021, and 2022 by the Puget Sound Business Journal.

Nectar Social is back in WWD, this time on the men's beauty boom.
We analyzed 1.8M conversations across TikTok, Instagram, and Reddit (Jan to Jun 2026) to build our Gen Z Men's Beauty Resonance Index. The one-line thesis: three derm staples earned the top, one brand built a cult, and one brand cracked the partner channel.

What the data showed:
→ The top 3 aren't men's brands at all. Cerave (96), The Ordinary (94), and La Roche-Posay USA (91) lead because Gen Z men appropriated unisex derm staples instead of waiting for male-coded products.
→ Dr. Squatch (#4) is the DTC exception, turning limited-edition collabs and soap-bar tier-list culture into a real community moat.
→ hims & hers (#6) cracked the partner channel by medicalizing hair loss as a treatable concern, making it the brand wives and girlfriends bring into the household.

Harry's (#8) shows that shift in motion, growing from shaving into targeted treatment. As cofounder and co-CEO Jeffrey Raider put it, the Harry's customer "thinks of it as investing in himself."

The methodology mattered here. We weighted durability over volume, scoring brands that win across multiple pathways (organic reach, Reddit authority, and partner procurement) higher than ones dominating a single channel.

The takeaway: Gen Z men aren't a monolith, and the loudest corners of the internet aren't where the category is actually being won.

Thanks to the WWD team. Congrats to others also featured cc Cetaphil, Curology, Nizoral, Procter & Gamble

A math example on scaling brilliance: In the movie The Man Who Knew Infinity, Ramanujan became a much stronger mathematician after the Hardy made him write proofs.

A math example on compounding brilliance: In the early days we had Blueprint for collaboration between mathematicians and Lean experts. This made large-scale formalization projects possible.

Verification is for better generation, for optimal design. Better code generation inspired by verification. Better engineering design inspired by verification. Specification is dynamic, a breathing living subject, grounded by AI generated tests and refined constantly by proofs.

Verified AI accelerates superintelligence, esp agentic superintelligence. Code with proof is the sustainable way to scale up agents and for humans to intervene less and less. From IDE to coding agents, we believe this is the next phase of AI coding.

What does it mean for a machine to truly understand the world — not just through language, but through sight?

Today's most advanced AI systems can write, summarize, and reason with words. But when it comes to the visual world — understanding space, objects, and relationships — they still fall short in surprisingly fundamental ways.

Dr. Ranjay Krishna is working to close that gap.

An Assistant Professor at the University of Washington, Dr. Krishna's research explores how machines might learn to think visually — not just recognize images, but reason through them.

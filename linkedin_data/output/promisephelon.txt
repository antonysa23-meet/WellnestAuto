Before I became an investor, I spent 17 years in Silicon Valley.
I built engineering teams.
I raised capital.
I bought and sold companies.
I led through uncertainty — and I learned what it really takes to build.

After my last company sold in 2021, I asked myself:

“Do I want to lead another company through the next wave of volatility?”

The answer was no.
I wanted to take what I’d lived — not just learned — and invest it in people. In Founders. In scale.

That’s how Growth Warrior Capital was born.

We’re not here to chase timing or brag about picking.
We’re here to build beside the most dangerous CEOs.
To help them drive revenue, scale efficiently, and thrive through market chaos.

Because today’s Founders don’t just need capital.
They need partners who’ve lived the black swans, not just observed them.

I call GWC "operator-led." That word can turn into a hollow badge just as easily as "human-powered" is becoming one in marketing right now.

Ford proved why the operator-led distinction matters. The automaker spent three years hiring back 350 veteran engineers, the ones its own people call "gray beards," after leaning too hard on automated quality systems. 

The company acknowledged that its previous over-reliance on AI to replace these workers cost it “hundreds and hundreds of millions of dollars” in recalls and warranty repairs.

AWS made a related bet this week. A $1 billion investment to embed engineers directly inside customer teams, not to advise, but to build alongside them until the organization can operate on its own.

The stated reason is telling: customers have moved past exploring what AI can do, and most of them still can't make it core to how they work without someone in the room who's done it before.

Ford didn't abandon automation either. It kept building it the whole time. The actual lesson is narrower than "AI failed." Capability and readiness are two different things, and most of this year's AI data measures only the first.

That's the same test I try to hold myself to. Not whether "operator-led" sounds right on a slide, but whether it's actually true of how GWC works, week to week, company to company.

I wrote about this, Ford, the AWS announcement, the data behind it, and what we built The Bang around, in this week's newsletter.

The CEO of Path Robotics said something a few months back that's stuck with me since.

A demo that works 70% of the time might get a standing ovation in a pitch meeting, but it gets rejected on a factory floor, where manufacturing needs something closer to 99%, since the cost of being wrong is so high.

That gap between impressive and trustworthy is where we spend almost all of our time right now, and robotics is just one place industrial AI shows up.

A demo answers a narrow question: can the system handle the use case someone chose to show? That question gets answered in a pitch meeting, and pitch meetings are the least useful place I see this category right now.

What actually tells me something is whether a team is obsessed with the part that never makes the deck, the months of work that get a system from impressive to something a buyer will trust enough to run without anyone watching.

That obsession is what I mean by "dangerous founder." Not reckless, not chasing a headline. Dangerous because they'd rather spend a year on the part nobody sees than a week on the part that gets applause, and dangerous because they know exactly what that year buys them.

The edge cases and failure modes nobody else bothered to solve become the thing a competitor can't copy in a quarter, which is a different kind of leverage than a good demo ever gives you.

It also tells you something about timing. The companies with that obsession usually look unimpressive from the outside for longer than a founder with a good demo can stomach, which is exactly why most investors never find them.

Being operator-led means we know what that looks like from the inside, since the real work rarely looks like anything worth funding until it suddenly is.

Most of what we're watching right now doesn't have a name you'd recognize yet. That's exactly where we want to be.… more

What to do when a customer looks you dead in the eye and says: "When the marginal cost of your software is less than a penny, shouldn't I pay less?"

He's not wrong. He's just early.

This is the gauntlet clients and customers are throwing down — taking pricing and "subscription-level" control back from service providers and tech vendors. 

And here's what most people miss: when marginal cost goes to zero, cost-based pricing dies with it. The only thing left worth paying for is the outcome.
We called this two years ago — customers don't want to pay SaaS annuities anymore. Now it's here.

Which is why Keenya T. Justice, Esq., MBA is one of the few attorneys I've seen actually get it. Her take mirrors what we said on stage a few weeks ago at the Growth Warrior Capital BANG event: it's about outcomes, not outputs — and the worst basis of all is inputs. By-the-hour pricing may not be done, but its core assumption is certainly under threat.

That early understanding - that value lives in the connection between disciplines—directly informed how I approached AI at TJF. We did not view our systems as separate categories of work. We viewed them as parts of a single operating environment. The goal was never to layer AI on top of existing tools, but to allow AI to see across workflows so it could actually support how the firm operates in real time. 

What I have learned is that the firms that will benefit most from AI are not the ones with the best individual tools. They are the ones with the most integrated systems. At dinner recently, a colleague told the table, with total confidence, that there would be no developers left in 10 years.

Nobody had their facts wrong. Junior hiring is down, and AI writes code faster than most entry-level engineers can, but I pushed back anyway.

Not because the data is wrong. But because my colleague was describing a developer the way you'd describe one if you'd never actually worked closely with a good one.

The best engineers spend their time reading someone else's codebase, deciding what's worth keeping, and rebuilding the foundation while the lights stay on. The way I see it, the job was never really about typing code; it was about judgment under constraint, and that part isn't going anywhere.

In software, a bad judgment call usually shows up as a bug. In the industries I invest in, manufacturing, logistics, infrastructure, defense, and agriculture, it shows up as a physical failure.

It's also why I stopped asking teams whether they use AI. Everyone says yes now, so the question no longer tells me anything.

What I ask instead is who'd notice if the person doing the real thinking on that team walked out the door.

Most companies making the FDE hire right now are asking the wrong question first.

The conversation in every LinkedIn post, every WSJ article, and every announcement from Anthropic and OpenAI is about whether the role is worth $250,000 to $300,000 in base salary.

It’s almost always preventable if the right foundation gets built before the job description goes up.

The most valuable data a factory owns is usually written on a post-it note, taped to a clipboard, or carried in one operator's head. No system tracks it. No one can see it. Joanna C. Cooper, who ran a 1,700-person operation at Daimler Truck, said it plainly: the biggest obstacle to digital manufacturing is paper and post-it notes.

That sentence is the whole opportunity. Once that knowledge becomes visible, it can train AI employees and hand your best operator a tool that makes them ten times more effective. The hard part was never the software. It is building the product around what that proprietary data finally makes possible, and pricing against the outcome the operator was already trying to deliver.

This was the line running underneath every conversation at THE BANG on May 19, our industrial AI salon, where we put the builders, buyers and backers of the 4D Economy in one room at the GWC offices in San Francisco.

Erik Nieves of Plus One Robotics has spent 25 years in industrial robotics and still calls himself a human exceptionalist. His position: robots do work, humans rule. Industrial value gets delivered through the operators, the people pounding steel and driving picks per hour. The machines serve them.

Matthew Kael Swanson built StaffAI to be paid on commission, not seat license. He drew the line the rest of the market is still circling. In an agentic world, you get paid on outcomes. Outputs are not outcomes.

The pressure under all of it is labor. The average farmer is 58. Roughly 11,400 people retire every day, and the industrial replacement rate runs five to two. You cannot recruit your way through math like that. You rebuild around what AI makes possible, or the work stops getting done.

The operators doing that rebuild refuse to sell the artifact. They sell the result, and they own what happens when it fails. That is the harder commitment, and it is the one we underwrite.

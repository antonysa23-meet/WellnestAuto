About
Mallun Yen has devoted the past 20+ years to changing the status quo and shifting industry behaviors, as an enterprise executive, founder, operator, and category-creator. These experiences laid the groundwork for Operator Collective, a venture fund and community she founded in 2018 to make venture accessible to a group critical to a startup’s success but largely left out: top operators who have built and scaled the world’s most successful tech companies and are now driving AI transformation in and for their companies. Portfolio companies include Midi, Ramp, Waymo, Vercel, Lovable, ASI, and more.

She is currently a member of the Board of Directors of Everpure (formerly Pure Storage NYSE: P) Board of Directors. Mallun was named to the Forbes 50 Over 50: Money and is the first Fortune Most Powerful Women Summit guest co-chair to serve two consecutive terms.

Prior to founding Operator Collective, Mallun was at the forefront of other category-creating companies also rooted in community, including venture-backed RPX ($0-$100M and IPO in 3 years), SaaStr (world’s largest b2b software community), ChIPsNetwork.org (5,000+ members and 25+ chapters around the world). Mallun was also VP of Worldwide Intellectual Property at Cisco Systems.

Just because you led through the last tech transitions doesn't mean you know how to lead in the age of AI.

That was one of many messages that came through loud and clear when we gathered 75 senior tech executives in one room last week for what's been missing from the AI conversation: unfiltered operator truths about what enterprise AI transformation actually looks like inside their companies and for themselves.

As Erica Ruliffson Schultz put it: "What will define success going forward is our ability to learn. Each of us has to be willing to go back to being a beginner."

Our inaugural AI Transformation Exchange, co-hosted by legendary operators Erica and Claire Hughes Johnson (both founding OpCo LPs and board members), brought together leaders from Anthropic, Stripe, Vercel, Databricks, Toast, Box, Confluent, Microsoft, Amplitude, and more. We anchored it with our first-ever OpCo Intelligence State of AI Transformation research report (announced today in Axios by Madison Mills), surveying 123 senior operators with a median 22 years of experience.

Here's a snapshot of what we heard and what the data showed:

✔️ 77% are actively executing on AI initiatives. Our operators shared how they are making it happen.
✔️ 69% say AI is reshaping talent profiles and job roles. AI is changing what people do long before it changes how many people do it.
✔️ 67% say productivity is AI's biggest benefit. Hear how those gains are being measured (or not). 

They also share what's holding AI back from true enterprise adoption and what solutions they are looking for.

This is the space Operator Collective 🔆 was built to fill. Our 250+ Operator LPs are the leaders who scaled their companies through SaaS and cloud and are now relearning what it takes in an AI-native world. Our portfolio companies are building the tools these operators use and evaluate every day.

The full blog post digs into the themes with perspectives from Elena Gomez, Kieran Snyder, Jeanne DeWitt Grosser, Maia Josebachvili, Kate Jensen, Spenser Skates, Yanbing Li, Nancy Wang, Olivia Nottebohm, Bijal Shah, Christina Liu, Colleen McCreary (Wheeler), and Anu Bharadwaj.

The hype is everywhere. Operational reality is harder to find. Much more from us soon!

OpCo was built on a simple thesis: companies succeed because of the people who build them. In this AI transformation moment, that's never been more true - of the founders we back, of our Operator LPs driving change inside companies like Anthropic, Databricks, OpenAI, NVIDIA, and Stripe, and of our own internal team.

We've always been lean, and I like to think we've always punched above our weight. What has let us do that is the operating foundation we've built: data-first from day one. We just wrapped an offsite in Austin last week where the entire team aligned on how we're building on that foundation to work better with AI - what's working, what we're testing, and where we're pushing ourselves to change.

A big part of that foundation is Anna Jacobson, my third hire at OpCo. Bringing on a data scientist in venture six years ago was an unusual call, but one of the wisest we made. Anna has been obsessive about keeping our data clean, making sure what we "know" is actually reliable, and building workflows that hold up. That discipline is what lets us be AI-forward now. We’re formalizing AI enablement as a core part of how we operate, and Anna is officially adding a new hat as Chief AI Enablement Officer to what she’s been quietly doing for years: building internal systems that make the whole firm sharper, help each of us push on what's possible, and share what we’re learning back out to our community.

We're doing this with a structural advantage we've had for years. Most people observe enterprise AI adoption from the outside. We have a direct line to the operators inside the companies where it's actually happening - the same operators who buy the tools our portfolio companies are building. That signal shapes how we track categories, evaluate founders, and help our portfolio navigate enterprise sales.

The AI shift is also changing the talent equation in ways the market hasn't fully internalized yet. Companies are scaling at unprecedented speed, and learning velocity and the ability to redesign how work gets done are becoming table stakes. That's why I'm excited to share that Lynn Stepovich Carter has joined us as our first Operating Partner, Talent & Growth. Lynn is an exceptional talent leader, a longtime friend (since high school!), and an LP. She helped Reed Hastings build Netflix engineering through hypergrowth, and partnered with Jay Kreps and Erica Ruliffson Schultz as Confluent built its executive team from early days through post-IPO. She'll advise founders as they build teams for an AI-native world and help us deepen our operator community.

We're building genuine operational fluency ourselves - and developing a first-person understanding of the exact questions enterprise buyers are wrestling with. The same questions our portfolio companies are working to answer. Firsthand experience makes us better partners to the founders building those answers.

Not every company that got a big valuation in 2021 is going to make it out the other side - and frankly, some of them shouldn't. But there are a handful that are doing the real work to earn what comes next and have been steadily growing in value since. Hightouch is squarely in that group.

We first backed Hightouch at the Series B (rare for us) with a supporting check, when Kashish Gupta, Tejas Manohar, and Josh Curl were building what was then called Reverse ETL - a way to get customer data out of the warehouse and into the marketing and sales tools that teams actually use every day. The category wasn't fully formed yet, but the problem was real and the conviction of the founders was clear. By Series C in 2025, they had grown into the leading Composable CDP, shipped AI-powered features, and had hundreds of customers like Ramp and Spotify running serious marketing programs on the platform. We were excited to move them from a supporting investment to a core one when we joined that round.

Today they announced a $150M Series D led by Goldman Sachs and Bain Capital Ventures (BCV) at a $2.75B valuation, with revenue now over $100M, growing more than 100% in each of the last two years with real enterprise contacts, expansions and durable sustainable revenue. And what they've built now goes well beyond the CDP category they helped define. Their agentic marketing platform gives AI agents the ability to research audiences, generate on-brand creative, and execute campaigns across email, advertising, SMS, and web - all running directly on top of a company's own customer data, with their brand context, inside their existing systems and guardrails. 

This latest product is big. The conversation we keep hearing from our Operator LPs at companies like these has shifted from "show me what AI can do" to "does this work inside our actual systems, and can I trust the output." That's exactly the problem Kashish and Tejas built Hightouch to solve.

Congrats to Kashish, Tejas, Josh, Cherry M., Brian Kotlyar and the entire Hightouch team on this milestone, and to Darren Cohen and the Goldman Sachs team for leading the round (and thank you to Anu Bharadwaj for the initial intro)! 

You’ve come so far in such a short time, with lots of room ahead. Let’s go Hightouch! 🚀… more

69% of the AI tools named in our survey of 123 enterprise operators were cited only once. My take: that's actually good news for founders! Operators are experimenting, often with multiple tools in parallel, and the enterprise market is wide open -- if you can actually get to them (they say lack of time is one of their biggest obstacles).  And build what they actually need.

Many fast-growing AI startups are bottoms up, PLG-type with strong sign-ups but real churn. They know enterprise is the path to durable revenue, but it's a different motion. So what do enterprise operators actually want?

- Tools that plug into existing systems and unify fragmented data
- AI that takes action, not just surfaces recommendations
- Deep domain expertise in their specific workflows

And 70% have no formal way to measure AI's impact. I wrote about the implications for founders in Fortune, including what's changed about selling into enterprise, and what's still fundamental.

Huge thank you to Anna Jacobson for spearheading our inaugural OpCo Intelligence AI Transformation Report and making our firm data-forward so we can get a direct line into how our Operator LPs are actually operating and what they need.

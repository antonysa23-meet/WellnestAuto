About
I build the infrastructure that turns promising ideas into fundable companies.
 
My career has lived at the edge of emerging technology and institutional reality. I started as a journalist covering government and environmental issues, then clerked for two federal judges — experiences that shaped how I think about power, incentives, and unintended consequences.
 
I went on to build two startup-focused law firms from scratch, advising founders on IP, employment, regulatory risk, and commercial strategy across more than 100 clients. I later co-founded and served as General Counsel of a venture-backed global sports technology company, integrating product development with international data privacy strategy while helping raise nearly $6M in early capital.
 
I co-created Lunar Startups — an accelerator incubated within American Public Media — and designed a milestone-driven system to move early-stage founders from concept to investment readiness. As an expert legal consultant to the United Nations, I helped translate international standards into national legislation.
 
Today I'm a GP at Tundra Ventures, where I co-manage a pre-seed fund investing in health, wealth, and resilience infrastructure — sourcing deals, structuring investments, and supporting a portfolio of 13+ companies as they raise subsequent rounds.
 
Through Base Layer, I provide 1:1 business consulting to first-time, domain-expert founders getting ready to raise their first round — covering governance architecture, cap table structure, IP strategy, and fundraising infrastructure. Together, we build the investor strategy and business infrastructure to move investors from "no" to "heck yes!". 
 
Across all of it, the throughline is the same: translating ambiguity and complexity into durable systems that allow innovation to scale.

When I was first raising money as a co-founder of a Minneapolis-based sports tech startup, I truly didn’t understand the investor mindset. 

As a founder, you are so focused on your one idea, and you see the potential for greatness, and you don’t understand why people JUST DON’T GET IT and why can’t they WRITE A DAMN CHECK ALREADY????

But investors are seeing the startup ecosystem with a wide lens. They are seeing dozens, hundreds, even thousands of companies. 

That means:  Your idea and vision will NEVER be as important to them as they are to you. They have plenty of other things competing for their time and attention. 

So the trick is to figure out how to:  

❄️ Stand out (in a good way), 

❄️ Not unintentionally disqualify yourself from consideration, and 

❄️ Start building a good ongoing relationship and a good reputation

I wrote a guide for founders just starting out on the harrowing, Oregon Trail-like fundraising journey. You need the right supplies, the right support, a thick skin, and a healthy mindset. 

Consider this your friendly general store, and stock up! Link in comments.

P.S. My beloved Oregon Trail game was invented in Brooklyn Center, Minnesota (a first-ring suburb of Minneapolis, and my sister's workplace!). You can support immigrant families affected by the ICE occupation with food boxes through CAPI, linked below.

Twelve years ago (back when Obama was starting a second term, Miley rode a wrecking ball, and the GOT Red Wedding shocked us all 💀🗡️), I started obsessing over one question:

❓How do you equip very early, first-time founders with the most effective and efficient foundation for a successful startup trajectory?

From building my first business (a startup law firm) to becoming a venture-backed tech founder …to building Lunar Startups and now leading Tundra Ventures with my incredible partners, I’ve been iterating this system nonstop. 

🤩 Many of you reading this have been part of my process! 🤩

Working alongside 77 Lunar companies (💛), I built the MVP:  a milestone-based business system + killer digital support platform + tailored coaching. We called it Lunar Everywhere.  

❄️Then, we baked those milestones and coaching straight into Tundra’s due diligence and CEO support model. 

Today, for the first time, I’m opening public access to a fresh version of this system. It’s called Base Layer…because it’s the warmth you need to survive (and thrive) in the frozen startup tundra.

⏩Alongside my work at Tundra, I’ll be opening up a *very small* number of 1:1 consulting spots in Q1 for founders who want to build an investable company from the inside out – think ex-corporate domain experts with a fab product, real expertise, and zero desire to wander blindly through the startup wilderness.

The lighting was rough, but my fellow Venture Summit West investor judges were🔥🔥🔥! We listened to almost 100 early-stage pitches over two days. Here are some takeaways for founders:

💎 The need to pare down your story into a clear, simplified-yet-compelling narrative is MORE critical in the age of AI than it ever has been before.

Less than a week later, I only remember a handfuls of the pitches I saw. And it's because they were simple and clear. (I won't forget the one about stabbing blindly into someone's brain for quite some time.) 

The goal of a 3-min pitch is to generate enough interest for a next conversation. That's it. It's not to tell your entire story! 

💎 If you are giving a three-minute pitch, practice toward 2:45 and don't go over. Very few people finished cleanly in the time allotted, and it wasn't the best look.

💎 Your most compelling pitch details might be hiding in your conversation. I talked to some of the founders before I heard their pitches, and several of them left out what I thought had been the most interesting parts of our conversation.

💎 Don't forget the power of storytelling. Founders who had a personal story to tell - whether about themselves or a customer - were always more memorable. You can almost always layer a quick narrative over your pitch.

💎 Remember that although most investors aren't listening to 100 pitches in two days, they are always fielding a constant stream of incoming knowledge. Use that understanding when you create your pitch -- be simple, be clear, be memorable! As my friend Julie Kucinski says: "CEO strong, 10-year-old simple."

The NIH SBIR/STTR solicitation dropped & the budget caps are WILD. Founders can request up to $700k for Phase I. Let me break down how I'd approach this cycle if I were applying in Sept 2026...

NIH has significantly increased SBIR budget caps across many Institutes, with several now supporting up to $700K for Phase I and $3M for Phase II awards. 

But, WATCH OUT. 

Most founders are going to look at these numbers, get dollar signs in their eyes, and start loosing their minds. 🤯 That's exactly why many of them will approach this cycle the wrong way. 

Instead of asking, "How much money can I snag?" here's what I'd do if I were applying in Sept. 2026:

1️⃣ Start NOW. 
Expect application volume to be high especially due to the missed Jan. and April 2026 deadlines. Many founders have been waiting for NIH funding opportunities to reopen, and they're not waiting until the last minute.

2️⃣ I wouldn't sit this cycle out because it's "too competitive."
I've heard this argument a lot. Competition will be fierce, but NIH still has A LOT of funding to deploy. In fact, budgets are larger than they've been in years. More applicants does not automatically mean fewer opportunities.

3️⃣  Be strategic. 
Don't submit as many proposals as possible. That would look horrible on your end. Instead, focus on the right Institute, the right fit, and the strongest story. 1 well-positioned application is worth far more than 3 rushed ones.

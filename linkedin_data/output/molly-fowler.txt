1yr • 

One of the most unexpected outcomes of Dorm Room Fund has been just how powerful the alumni of our investing program have become to the longevity of our fund. Michael T. (CEO of Cursor/Anysphere) -- who just announced his massive $100M series B at a $2.5B post-- is no exception.

When I joined DRF in 2019, we had signs of real product-market-fit for our founder product: we had helped some really incredible companies like Shield AI, Athelas, FiscalNote, Brooklinen, Dagne Dover, and RapidSOS and others get their start. But our alumni community was really in its nascent stages (kudos to Bruno Faviero and Rei Wang who had shepherded that effort).

Today, those brilliant 30-ish humans that we select each year to join our student team have become a core part of why DRF will be a long term success. Not just because they're ridiculously talented (our acceptance rate is lower than even their most elite schools), and go on to do ridiculous things (start companies, invest as partners of funds like Sequoia Capital, Bessemer Venture Partners, Andreessen Horowitz, Accel and many funds of their own making, and start design studios that help us with fundraising decks, Albert Dong), but because they invest in our community. By hanging out with our current students; by helping with diligence; by supporting our massive recruiting effort every fall; by investing in each other, hiring in each other, and starting companies with each other; and by mentoring and advising our founders.

But one really special thing has started to happen at DRF, and that's what I wanted to share today: DRF alumni are starting AMAZING companies and they're bringing us along for the ride. It started when I was there with folks like Toni Oloko at Dandy and Will M. at ScienceIO who brought us on to their cap tables as well as letting us support them as founders, and has continued with amazing folks like David Mackanic at Anthro, Jianna Liu, Grace Gerwe, Riley Soward, Nigel Coelho, Daniel B., and Sean Nissenbaum. The success of their companies directly impacts the long term success of our fund in a wholly new way.

That's why I'm so very excited about Michael, and Cursor's announcement-- Michael is proving yet again that DRF helps mint extraordinary outcomes, and that we're a part of their longterm journeys as humans & builders.

So congrats on a ridiculous round, Mike! Glad it only took 3 years for Thrive Capital, a16z, and Benchmark to figure out how talented you are. :)

Today, we're sharing a new state of the art for computer use.

Our system holds the two highest verified scores on OSWorld, the standard benchmark for AI agents that operate a computer like a person: 83.6% using Claude Opus 4.7 and 81.5% using Claude Sonnet 4.6. The human baseline is 72.4%.

Computer use is becoming the new primitive for automation. An agent that can operate software the same way people do isn't limited to the integrations someone built in advance – the action space is everything on the screen.

The benchmark measures whether the agent can do the work. In production we've learned the harder problems are knowing what the work is, keeping the agent's actions bounded, and proving the work was done correctly rather than merely plausibly.

Today, we’re announcing Adaptive’s $50M Series A led by Felicis and our $10M Seed led by Bain Capital Ventures (BCV).

There are a lot of AI health companies. But care can’t be delivered through a screen. Real care happens physically, between a clinician and a patient. And as a country, we don’t deliver enough of it. We underutilize our clinicians.

The barrier is administrative.

Post-acute home health, skilled nursing and physical therapy, is a rapidly growing $140B industry that spends 40% of revenue on admin costs and fails to treat two out of every five patients referred to it. We have enough clinicians to treat all of those patients. The admin burden and the care coordination problem are what stand in the way.

That's the problem Adaptive exists to solve. We're an AI-native healthcare provider replacing administrative operations with a unified AI operating system which lets us service visits at a lower cost structure which no one else in home health can match.

We use the margin that AI unlocks to pay clinicians more, deliver higher-quality care across every patient, and be the best partner for health systems, clinics and insurers because we can treat their neglected patients. We don't play reimbursement games or lean on short-term government programs. Instead our mandate is simple: drive admin cost to zero, and recycle those savings into a compounding flywheel that lets us serve the entire $140B market.

Since our 2025 Seed raise, we've delivered over 100,000 patient visits. Today we work with over 500 healthcare organizations across Texas, including every major Texas health system, and by the end of the year we'll be live in several new states. We've become a locus of technical talent unprecedented in healthcare, letting us operate at the velocity of an AI company.

Thank you to my co-founders Logan Stinson, Ryan Tolsma, and Hunter Stinson BSN, RN, the entire Adaptive team for building this with me, and our investors across Felicis, Bain Capital Ventures (BCV), Optum Ventures, Sunflower Capital, BoxGroup, SV Angel, Constellation, Dorm Room Fund, and Conviction for believing in us. I’m excited for what’s next.

Our Summer Tracks program returns! Entering its eighth year, this six-week opportunity remains one of the most beloved entry points into VC. Applications are open and reviewed on a rolling basis — final deadline Sunday, May 10th at 11:59 PM PT.

We have five tracks, each designed for a different student:

🌈 PRISM is for LGBTQ+ students, because venture thrives when every voice has a seat at the table. Peter Thiel co-founded PayPal and backed Facebook; Martine Rothblatt led investments in Stripe, Duolingo, and Lovable; and Tim Cook ... is Tim Cook.

👩‍💻 Women's Investor Track is for early-career women and women-identifying students breaking into VC. Some of the sharpest minds in venture are rewriting the rules one fund and one deal at a time.

🧠 PhD Track is for STEM PhDs who want to turn research into product. If you're a PhD thinking about building or commercializing, this track helps you develop the mindset, skills, and community to scale your work — and raise your first round.

🌄 Frontier is for students who want to break into VC from outside the traditional mold. Pejman Nozad forewent a degree entirely, while Chris Sacca was minted in the SUNY system and then chose a law degree over an MBA. Your path doesn't have to look like anyone else's.

I don’t think you should go into venture capital. Or at least, that’s the advice I find myself giving more often than not.

I spent 6+ formative years at First Round Capital before leaving to operate at Clay as Chief of Staff (joining as employee ~85 → 375), so I’ve seen both sides of the table. First Round was an incredible place to grow up professionally, and I learned from some of the sharpest, most thoughtful people in the industry. 

Because of that, I get asked for career advice pretty regularly, usually from people thinking about VC. After a lot of coffee chats and calls, I’ve noticed the same pattern: people are drawn to venture without really understanding the nature of the job.

Early-stage venture comes with long feedback loops, unpredictable outcomes, intense competition, and more. If you’re considering it, I wrote more about why it might not be the right path - link below.

P.S. If you're a VC looking to operate - come join us at Clay! We have folks from Sequoia, Thrive, Insight, Lerer Hippeau & more :)

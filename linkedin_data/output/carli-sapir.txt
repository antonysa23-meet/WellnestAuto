For a long time, the conversation around women’s health investing centered on one question: why does women’s health matter?

That conversation is finally changing.

The more interesting question now is how we invest in women’s health—and whether our investment frameworks are actually sophisticated enough to capture the opportunity.

In this piece for Forbes, I argue that many current investment theses still treat women’s health as a niche category rather than what it really is: a massive, system-level opportunity to rethink care delivery, prevention, and longitudinal health data.

The next generation of companies in this space won’t just build point solutions. 

They’ll build integrated care models, richer clinical datasets, and infrastructure that supports women’s health across the lifespan.

That’s where the real value creation will happen.

Male Birth Control is a $10B+ Ghost Market. 

I co-wrote this Fast Company piece with Stasia Obremskey, Managing Partner of Foreground Capital, on why male birth control represents one of the most overlooked opportunities in healthcare.

Amboy Street Ventures and Foreground Capital are proud to back Contraline, which is developing a non-hormonal, long-lasting, and reversible male contraceptive. 

For decades, contraception has placed the physical, medical, and economic burden almost entirely on women—while men have had just two options. That imbalance is finally starting to change. For the first time, multiple male contraceptives are in clinical trials, and demand from men is far stronger than most people realize.

This isn’t just about a new product—it’s about shared responsibility, expanded choice, and unlocking an entirely new healthcare category.

At JPM Health, I was on the mainstage speaking about investing in women's health and Amboy Street Ventures co-hosted an event on AI in Women’s Health. It was a conversation that went beyond “what’s possible” to focus on what actually works—and what responsibility comes with AI at scale.

We covered:

How ŌURA is using AI on top of deep physiological data to generate insights that are specifically meaningful for women with Jill Angelo

How AI will reshape care delivery and reimbursement—and why measurable outcomes and ROI matter as much as innovation, as framed by Kulleni Gebreyes

How founders and investors can ensure AI doesn’t compound bias in women’s health data, and who should ultimately be accountable, shared candidly by Amanda Ducach, MBA of Ema

Grateful to Janet Foutty for opening the discussion with perspective shaped by decades of systems-level leadership, and to Adrianna Samaniego (she/her/ella) for closing with an investor lens on where durable data moats and defensible business models will emerge.

Thank you to Jill Angelo, Kulleni Gebreyes, and Amanda Ducach for such a substantive, honest conversation—and to Cherryrock Capital for partnering with us to convene it. Thank you Stifel Financial Corp. for sponsoring and Deloitte for providing the venue. 

AI will absolutely play a defining role in the next chapter of women’s health. 

The real question is whether we deploy it in ways that truly improve outcomes, equity, and access.

A powerful reminder of what’s possible when philanthropic capital is used to cut through barriers and take bold, system-level shots at some of the most urgent challenges in women’s health.

I'm incredibly proud to announce that Amboy Street Ventures co-led Nest Health's $22.5M Series A alongside 8VC and Blue Venture Fund! 

At Amboy Street Ventures, we invest in companies that fundamentally expand access to care for women and families, and Nest exemplifies this mission by bringing comprehensive medical, behavioral, and social care directly into the home. What excites me most about Nest isn't just the market size—it's their proven ability to solve one of healthcare's most intractable problems: care fragmentation for our most vulnerable families. 

Nest's performance validates everything we believe about value-based care done right: huge ROI for payer partners, +98 NPS scores, and 60% reduction in ER utilization. They've cracked the code on whole-family care economics while serving the Medicaid population that traditional healthcare has systematically underserved. Rebekah E. Gee MD MPH and Rebecca Kavoussi aren't just building a healthcare company; they're architecting a new care delivery paradigm that treats health as a household asset, not an individual liability.

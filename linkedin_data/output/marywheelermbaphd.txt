Share the good news.

A new medicine for pancreatic cancer patients just offered incredible new hope — and created a historic moment this past week in the extended cancer community.

I was inspired to write this after speaking with a friend who does not work in biopharma or early-stage venture. She asked what I had seen recently in my work that felt inspiring.

It struck me that while my inbox and LinkedIn feed were full of inspiring data from the American Society of Clinical Oncology (ASCO) (pronounced "as-co") meeting and extraordinary progress for cancer patients, hers were not.

That matters. It is a privilege to see glimpses of the future for cancer patients. It is also our responsibility to help share that progress beyond our own sector, in language people can understand.

At ASCO in Chicago, Phase 3 data were presented for Revolution Medicines’ daraxonrasib in metastatic pancreatic cancer — a disease commonly driven by mutations in RAS, one of the most important and historically difficult causes of tumor growth. 

When the data were shown, the packed room stood up. See link below to a video on instagram to make it easier to share:

https://lnkd.in/gC5yPNwV

Thousands of oncologists, nurses, drug developers, researchers, investors, patients, and advocates rose to their feet. That almost never happens. It was a historic moment and a moment of gratitude.

Pancreatic cancer is one of the hardest cancers to meaningfully move. For patients and families, the diagnosis too often comes late, with too few options and too little time.

The RASolute 302 data showed a meaningful survival benefit — median overall survival of 13.2 months versus 6.7 months — along with improvements in quality of life.

Being at ASCO when results like these are announced feels like watching Simone Biles compete at the Olympics, or watching the Bulls win a three-peat in Chicago. It is not just a win. It is witnessing a moment when what is possible changes.

As a biotech VC at Canaan, moments like this are a reminder that our work — and the work of small biotechs — matters. Daraxonrasib’s story began at Warp Drive, a small venture-backed biotech that took on a hard problem and endured many twists and turns. Their work ultimately became part of Revolution Medicines, which had the capital, ambition, and clinical development expertise to move a bold program forward.

This is how progress often happens in biotech: years of hard science, uncertainty, iteration, risk, perseverance, and ultimately, data that change the field.

Congratulations to the Warp Drive and Revolution Medicines teams, the investigators, the investors who supplied the necessary capital, and most importantly the patients and families who participated in the trial and helped make this progress possible.

I hope one day we will see a standing ovation for a cure. Today, we are one step closer.

Andreessen Horowitz is not a venture capital fund.

And it’s not just Andreessen. It’s all the big funds.

They started out as VC. They operate funds that invest in private early stage companies. But they haven’t been VC funds for a very long time.

That’s not a knock on their success or influence - both of which are massive.

But these aren’t VCs anymore. They’re what I call Consensus Capital: big finance with a Sand Hill Road address.

To continue calling them venture capital is both disingenuous and damaging to how we understand our industry.

I vote we stop.

Why do I care so much about this? 

For LPs: Because when LPs write checks into these funds, they should know what they’re actually buying. True venture capital is about messy, early, conviction-driven bets on non-consensus founders. Consensus Capital is about indexing into the obvious - paying up for access to a narrow band of “consensus” founders and making money off the beta. Those are two entirely different products.

For founders: Because the label shapes their expectations. Consensus Capital flows to a very particular pedigree of founders - those from a handful of schools, startups, or AI labs who are highly discoverable (sometimes literally by AI agents). If that’s them, capital will be abundant and easy. If it’s not, they need to know there are still alpha-seeking VCs out there, the ones who look for outstanding non-consensus builders like Marc and Ben did 25 years ago.

For the ecosystem: All the VC commentators (myself included) are tripping over ourselves about what this all means. My B-school classmate Rob Go wrote about VC’s existential crisis. Sapphire’s Elizabeth "Beezer" Clarkson says venture is broken. Carta data guru Peter Walker talks about the bifurcation of venture capital. Eric Newcomer describes it as a break between the “haves and the have nots”. Calling it all “venture capital” distorts the data. Mega-rounds and mega-funds skew every benchmark. Split them up, and the ecosystem gets better data, better benchmarks, and a truer picture of what’s really happening out there. 

Consensus Capital and Venture Capital are two different investing strategies (that can very much co-exist!). If we treat them like it, everyone would be better off. More in my substack in the comments. … more

Today, I'm proud to unveil the #vcsforkamala pledge.

As of this morning, 110 VCs representing ~$150 Billion in assets under management have signed.

Over the past few weeks, like so many of you, I’ve watched countless venture capital power players announce their support of Donald Trump. Many of them are VCs I respect and admire. And I’m not so much surprised about seeing VCs express conservative viewpoints - some of which I share.

But this particular election - there are much more existential issues at stake.

And so last Tuesday I went online, bought a URL, reserved a social handle, and wrote a 3 paragraph pledge in support of Vice President Harris. On Thursday night, I sent it to a dozen or so friends who volunteered to help. By Friday morning, Mark Cuban and Reid Hoffman had signed. A dozen more joined in the hours that followed, big enough names that there was no turning back.

As of right now, 110 VCs representing about $150 Billion in assets under management have signed the pledge. I believe there are at least a hundred more, just from our outreach, that would sign the pledge but are prevented from doing so by their firms’ internal policies.

The names on this list are not just well known democrats - they are moderates and independents, too. Each person’s reasons for signing are their own, and they vary. What we share is a commitment to safeguarding American democracy, and a desire to engage with each other and with elected leaders on the best path forward on the issues that we care about.

In the last five days I have asked myself, over and over, why I am doing this. There are so many VCs out there with far more power, money, and fame than me. So many that have far more to gain and far less to lose by taking a stand.

Here’s my why.

I’m doing this because I have kids. I’m doing this because they are girls. I’m doing this because to have economic prosperity, we need institutional stability. I’m doing this for the thousands of founders who are watching our industry aghast. And I’m doing this because Silicon Valley power players that have lost touch with the rest of us, don’t speak for all of us.

I am in awe of what a small group of volunteers accomplished in the last 5 days. Thank you so much to all of you, those who need to remain anonymous, and to everyone who was brave enough (and able to) sign the pledge.

Check out the full list of signatories, and sign the pledge, on www.vcsforkamala.org

And join us.

At RA Capital, we all try to improve everything... board governance, IPO process, the IRA, insurance reform, healthcare economics literacy, and this one attempt at upgrading sour patch kids (can't be done, unlike everything else on this list).

What you may not know is that our awesome GC Sarah Reed overhauled the documents many of you use in starting your companies. You can read about her contributions and leadership in this recent 2023 study by Stanford Law's Robert Bartlett that tracked the spread of the NVCA model docs she created and which The Deal's David Marcus last week called 'One of the great achievements in American corporate law in this century'.

Here are the first two paragraphs:

Two events made 2003 an especially pivotal year for scholars of venture capital (VC) finance. The first was the publication in The Review of Economic Studies of Steven Kaplan and Per Strömberg’s “Financial Contracting Theory Meets the Real World: An Empirical Analysis of Venture Capital Contracts.” ... Today, the paper remains standard reading for courses in VC finance, and with over 3,000 academic citations, it represents one of the most cited papers in finance.

The second, far less heralded event, was the convening of roughly two dozen lawyers in a conference room in Boston, Massachusetts, at the urging of Sarah Reed, then General Counsel of Charles River Ventures. Frustrated by the lack of standardization among VC financing contracts, Reed had assembled the lawyers with the goal of fundamentally changing the very system of contracting studied by Kaplan and Strömberg. In particular, Reed’s vision was to create a standard set of financing documents that would be used across all VC financings, thus commodifying the legal technology undergirding the VC ecosystem. Over the ensuing weeks, the group drafted a “model” term sheet along with five related financing agreements that were subsequently hosted on the website of the National Venture Capital Association (NVCA). These model documents, which have colloquially been called the NVCA model documents, have been offered for free to any lawyer or investor since December 2003 with the goal of eliminating much of the variation in VC contracting identified by Kaplan and Strömberg.

📊 Like many people in the community, I was happy to see the Prosperity Council's recommendations yesterday afternoon. In particular, I was delighted to see that the Council recommended reconnecting the Qualified Small Business Stock (QSBS) policies to the federal code to help Oregon retain and grow emerging businesses. Thank you Curtis Robinhold, Renee J. James, and the rest of the council members.

📊 One thing we heard loud and clear is that constituents need to reach out to their local legislators to both educate and share why decoupling QSBS is important to the state's startups and entrepreneurial community. 

📊 If you are a founder and need talking points about the importance of QSBS, the Oregon Entrepreneurs Network team is here to help!! We have simplified this complex tax policy discussion into a top 5 list of how QSBS spurs innovation. This policy....

1️⃣ Lowers the cost of risk for founders 2️⃣ Attracts early stage capital 
3️⃣  Enables competitive compensation for key early hires 4️⃣ Encourages longer holding periods that reward patient capital 5️⃣ Concentrates the benefits of the policy where innovation happens by targeting sectors like tech, bioscience and manufacturing.

As the Prosper Portland liaison and partner for the clean energy industry, I just want to share a few reflections about the Panthalassa Series B $140M raise and its relevance for Portland. 

1.     Historical Moment. This is by far the biggest raise for a clean energy hardware startup in the City of Portland, possibly ever. I hope we can all take inspiration from the level of ambition that Garth Sheldon-Coulson Rob Williams and the full team at Panthalassa have to solve our planet’s energy needs. This is a big shot company that could have a monumental impact on our planet’s future. This is an ambition that resonates with Portland to our core and I believe one of many reasons they chose Portland to establish their HQ. 
2.     City of Portland Location. Another reason this is a big deal is that most of our regional successes past and present (i.e. Cascadia Motion, ESS, Powin, etc) have established their operations completely outside the city boundaries. For many, it’s usually due to the lack of industrial land and facilities that meet the size and requirements they need. You just can’t find the kind of industrial facilities that clean energy companies need scaling their production in the City even though we are home to the state's largest industrial district. Many folks don’t know this and I wish more people did. The general public can help by supporting industrial land readiness efforts at the state and local level if you want to see more high quality clean energy manufacturing jobs in Portland.
3.     Talent. Unique to Panthalassa was also their desire to attract top talent who wanted livability and access to city amenities that Portland offers. They both attract engineers from companies like Blue Origin and Space X while also recruiting locally, mentioning how impressed they are with the quality and depth of the talent pool here. The team also speaks to how pleased they are with the grads coming out of Portland State University, our city's hidden gem. It turns out non-traditional students who get their degrees after having already been in the workforce are excellent employees at a company that centers innovation at its core. 
4.     Industrial and marine Infrastructure. Another appeal is Portland's marine infrastructure, including a robust ecosystem of supply chain companies for metal fabrication. Those supply chain partnerships were crucial for building their pilot units here.

We are all so excited for this company to succeed, both to show how Portland’s talent and infrastructure can deliver for a company like this but also because we share a deep desire to take care of our planet while solving for our energy needs.  Thank you Garth Sheldon-Coulson and Panthalassa for choosing Portland!

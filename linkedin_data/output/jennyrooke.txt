I so enjoyed joining Carol Massar and Tim Stenovec on Bloomberg Businessweek Radio last week, as always! It was energizing to discuss why I’m increasingly optimistic about biotechnology as we head into 2026. Here are some highlights:

1. Public and later-stage private capital is flowing back into biotechnology.  

The market narrative has been that biotech is broken, but biotech IPO activity has accelerated in 2026, with more than 11 companies already going public as of May and collectively raising roughly $3.5B. In private markets, Blackstone raised its sixth fund - the largest ever life sciences fund, at $6.3B. 

2. That market momentum extends well beyond therapeutics.  

Some of the most interesting activity is happening across tools, diagnostics, medtech, and infrastructure. The Alamar Bio IPO and Blackstone’s broader healthcare and life sciences strategy both reflect growing recognition that enabling technologies and biological infrastructure can drive outsized value creation, in and of themselves.

3. Incentives are improving for more precise and multimodal approaches.  

Whether it’s CMS coverage decisions for novel neurodegeneration biomarkers or modernization of government funding models through programs like ARPA-H, we are seeing efforts to better align incentives around detection, measurement, and multidisciplinary innovation. There is still enormous work to do here, but the direction matters.

4. The conversation is expanding to encompass the breadth of biotechnology.

Biotechnology is increasingly being recognized as a general purpose technology — with implications not only for healthcare, but also for manufacturing, economic growth, competitiveness, and national security. (At Genoa we call this #BioConvergence.) Gatherings like the Stanford/Hoover BLISS Summit and SynBioBeta reflect how seriously leaders across policy, defense, industry, and technology are taking this shift.

5. And of course, AI continues to redefine the next era of biology.  

Biotechnology may be one of the most important interfaces where AI meets the physical world. Biology is extraordinarily complex and full of unknowns; AI gives us tools to navigate that complexity more intelligently and efficiently. Yes, there is hype. But there are also real solutions being built — as reflected in moves like Roche’s acquisition of PathAI and the broader investment into computational diagnostics and biological data systems. Biology is becoming an engineering discipline — driven by advances in AI, automation, measurement tools, high-throughput experimentation, and computational modeling — with enormous implications for the pace and economics of innovation.

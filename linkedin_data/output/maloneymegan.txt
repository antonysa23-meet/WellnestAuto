About
Megan is the Founder and Managing Partner of Dria Ventures, a pre-seed and seed stage firm focused on technical founders building in critical industries, from healthcare to home services, construction, and restaurants.
 
Prior, Megan was an investor at General Catalyst, where she backed early-stage companies in these industries, including Bitwise and Pulley. She also served as a board observer for Livongo (sold to Teladoc for $18B), Grammarly, Gusto, Audius, and Samsara, helping each with growth, fundraising, and go-to-market.
 
Megan is a graduate of Harvard Business School ('22). While at HBS, she served as co-chair of the first ever Black Investment Club conference, featuring speakers including Ken Chenault (former CEO of American Express), Ursula Burns (former CEO of Xerox), and Seth Klarman (Baupost). She also served as Venture Capital Panel Director for the Women's Students Association conference.
 
Prior, Megan was an analyst in the institutional equities and technology investment banking teams at Morgan Stanley. She holds a Bachelor of Arts from Columbia University in Economics and Music.
 
In her free time, Megan writes, reads biographies and mysteries, and enjoys discovering new music. She also gives back, having served as Treasurer of CDFI Working Solutions and head of JLSF's partnership with Breakthrough, an organization that supports underserved students on the path to college.
 
Megan has been featured in Poets and Quants, HBCUvc's 31 under 31, and Elpha AMA.

After years of conversations with founders facing impossible cost pressures, Dria Ventures Fund I has officially closed. We’ve raised $8 million to back early-stage founders tackling the cost crises breaking our economy: enormous healthcare costs for patients and crippling software expenses for Main Street businesses. 

These focus areas are deeply personal, which makes Fund I even more special. Experiencing the results of fraud on my mom’s medical practice over a decade ago and the impact of a Livongo device on my grandmother’s diabetes care shaped the investor I am today.

With Fund I, we’re investing in solutions that meet people where they are – from AI copilots for small restauranters to genomics accelerating affordable IVF.

We're grateful to the LPs who went beyond writing checks to bring their networks, expertise, and belief in our thesis from day one. They include Next Legacy Partners, GEM, Spring Point Partners, General Catalyst, Handmade Capital Management, and Stardust. Special thanks to individuals like Elad Gil, Katherine Boyle, Glen Tullman, Lee Shapiro, Diogo Mónica, Jules Maltz, Andy McCall, Andy Chen, Jeffrey Bussgang, and so many others who believed early. 

And to the founders building with us: Thank you. It's a privilege to be your first call. 

Read more about the why behind this fund and the journey to get here in my recent Substack: https://lnkd.in/egnNyxRH

Three years ago, I wrote that HBCUs mostly aren't LPs in Venture Capital funds. It's easy to point something out though, and harder to do something about it. 
 
Today, a collective of us are doing something about it and launching The Historic Fund, a fund-of-funds donating $10M to enable 9 HBCUs to invest in some of the top venture capital firms in the world -- Acrew Capital, B Capital, Cowboy Ventures, First Round Capital, FirstMark Capital, Foundry, General Catalyst, Spark Capital, Union Square Ventures, and fund-of-funds First Close Partners. 
 
The Historic Fund builds on the work that the late Dr. Frederick S. Humphries (prev. President of FAMU and Tennessee State University) started. I believe that generational change starts with a courageous act, and Dr. Humphries was nothing short of bold and courageous. For a sector still running on software from the 1990s, healthcare is adopting AI at twice the rate of the broader economy. That shift is quietly rewiring who controls the system of record.

In her latest piece, our Founding Partner Megan Maloney unpacks how a new category of "Healthcare Administration Agents" is being built atop EHRs like Epic — not to rip and replace them, but to own the high-ROI administrative workflows that write back into them. As Megan puts it: "These agents will not displace EHRs at first. Instead, they'll focus on high-ROI administrative workflows that require writing data back into the EHR for billable events."

Her thesis on what wins? Workflows that are specialized, consequential, and billable. In her words: "The HAAs that can nail the right to become a core workflow will be among the most differentiated and highly scaled companies we have seen in healthcare."

Most restaurant inventory management systems take months to onboard (if they finish doing so).

And too often, after all that work, operators still don’t use them.

At Kintow, we believe onboarding should feel radically different: faster, lighter, and very soon, self-serve.

We’ve been able to get customers live in as little as two weeks. We built AI tools to do the painful implementation work that has historically required months of manual effort. Huge shoutout to Marcus Andren, who has helped push this foundation forward.

Internally, our agents (with supervision) help us extract messy invoices directly from emails, normalize vendor data, generate recipes from unstructured recipe books, reconcile discrepancies, and turn all of that into operational workflows.

Our orchestrator now exposes these same capabilities directly to users, giving operators the ability to review discrepancies, make bulk edits, correct data, and build workflows themselves.

There are 400,000+ independent restaurants in the U.S. Most do not have data teams, implementation teams, or months to spare.

To help them become more profitable, Kintow has to be fast to adopt, useful from day one, and affordable for the operators traditional enterprise tools were never built to serve.

Huge credit to Melody Chen, who has been driving a lot of this forward. What she built over the past two weeks would have taken an entire product team years before access to these models.

1mo • Edited • 

I went into medicine expecting to spend my career in patient care.

What I found working inside Commonwealth Care Alliance, Humana, and other enterprises was that the hardest problems weren't clinical. What I saw on the hospital floors was similar — science works, we have amazing clinicians, and care model innovation has been tremendous.

The biggest problems were always financial and administrative.
$1 trillion a year goes into the administration of healthcare. The cost of the contract-to-payment process in healthcare is equivalent to the amount of money that goes into paying every clinician across the country, which is unscalable and untenable.

The underlying cause to this administrative dysfunction was clear: 
Contracts sat in shared drives. Spreadsheets drove billion-dollar payment decisions. Organizations on both sides of the payor-provider relationship made strategic decisions in the dark and discovered payment problems years after the fact.

Today, we’re announcing Kubera Health's $6.5M funding round led by Upfront Ventures, with participation from Company Ventures, Dria Ventures, and SemperVirens VC.

We're building the contract-to-payment system of record for American healthcare — the financial infrastructure connecting every contract to every payment. The platform is live, managing thousands of contracts and processing billions in payments for hospitals and health systems today, with every customer to date expanding their relationship with us.

A few people I want to thank: 
To the founding team, working with you has been the best part of building this. You took the bet on the hardest version of the problem. Root causes in deeply entrenched industries were not meant to be easy to solve. 
To our early customers, thank you for trusting us when we were still proving the thesis. We are inspired by you to make healthcare better, while you work in the trenches day in and out. 
To our investors and supporters, we're grateful for the partnership from the idea on a post-it days (Kevin, Kesar, Christina, Margaret, Mark, Matthew, Megan, Robby, Colin, Simeon, Maurice, Saiba, and many more).

The clinical work in healthcare is hard enough. The financial system underneath it shouldn't be.

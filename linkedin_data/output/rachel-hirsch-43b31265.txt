Wellness tech is quietly hitting a trust ceiling.

People are often surprised I don’t wear an Apple Watch.

I’m pro-health.
I love data.
I invest in wellness companies for a living.

But seeing my steps doesn’t make me walk more.
Seeing my HRV doesn’t clear my calendar.
A number on my wrist doesn’t integrate into my life.

This is the quiet tension at the center of wellness tech.

We’ve built incredibly sophisticated tools to measure the body.
We’ve been far less successful at turning measurement into support.

Take sleep tracking.

Your score arrives.
It tells you that you slept poorly.
You already knew that.

What would actually change behavior isn’t another dashboard. It’s if that data quietly traveled somewhere useful.

If:
 • my barista made me something lower-caffeine
 • my trainer adjusted intensity without me explaining I’m exhausted
 • my chef (aspirational) matched dinner to recovery needs

Then yes. I’ll wear every sensor available.

But human behavior doesn’t improve because it’s observed.
It improves when friction is removed.

Right now, many wellness products deliver raw data and quietly outsource the hardest work: interpretation, prioritization, execution.

Over time, that creates fatigue.

Not because people don’t care about their health.
But because they’re being asked to manage it like a project without a project manager...

The most interesting shift in high-end wellness is the quieter tech.
Technology that recedes into the background.
Data that flows downstream to the people and systems already shaping daily decisions.
Fewer graphs. More orchestration.

The future of wellness tech won’t belong to the companies that track the most.
It will belong to the ones that translate best.

I never thought I’d see my face in a global campaign.

A few months ago, one of my biggest supporters, Heather, put my name forward for something that terrified me: being the face of yoga for lululemon’s new membership launch.

That meant stepping into a full-day photoshoot—hair + makeup, clothes tailored to me, with *real* models. My first instinct? Imposter syndrome. I am not a model, plus I get nervous about pretty much everything. 

And yet, yesterday the campaign went live—on lululemon’s website, in their emails, in stores, and online around the world. There’s no universe where I would’ve imagined this happening. And yet—here we are. 

Standing in those moments of deep discomfort taught me something I keep circling back to: confidence rarely shows up first. It’s built after you do the thing you thought you couldn’t.

So, if you happen to be shopping and spot my “Where’s Waldo” moment on lululemon walls or see me on their website—please send me a pic.

A wave is moving through consumer, and it’s bigger than the headlines about “portfolio reviews” suggest. On the surface, it looks like corporate housekeeping. In reality, it’s a structural shift. The world’s biggest companies are reorganizing around wellness because that’s where the next decade of growth lives.

Consumers aren’t dabbling. They’re reallocating. Their dollars are migrating toward anything that promises more energy, more years, more balance, more control.

- Nestlé is reviewing its entire VMS (vitamins, minerals, supplements) business to focus on healthy longevity, GLP-1 adjacencies, and women’s health.
- Hormel Foods is restructuring and leaning into protein-forward categories.
- Coty, The Estée Lauder Companies Inc., and other beauty giants are reassessing their portfolios because the fastest-growing beauty segments are the ones tied to skin health, barrier function, hormones, and dermatology-grade performance.
- The Coca-Cola Company is exploring the sale of Costa Coffee as it reallocates toward hydration, functional beverages, and metabolic health.

When multinationals start behaving like wellness companies, it isn’t a trend. It’s an economic reset.

For a decade, wellness was treated as a niche. A vertical. A lifestyle line item. That world is gone. Wellness is becoming the infrastructure of consumer behavior, the blueprint of product strategy, and the organizing principle for conglomerates deciding where to place billion-dollar bets.

Margins live in wellness. Loyalty lives in wellness. Repeat purchase, community, culture, aspiration, identity. All orbit wellness now.

This is exactly the shift Wellness Growth Ventures was built for: the belief that wellness would stop being a category and start becoming the operating system for modern life.

The next decade won’t be defined by “wellness brands.” It will be defined by wellness-native companies across every sector. Food, beauty, personal care, luxury, retail, fitness, tech, healthcare.

All of it dissolving into one truth: Wellness isn’t the future of a sector. Wellness is the future of everything.

I went to a longevity doctor to talk peptides.

Because at this point, it felt like everyone had one except me.

I've interviewed the founders building the companies. Done the podcasts. Read the research.

Somewhere around 90% of my friends are on something.

And I couldn't figure out, was I behind? Or was I just being responsible?

So I did what I always do when wellness gets louder than the science. I booked an appointment with Ryan M. Greene, D.O., M.S. at Monarch Athletic Club. 

I walked in convinced we were about to build my peptide protocol.

It took my doctor about 20 seconds reading my medical history to completely change everything I thought I knew about peptides.

Years ago, I had a tumor. I'm completely healthy now. But he explained that, given my history, anything that influences growth signaling deserved a much more conservative conversation than the one I'd been having in my own head.

Because those pathways don't know the difference between a tendon and a tumor.

That was the moment I realized I'd spent months researching peptides, and almost no time asking whether they were actually right for me.

It also made me wonder how many people are building longevity protocols from TikTok, Reddit, and "day in my life" Reels before anyone has ever looked at the few lines in their medical history that actually change the answer.

We're entering an era where consumers have access to more health information than ever before.

The challenge isn't finding information anymore. It's knowing which information actually applies to you.

On my newsletter, I break down the five major peptide categories, the one the internet has become almost irrationally obsessed with, and the three things Dr. Ryan told me that completely changed how I think about peptides.

Hims & Hers doesn't actually need Ozempic to be forever.

Their business works better if it isn't.

A huge share of GLP-1 prescriptions right now aren't going to people chasing 40-pound losses. They're going to people who want less food noise. Less alcohol. Who already lost the weight and just want to keep it off.

The "obesity drug" era is already over. Most people just haven't noticed yet.

And Hims noticed first.

You walk in for weight loss. They ask a few follow-up questions. Six months later you're on a sleep aid, a hair loss script, a B12 add-on, each one billed separately, each one on autopilot.

That's not a drug company. That's a CRM with prescribing power.

The real tell? The downstream winners have nothing to do with injections. Diagnostics. Muscle preservation. Protein for people who now eat like birds. Bone density (yes, still banging the bone density drum).

Last night I moderated a fireside chat inspired by the challenges and successes I see in my work with founders navigating enterprise deals. There are two distinct sides, the startup's and the corporate sponsor's, each with its own nuance of the sale process. I sat down with Altari Ventures portfolio founder Kevin Miyamoto, COO of Identifee, and Trey Ragland of Corporate America Credit Union, one of Identifee's largest corporate partners, to walk through how their partnership got built.

What stuck with me is how much of the story was really about listening, and how listening compounds into relationship depth over time. Understanding your client's business and being able to offer thoughtful ideas to improve it is the foundation of trust, matched only by hustle and can-do customer service. That is a significant level up from wowing prospects with the product you have built.

Kevin's instinct to outwork the Salesforces of the world is a critical success factor because massive players are structurally incapable of the customer intimacy that differentiates. The best startups know their clients' business inside and out. Land and expand gets talked about as a smart sales mechanic, and we touched on that too, but underneath it is patience. You earn the second contract by overdelivering on the first, nurturing connection with your champion, and building relationships with a broader set of internal stakeholders as the institutional partnership grows.

The part I want every founder to note is the power of pure hustle. In Trey's words, "Kevin is everywhere," and that physical omnipresence creates urgency in moving the collaboration forward. It keeps Identifee differentiated from most vendors who think a check-in email counts as relationship management. Hustle, combined with domain expertise and relentless research, lets founders stay top of mind and be visibly relevant real time rather than periodically present. 

Last but not least, the sale doesn't end with a signed contract. Arguably, it is just the beginning, and founders have to work harder to keep proving value and ensuring longevity of the relationship.

Thank you panelists and guests for joining the event! And massive thank you to BrainStation for hosting us!

Founders - when an investor asks about your competition, please don't say no one is doing what you're doing. Unfortunately, I see this too often in meetings and in answers to the competitive question on my website intake form.

First, it's almost never true. Unless the problem you're focused on has genuinely never existed before, someone is solving it. Maybe poorly, maybe partially, maybe expensively - but it's getting addressed somehow. And with how AI has compressed the time from idea to working code, if your stated problem is as large and urgent as you've described, new entrants are being minted as we speak.

Second, and more importantly, you're misreading the question. It's not a pop quiz on market mapping. It's an invitation to show you know your space and gaps, understand your position in the ecosystem, and have clarity around why you will win. That's the real answer investors are waiting for, and when a founder gives it well, it changes the momentum of the pitch.

That question is one of your best moments in the conversation. So quick advice: don't leave it on the table.

One pattern I've been examining closely of late is how domain expert founders sell.

In enterprise fintech, domain expertise is table stakes. My thesis is built on opportunities where the "fin" matters as much as the "tech," and the founders who can translate deep industry knowledge into a product experience are the ones who stand out at the earliest stages. But that same expertise can work against urgency in the sale.

I've noticed that they can bring an industry insider's patience to their go-to-market motion. They'll let two weeks become four between follow-ups because that's the tempo they know the other side follows and they want to be respectful of that. They'll accept the long sale timeline as inevitable rather than as a constraint to engineer around. Their sales approach can be too subtle: demonstrating credibility through multiple conversations, trusting the relationship to move the process. And then a quarter or several go by, the internal champion gets restructured out, and the deal is back at zero.

The founders who accelerate instead use what they know to find the shortcut, not justify the long road. They understand institutional dynamics well enough to spot the emerging champion before their sponsor loses influence. They'll convene the right people at an event that makes a splash. They’ll craft an extra precise marketing message based on what they know from internal data makes their clients buy — rather than just putting out more content with what the industry should be thinking about. They’ll lean into risk management narrative to demonstrate the consequence of slow action or complete inaction. This is how their domain expertise accelerates sales and turns the company into a differentiation story.

I find this to be one of the harder qualities to screen for at the Pre-Seed and Seed stages, where the sales motion is just getting started. I'm actively developing new frameworks for how to surface it earlier in diligence. Is anyone else thinking about this? Would love to hear from other investors who deal with industries where domain expertise is critical.

Today we announced Fence's $20M Series A, led by Galaxy Ventures, with participation of ParaFi Capital, Crane Venture Partners, Charlie Songhurst, and other extremely supporting, experienced and well-connected investors.

This is an important milestone for our company, but more importantly, it is a signal that asset-backed finance is ready for a new operating layer. 

Asset-backed finance is one of the core funding markets behind the real economy. It finances receivables, consumer credit, invoices, auto loans, mortgages, BNPL, earned wage access, and many other asset classes that businesses and consumers rely on every day.

But the infrastructure behind these facilities is still too manual.

Verification is periodic. Reporting is sampled. Cash reconciliation is slow. Borrowing base calculations live in spreadsheets. Covenant monitoring happens after the fact. Capital moves slowly through too many handoffs.

That creates friction for everyone.

Asset originators see idle capital. Investors get information late. Operators spend time reconciling instead of scaling. The more control a capital provider wants, the more operational burden the borrower usually takes on.

Fence changes that tradeoff.

We provide the software infrastructure to run asset-backed finance facilities in real time: asset verification, borrowing base calculations, covenant tracking, cash reconciliation, and automated capital movement. Powered by smart contracts, tokenization and stablecoins.

Clients can start with one role, such as Verification Agent, Calculation Agent, Collateral Agent, or Paying Agent. But the real power comes when those functions operate together as one continuous system.

One platform. No handoffs. No delays.

Today, Fence supports almost $1.5B in assets under administration across live institutional facilities. We have helped clients achieve up to 40% lower cost of capital and up to 80% lower operational overhead.

These are not pilots. These are real facilities used by institutional capital providers and asset originators today.

With this Series A, we will expand our presence in the U.S., grow our team in New York, deepen our product, and continue investing in institutional-grade infrastructure for the next generation of debt capital markets.

To our clients, partners, investors, and team: thank you.

The future of debt capital markets will be real-time, transparent, and programmatic. We are building the infrastructure to make that future work.

About
Jessica Robinson is a mobility investor, entrepreneur, educator, and champion of Detroit.
 
As co-founder and managing partner of Assembly Ventures, a transatlantic venture platform, she invests in early-stage mobility, physical AI, and digital infrastructure entrepreneurs. The firm integrates capital, governance, and hands-on partnership to help founders navigate complex technical, regulatory, and market systems.
 
Jessica draws daily on an operating background that spans a high-growth startup, a world-class accelerator, a top-five global automaker, technology workforce development, and venture capital. She's seen business opportunities from the operator side, the corporate side, the talent side, and the investor side.
 
Before Assembly, Jessica conceived and launched the Michigan Mobility Institute to accelerate workforce development for the mobility industry, leading it through its transition to SAE International. She built and led City Solutions at Ford Smart Mobility, defining the strategic rationale for local engagement and recruiting the team of Senior City Managers that provided community context and stakeholder engagement. At Techstars, she operationalized a repeatable corporate accelerator model with partners including Ford, Target, Virgin Media, and Anheuser-Busch InBev. Earlier, she played pivotal roles building Zipcar across North America in growth, operations, and marketing.
 
Jessica serves on boards and advisory boards across her portfolio and industry and as a nonprofit director for the Automotive Hall of Fame and the Citizens Research Council of Michigan. She has served as a Governor's appointee to the Michigan's Workforce Development Board, is a Harvard Young American Leaders alumna, a Next City Vanguard, and was named Entrepreneur-in-Residence at the Henry Ford Museum of American Innovation. She also teaches senior leaders to navigating technological disruption as a faculty member at Fast Future Executive.
 
Jessica is based in Detroit and graduated summa cum laude from the University of Massachusetts with a BA in anthropology.

Last week in London, our team participated in two powerful gatherings: the Financial Times Future of the Car conference and our own Assembly Ventures Summit.

Both reminded me that real innovation is forged in conversation, collaboration, and execution. At the FT event, I spoke with global leaders navigating the future of mobility from connected vehicles to EV infrastructure to industrial policy. And then at our Assembly Summit, we brought together the people building that future every day, the founders, investors, policymakers, and operators across North America and Europe.

When we launched Assembly Ventures in 2020, it was with the belief that the movement of people, goods, data, and energy were foundational to how the world works. And what stood out last week was seeing the leaders from our portfolio companies take the stage to share progress and hard-won success.

A standout moment for me was hearing Ben Pope from Sortera Technologies, Inc. share an update on their Indiana facility, where teams work alongside AI-powered systems to return aluminum to the U.S. industrial base. These are new jobs doing work that once happened overseas and didn't exist before the company got started. 

Thank you to our founders, LPs, featured speaker General David H. Petraeus, US Army (Ret.), volunteers Garv and Anthony from MBA programs at Imperial Business School, and all of the other partners who made the week so meaningful. And to our incredible team (Chris, Felix, John, Sujoy, Christine, and Christian) who made it all happen.

Now we get back to work!

Looks like we struck a nerve...

Since yesterday's announcement I've gotten a lot of questions about how the Two Boxes returns benchmark actually works. Here's the short version. Full details on the page in comments.

1. Apply, and we'll consider you for the Q3 cohort.
2. If you're in, you route all or a portion of your returns to us for the benchmark period. With most modern RMA providers this is a quick config change (minutes), not a project. 
3. Within days of the change, those returns will be processed at one of our best-performing partner facilities.
4. Every unit is received, inspected according to your custom SOP, and graded.
5. We capture every data point you could want on each one, in real time, in the Two Boxes portal (and through our MCP server, if you want to pull it straight into your own tools).
6. You get a benchmark report built on your real returns data: where you stand today, the ROI on the table, and specific ways to improve your operation.
7. Then you decide what to do: keep sending returns to a partner, deploy Two Boxes in your own facility, or deploy it with your 3PL, all before peak returns season rears its ugly head.

You probably aren't following this case, but Citizens Research Council of Michigan is. After a family here in the state inherited a home, an administrative error wiped out their homestead exemption. They didn't know about it, so they didn't pay the resulting $2,000 bill. After the county foreclosed, it sold the property at auction for far below its value. The new buyer went on to resell it for nearly $200,000. That case, Pung v. Isabella County, is now before the U.S. Supreme Court.

Last week, the CRC published a brief on what needs to change, starting with homeowner education. But policy work is needed, too, with a cleaner surplus claims process, more transparent auctions, and a review of how penalties compound.

About
Paige is the owner, CIO, and general partner of K Street Capital. 

As Managing Partner, she leads the investment team and advises on several portfolio company boards. She grew the organization by 10x in terms of annual capital invested, founded the funds, and the fund of funds vehicles. She has personally made over 40 investments into early stage companies (28% IRR) leading to 2 unicorns and 31 subsequently marked up institutional rounds to date, including several material all cash exits.

Prior to that, she founded a venture-backed fintech company, GoTab, which she successfully exited in 2018. 

Paige is also the owner and chairman of Everest Capital Group, an FP&A & strategic finance consulting group. With nearly 2 decades of financial experience including acting CFO and executive management roles, she built a team that delivers a unique combination of operational excellence and strategic financial advice to their clients. With proven success in, and passion for – early stage financings, M&A transactions, executive financial leadership, and projection-based cash flow analysis  – Paige has provided senior counsel to a diverse set of companies ranging from early-stage, VC-backed startups to later stage PE-backed corporations.

Paige is an avid reader, traveler, and runner. She has run over 40 full marathons across the world, and was the youngest woman to run a marathon on all 7 continents in 2010 (which she has now completed twice).

Huge congrats to the CaryHealth team! #ACQUIRED by CareTria! 

Just over 7 years ago, we made one of our earliest bets on CaryHealth (Then, CaryRX). Some of you know the story...

I was sick of the pharmacy process, as a customer. I was in the market for something newer/better/faster and far more efficient. 

Felt like I had tried everything... CVS, Pillpack, etc. I'm googling around... CaryRX pops up in a sponsored search result. I'm curious, I click in, I try it, it's FANTASTIC. 

I'm thinking, this has to be a huge company, let me look it up... Nope. It's small, it's new, it's somehow right in my backyard in DC.

It's probably too good to be true but I reach out to the info email address anyway and the CEO actually returns my note. That's when I drive over to meet Areo Nazari and Kevin Lance at the pharmacy for the first time. 

I personally invested, joined the board, quickly became the first institutional capital in the company, backing them 3 times, watching them skyrocket in revenue and pivot to a multifunctional pharmacy platform serving far more than just their DTC customers. 

There were a lot of reasons not to invest. Too much competition, no initial IP, not a big enough market for the top 10 VCs to be interested in, the list goes on. But, what trumps all of that, is the founders. I knew that they would build something big. I didn't know how big or how much it would change along the way, but man am I glad I made the bet on them, that they were going to figure it out. 

I'm so proud of the team!! And lucky to have been a tiny part of that journey. Lucky to be able to call them my friends. Lucky to be able to return multiples of capital to every single investor on the cap table... And a little bit sad that my favorite quarterly board meetings with Areo, Kevin, and Nima Negahban have now come to an end.

The truth is, one of the most important qualities a venture investor can have is curiosity and an open mind. The best deals are non-consensus and hard to come by. Finding things in the wild is a long hard road, but it is also the truest and most gratifying part of the job. 

Can't wait to see what these guys do next!!!!

Founders spend a lot of time comparing valuations. Far fewer spend time comparing true investor incentives.

One of the biggest lessons I've learned building K Street as both a syndicate and a venture fund firm is that investors can look at the exact same company and reach very different conclusions. Not because one is right and another is wrong, but because they're evaluating the opportunity through different lenses.

Every investor has a unique set of goals, constraints, and responsibilities. Some are focused on portfolio construction and fund returns. Others are thinking about strategic value, relationships, or long-term success in an industry. These differences naturally shape how opportunities are evaluated.

That's why fundraising isn't just about finding capital. It's about finding alignment.

The most successful founder-investor relationships tend to be built on a shared understanding of what success looks like and how the company plans to get there. When expectations, incentives, and time horizons are aligned, both sides are better positioned to support one another through the inevitable ups and downs of building a business.

Valuation is an important part of any financing discussion, but it's only one piece of the puzzle.

Capital is relatively easy to compare. Alignment takes more time to understand, but it often has a much greater impact over the life of a company.

Startups are staying private longer than ever before.

For much of the venture industry's history, liquidity was expected to come through an acquisition or IPO. Today, those timelines are less predictable, and in many cases, much further away.

As a result, one of the most important questions in venture capital has become: How does liquidity happen when an IPO isn't around the corner?

On the latest episode of High Stakes, we sat down with Andrew Seter, Principal at Savano Capital Partners, to discuss venture liquidity, the rise of secondaries, and how investors are adapting to a private market that looks very different than it did a decade ago.

One of my biggest takeaways: secondaries are no longer a niche corner of venture capital. They're becoming a critical mechanism for founders, employees, and investors to access liquidity while companies continue to scale privately.

As companies stay private longer, understanding liquidity may become just as important as understanding fundraising. The firms and founders that navigate this well will have more flexibility in how they build, scale, and create value over time.

D.C.’s startup ecosystem is building real momentum.

Over the past several years, we’ve seen more founders choosing to build here, more investors paying attention to the region, and more high-growth companies emerging across sectors like cybersecurity, AI, healthtech, fintech, defense, and infrastructure.

That progress matters and maintaining an environment that supports innovation and entrepreneurship matters too.

There is currently a proposal being discussed as part of DC’s FY2027 budget process regarding Qualified Small Business Stock (QSBS) treatment. While technical in nature, policies like these can influence how founders, investors, and startups think about where to build and grow over the long term.

At K Street Capital, we believe D.C. has the foundation to become one of the country’s most important startup ecosystems, particularly in sectors tied to complex and regulated markets where the region has real advantages.

As this discussion continues, we’d encourage founders, investors, operators, and supporters to stay informed and engaged.

Strong ecosystems are built collaboratively, and long-term momentum is something worth protecting.

One of the biggest misconceptions about fundraising is that it's all about finding investors.

More often, it's about maintaining momentum.

In this week's episode of High Stakes, we sat down with Matthew Bressler, Co-Founder & GP at Lookout Ventures, formerly TDF Ventures, to talk about VC deal timelines and what actually causes deals to speed up, slow down, or stall altogether.

A few key takeaways:
• Momentum creates confidence. Customer wins, product launches, partnerships, and growth milestones give investors reasons to lean in.
• Preparation matters. When diligence materials, financials, and customer information are organized and readily available, the process moves much more efficiently.
• Diligence is usually a positive signal. Investors aren't spending time reviewing documents unless they're seriously evaluating the opportunity.
• Fundraising is a process. The founders who communicate consistently and stay organized tend to move through it much faster.

For founders raising capital today or planning to in the coming year, understanding how investors think about timing can make a meaningful difference.

About
As Managing Partner at Collab Capital, I lead the firm's mission to invest in the building blocks of shared prosperity. My work at Collab is informed by my 15 year career in tech and innovation where I've worn every possible hat. I've been a founder - successfully exiting my first startup, Partpic to Amazon in 2016, an angel investor, a corporate leader - creating Google for Startups' Founders Fund model, and an LP in a handful of funds. I have a vast and varied experience that informs my work today. I have been instrumental in sourcing, evaluating, and closing deals with innovative startups in various sectors, such as fintech, economic mobility, health, and infrastructure. 

In addition to my role at Collab Capital, I serve as an Independent Board Director at CallRail, a leading platform for call tracking and conversational AI, and as a Board Member at the Center for American Entrepreneurship, a nonpartisan research and advocacy organization that promotes entrepreneurship as a force for economic growth and social progress. I am also the co-owner of several small businesses including 310 Studios - a mixed use space for creatives in the heart of Castleberry Hill, as well as a host of family owned small businesses. I am passionate about fostering a more inclusive and diverse ecosystem for innovation and entrepreneurship, and I bring diverse perspectives and experiences to the teams and organizations I work with.

This month, we've told you a story in four parts.

It started with a number: nearly 4 in 10 HBCU students face food insecurity. It continued with a store: Savannah State's year-round mini-mart, built with Amazon and opened on Earth Day. And it grew into a model: more than 30 free grocery stores in schools and community centers across 19 states, built in under five years.

Every one of those stores exists because a partner stepped up.
A recording artist funded the first one at his former middle school. A tech company scaled it nationally. School districts, YMCAs, universities, and municipalities provided the space. The model is the same everywhere: a sponsor provides funding, Goodr builds and operates the store, and a local institution hosts it.

Summer is officially here. The campus meal programs that millions of students relied on are ending. The school pantries that families counted on are closing until fall. Most Goodr Grocery Stores inside schools close with them.

That's why Savannah State's store was built to stay open year-round. And it's why the next one should be, too.

If your company, foundation, or organization invests in education, food equity, or community resilience, this is what that investment looks like: a free, dignity-first grocery store where students and families shop for fresh produce, pantry staples, and household essentials — one that doesn't have to close when the semester does. Your partnership is visible. The community sees who showed up.

The meal plan ends. The need doesn't.

But it doesn't have to be this way.

One thing I’ve learned from coaching founders at iFusion and curating ideas at TEDxAtlanta:


Even as we celebrate the startup that might become the next unicorn, we overlook the small business that might become the next institution.


Atlanta wouldn’t be Atlanta without Mary Mac’s. Paschal’s. Busy Bee. Accapella Books.


Nobody thinks of them as startups. But they were, once. And, each of them needed customers. Employees. Space. Capital. A chance.


Over the years, I’ve had the privilege of working with venture-backed startups, Fortune 500 companies, local restaurants, caterers, retailers, and family-owned businesses.


What did they have in common? The most loved businesses create value beyond revenue; they create belonging.


They become the places where deals get done, ideas get exchanged, celebrations happen and communities take shape.

That’s why I was excited to see Homegrown and Square create another pathway for brick-and-mortar businesses to access growth capital.

Because every city needs startups.

And, every neighborhood needs institutions.

And 20 years from now, some of today’s small businesses may be the places future Atlantans can’t imagine living without.

(Bonus: The fact that Atlanta-based Collab Capital is an investor of Homegrown is next level meta.)

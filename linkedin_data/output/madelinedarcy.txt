Last week The White House announced an $8B initiative to expand access to nutritional products, services, and information. 

1 in 10 individuals in the US don't have access to reliable nutritious food and closing this gap will require both public and private sector solutions. 

Health is often thought of in the bounds of hospitals and pharmaceuticals. In reality, it requires navigating a complex ecosystem and necessitates increasingly connected cross-sector efforts. In fact, 80% of health is driven by social determinants (e.g, housing, transport, food, employment). 

In conjunction with The White House announcement, Instacart announced the launch of Instacart Health - beginning with funded wallets, digital SNAP integration, and a diet-related labeling system. It’s a clear opportunity given the government annually distributes an estimated $3.7T worth of social benefit programs through specialized payment networks. 

Tech is transforming the consumer experience of healthcare and I’m thrilled to see public and private sector commitments towards improving access, quality, and outcomes within healthcare. I’d love to hear what else is around the corner - please comment or send me a note.

On a quiet Sunday, I picked up The Next Day by Melinda French Gates and didn’t put it down until I’d finished the last page.

One passage that struck me was the story of two waves. To paraphrase:

𝘛𝘸𝘰 𝘸𝘢𝘷𝘦𝘴 𝘢𝘳𝘦 𝘩𝘦𝘢𝘥𝘪𝘯𝘨 𝘵𝘰𝘸𝘢𝘳𝘥 𝘵𝘩𝘦 𝘴𝘩𝘰𝘳𝘦. 𝘛𝘩𝘦 𝘣𝘪𝘨𝘨𝘦𝘳 𝘸𝘢𝘷𝘦 𝘴𝘵𝘢𝘳𝘵𝘴 𝘵𝘰 𝘱𝘢𝘯𝘪𝘤 𝘢𝘴 𝘪𝘵 𝘴𝘦𝘦𝘴 𝘰𝘵𝘩𝘦𝘳 𝘸𝘢𝘷𝘦𝘴 𝘤𝘳𝘢𝘴𝘩𝘪𝘯𝘨 𝘢𝘩𝘦𝘢𝘥. “𝘛𝘩𝘪𝘴 𝘪𝘴 𝘵𝘩𝘦 𝘦𝘯𝘥!” 𝘪𝘵 𝘤𝘳𝘪𝘦𝘴.
𝘛𝘩𝘦 𝘴𝘮𝘢𝘭𝘭𝘦𝘳 𝘸𝘢𝘷𝘦 r𝘦𝘴𝘱𝘰𝘯𝘥𝘴 𝘤𝘢𝘭𝘮𝘭𝘺: “𝘠𝘰𝘶 𝘩𝘢𝘷𝘦 𝘯𝘰 𝘯𝘦𝘦𝘥 𝘵𝘰 𝘸𝘰𝘳𝘳𝘺. 𝘠𝘰𝘶’𝘳𝘦 𝘯𝘰𝘵 𝘢 𝘸𝘢𝘷𝘦 - 𝘺𝘰𝘶’𝘳𝘦 𝘸𝘢𝘵𝘦𝘳.”

It’s such a simple story, but it hit me hard. How often do we see change as failure - when it might actually be a return to who we really are? A return to something more essential than the immediate response to what’s around us. 

The book is about letting go of the need to follow a fixed plan, instead staying open to what could be. Possibility over pressure. Listening over forcing.

It reminded me that reinvention rarely starts with certainty. It starts with paying attention, getting quiet, and moving forward in your own way.

Have you read it? Would love to hear what stood out to you.

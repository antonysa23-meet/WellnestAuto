I started a new role this week as a Lecturer in the Master of Sciences in Entrepreneurship program at UW Foster School of Business! My first class was a blast, and I'm looking forward to working with my students for the next three quarters.

I started with a reflection exercise intended to encourage the students to think expansively about their experiences, so I included a slide that listed several of my own areas of experience. My list purposely included both professional and personal domains, as I think great business ideas can come from unexpected places. 

The first slide is my list as I originally drafted it. I hit "Enhance" on Google slides, thinking it was just a stylistic feature that would automatically match the design of the rest of the deck. As you can see on the second slide, it changed the qualitative meaning of my slide by putting "Parenting young children," "Caring for senior dogs," and "Practicing yoga" in tiny font at the bottom of the slide. 

I was infuriated to see such a blatant example of the bias that plagues these tools and decided to share it with my class as a reminder of how important it is to bring heightened awareness and a degree of caution when using AI. 

Thank you Samantha Ogle for this exciting opportunity, Yoko Okano for graciously handing me the baton, and Elizabeth Scallon for being the catalyst!… more

I've been writing about women in business for nearly 20 years now. I got a reminder of how little progress there's been during that period when Fortune put out its count of the number of Fortune 500 female CEOs last week. Today there are 55 women running the biggest US public companies. When I started working at Fortune as a cub reporter in 2008, there were 12. (In 1998, there were just 2.) 

Objectively, this is an improvement. But if growth continues at this same pace, it will take about 100 years for women to reach parity with men in the corner office. What concerns me even more is that the number, while tied with last year’s record, didn’t even move an inch.

Taken alone, you could argue the stagnation is just a blip and not a broader sign that the hard-fought gains for women in business are stalling. That doesn’t appear to be what’s happening. When you look at other gauges of progress, it becomes clear that in many cases we’re seeing not just a flatlining but a full-on backslide.

Today we’re excited to launch mPACT — mpathic’s clinician-led benchmarking framework for evaluating how AI systems perform in high-risk conversations.

As more people turn to AI systems in moments of vulnerability, one question matters more than ever:

How do these models actually perform in high-risk, real-world conversations?

mPACT was designed to help answer that question.

Built by licensed mental health professionals, mPACT evaluates how AI systems detect risk signals, interpret context and severity, and respond in ways that are supportive and clinically appropriate. 

Our first benchmarks focus on three high-stakes domains:
❗️Suicide risk
❗️Eating disorders
❗️Misinformation

What we found:

Across leading models, there’s clear progress — systems are getting better at avoiding overtly harmful responses and recognizing distress.

But there’s also a critical gap: Avoiding harm doesn’t consistently translate into providing the support that a human clinician would.

In suicide risk conversations, top-performing models we tested* included:

✅ Google Gemini 2.5 Flash
✅ Anthropic Claude Sonnet 4.5
✅ OpenAI ChatGPT 5.2

*Tested April, 2026 

Each showed strengths — but no model consistently met the clinical rubric our licensed psychologists developed  across all dimensions.

In more nuanced domains like eating disorders and misinformation, performance was more variable  — especially when risk was subtle, indirect, or embedded in everyday language.

mPACT is designed to make these gaps visible, measurable, and improvable — bringing clinical rigor to how AI models are evaluated.

We’re grateful to the clinicians, researchers, and machine learning engineers who helped make this possible.

Learn more about the results here: http://mpathic.ai/mpact

mPACT is an independent evaluation. Model names are used for identification only. No affiliation or endorsement is implied.

At Microsoft, we believe that even the smallest moments of pause can make a big difference.

Since launching Breakthru in 2020, we’ve seen over 11,000 of our employees across 68 countries embrace these two-minute resets. Whether it’s a stretch, a breath, or a moment of calm, these breaks are helping our people feel better and work better.

Today is World Malaria Day. 

Please take a moment in your day to learn about how this devastating disease is impacting women and girls in the excerpt below from Lizz Ntonjira & Dr Jemimah Njuki's "Malaria’s Gender Divide: Why Women Bear the Brunt of a Global Health Crisis."

"Women spend four times as many days on caregiving compared to men – a stark reality exacerbated by recurring malaria infections within families as poverty traps women in cycles of economic dependency and limits opportunities for education and employment.

Women’s contributions to the global health system are estimated to be around 5% of global GDP. But around 50% of this work is unrecognised and unpaid. In malaria-endemic regions, this labour often takes the form of informal caregiving, as women provide care in up to 83% of malaria cases.
For community health workers, 70% of whom are women, the imbalance is even larger. Female health workers spend significantly more unpaid hours than their male counterparts, despite forming the backbone of malaria detection, treatment, and prevention efforts in rural areas.

Women and girls often lack decision-making power in their households, preventing them from accessing life-saving interventions like insecticide-treated nets or seeking timely healthcare. Cultural norms can dictate who uses a bed net or who receives care first, often leaving women and girls at greater risk.

Malaria is a leading cause of death amongst adolescent girls in malaria-endemic countries. Many are forced to leave school to care for sick family members or themselves, disrupting their education and increasing their vulnerability to early marriage or exploitation. Without targeted interventions, these gendered gaps will continue to undermine global malaria eradication efforts."

About
Venture Capitalist. Entrepreneur. Investor. Podcast Host. Content Creator. Community Builder.

I’m the founding GP of Park Rangers Capital, a pre-seed/seed stage venture firm backing elephant companies: durable, purpose-driven startups that turn customers into members and companies into communities. Read the essay that started it all: prc.beehiiv.com/p/elephantsnotunicorns

I also host the Trailblazers Podcast, where I sit down with contrarian tech leaders who’ve found conventional success in unconventional ways. Listen here: trailblazerspod.com

I’m a 3x founder (1.5 exits), employee #2 twice, and an angel/advisor to dozens of companies including Clay, Beehiiv, Superpower, and more. Alongside investing, I’m a content creator with 100K+ followers across platforms.

My venture firm Park Rangers Capital has received funding from Catalyze (funding by Skoll Foundation, Sorenson Impact, JP Morgan Chase), Recast (funding by Melinda Gates’s Family Office), Allocator One, Impact Assets, top GPs, unicorn founders, and executives from influential companies like Google, OpenAI, Anthropic, and others.

My work has been featured in Fortune, Forbes, The Information, New York Times, Business Insider, TechCrunch, etc and on our Park Rangers Capital blog I share essays like FFF is the new PMF and Getting Off the Alphabet Train where I share my perspective on the state of technology, company-building, and venture as an asset class.

Nas Daily on growing up Palestinian in Israel:

Nas says he spent the first half of his life trying to escape the place he grew up.

After earning a scholarship to Harvard, he met Jewish classmates for the first time and realized how different the world looked outside the environment he was raised in.

That experience shaped the mission behind Nas Daily: bringing people together instead of pushing them further apart.

He says those experiences continue to shape how he talks about one of the world's most difficult conflicts.

Nas on the Israel-Palestine conflict and the experiences that shaped his perspective:

“I happen to be part of 20% of Israel that’s not Jewish.”
“Arab, Palestinian, Muslim. As Muslim as it gets for hundreds of years.”

“I didn’t like the idea of being in a village, I didn’t like the culture of the village, and I didn’t like being looked down upon in Israel.”

“And so I escaped and went to Harvard, and that’s where I made Jewish friends. It took me 19 years to finally make a Jewish friend.”

“I’m here saying we didn’t want to live with Jews, and Jews didn’t want to live with us.”

“There’s a lot of blame to be assigned to everybody in the Middle East.”

“I grew up seeing people being torn apart because of labels. Because of your religion, where you come from, and what you believe in.”

“So the mission statement of Nas Daily is to bring people together, the opposite of what I grew up in.”

“My Israel-Palestine video that I made is not supposed to be divisive. It’s supposed to be uniting.”

“We are equally indigenous. We both have nowhere to go.”

“Would life have been better if I jumped on the Free Palestine train and made it a simplistic narrative?”

“I am a Palestinian. I understand how complicated this thing is.”

“The most dangerous thing for a Palestinian is not an Israeli, it’s another Palestinian with an AK-47.”

Why Nas Daily hasn’t paid for meat in 5 years:
His rule is simple.

If he wouldn't be willing to kill the animal himself, he doesn't believe someone else should have to do it on his behalf.

Nas says he would never kill a cow himself, so he stopped paying to eat meat.

That belief now extends to his company.

The business doesn't expense meat, which means when employees eat on the company's dime, the company is effectively vegetarian.

Nas on the personal rule that changed how his company operates:

“It’s been 5 years since I have not spent a single dollar on meat.”

“My opinion is if I'm willing to do it with my hands then I'll eat it or pay for it.”

“And so I can never kill a cow with my hands, so I can't pay for it.”

“We never pay for meat as a company, we don't even expense it.”

“It is so awkward when I take a customer to dinner and they order chicken and I tell them, ‘Can you Venmo me $5?’”

Nas Daily on the skill every founder should develop:

Most people are either great builders or great storytellers.

Very few can do both.

Nas says many creators are incredible at making things, but struggle to hire, lead, and communicate with a broader audience. Others are great at capturing attention, but lack the discipline to build.

His goal is to become someone who can use both sides.

That's also how he builds his company, balancing people who excel at creating with people who excel at operating.

Nas on why the best founders think with both sides of the brain:

“Most content creators are left-brained. They’re very creative but struggle to hire people”

“The right side brain people are great engineers but they struggle to get attention from the average person”

“The best people are left brain and right brain, if you combine those two into one person then you have magic”

“Half of my company is left brain and the other half is right brain”

“I’m trying to be that guy that has both brains”

“When you combine a video editor who’s really creative and an engineer who’s really good together under one roof, you get magic”

Nas Daily on the biggest lie creator-founders tell themselves:

“My audience gives me infinite distribution.”

Nas says having an audience can give creators cheaper acquisition, faster awareness, and a bigger first wave of users, but it does not solve the hardest part of building a company.

Once people try the product, the creator’s fame matters a lot less.

If the product is not good, people leave.

That’s why creator-led companies still face the same challenge as every other startup: building something people actually want to come back to.

Nas on why product beats distribution:

“Fandom is a great way to get somebody through the door, but people do not stay in the house if the product is not good”

“Justin Bieber tried to compete with Spotify when he was at his prime”

“It didn’t work”

“He got the first 10 million people to download. The product wasn’t good, so they left.”

“So yes, we’re very lucky to get the attention in the beginning, but our challenge remains the same as every startup founder’s challenge”

Why Nas Daily has worn the same shirt for 9 years:

He thinks the average person lives to 76, which means at 34 years old, he's already 44% through his life.

Every eight months, he changes the number on his shirt to reflect that.

It's a daily reminder that time is finite and every year matters.

Nas on the philosophy behind the shirt he wears every day:

“I haven’t changed my t-shirt in 9 years.”

“If I go to a wedding, I wear it and then I wear a jacket on top of it.”

“Sometimes I hide it for security purposes. There are some countries where I don’t want to be recognized, like Europe or the Middle East, so I try to blend in.”

“This means 44% of life. I’m 34 years old, I’m supposed to die at 76. That’s the average life expectancy, so I’m 44% done with life.”

“I started wearing this when I was 32%, so I spent 12% of my life wearing the same t-shirt.”

“It’s a real reminder to juice the heck out of life before it passes by.”

About
Rachel ten Brink is General Partner and co-founder of Red Bike Capital, an early stage Venture Capital fund based in New York that invests in Vertical AI, Fintech and Health & Wellness. She is the co-founder of Scentibrd, a Y-Combinator backed ecommerce subscription that raised $30M in venture funding and is a Top YC Company by Revenue (top 2% of all YC companies). Rachel was CMO/CRO leading Growth and Revenue, scaling the company to $300M+  in revenue and signing over 74 enterprise customers. 

Rachel has been a board member and senior advisor for various companies. Before this, she spent two decades as an operator building billion-dollar brands at Procter & Gamble, Elizabeth Arden, Estee Lauder, and L’Oreal. Rachel serves as Chair of the Board of Latinas in Tech, holds an M.B.A. from Columbia Business School, and graduated magna cum laude from Babson College.

Fun to be on Bloomberg TV with the brilliant Vonnie Quinn & Gina Martin Adams discussing what's next in #ecommerce and consumer #finance: 
1. Leverage AI: As retailers & ecommerce companies like Etsy Amazon eBay Wayfair look to cut costs - they will rely on AI for:
-Applications in marketing/ad spend optimization to lower CAC
-Improved recommendations to increase AOV 
-Optimize/ lower costs in Customer Service
-Optimize distribution centers and 3PL activities

2. BOPIS and More flexible fulfillment methods: Customers will continue to embrace pandemic-driven shopping behaviors like buy-online-pickup-in-store (BOPIS) and flexible fulfillment methods. BOPIS  expected to be $703B globally by 2027.

Target announced it will spend $100M in “sortation” center which aggregate shipments in a facility where packages arrive from about 30 to 40 nearby stores, get grouped into efficient delivery routes and get picked up by a 3rd-party carrier or a vehicle of a contract worker for Shipt (which Target owns) 

3. Mobile commerce:  m-commerce will continue to be a major driver accounting for 61%+ of digital shopping orders in 2023. 

4. Re-commerce: (i.e. selling used products) as sustainability and affordability take priority. 
65% of all shoppers already use a resale or re-commerce service.

Key is understanding re-commerce shoppers: Everyone from Gen Z to Boomers has an interest in resale, but different demographics have different needs, so retailers need to match their brand to the right audience;

30% of retailers are involved in re-commerce today- BUT they need the technology in place: Challenges from verifying authenticity to detecting counterfeiting are a big part of resale, separate supply chain- a lot of experimentation in the space

On the #fintech side- also discussed the impact of inflation, dwindling consumer savings + higher cost of capital on consumer spend => ie, people may delay purchases that require financing and be selective on their spend: shop at Walmart but  "more on food + less TVs"

Holiday ecommerce trends
1) More, earlier, and deeper discounts: 
-Companies started their holiday discounting early- around Veteran's Day. Amazon pre-empted everyone with Prime Day in October, Walmart doing new digital sales every Monday in Dec
-Inventory is finally back on track- supply chains have mostly recovered and brands ordered higher depths inventory than prior years to be safe, so plenty of product on shelf.  But this is pushing retailers and ecommerce brands to offer more sales to make sure they are not stuck after the holidays. 
- Consumers are making tradeoffs between what they need and what they want, and plan to spend less overall this holiday.  Because sales behavior started earlier, you will expect an earlier slowdown in DTC as shoppers get their shopping done early.

2) DTC being cautious with ad spend/ lower volume: 
-As advertisers are being more cautious with their dollars and less spend generally means less ecommerce sales since paid spending is such a key driver. Q4 spend is up but not as up as earlier years.  Particularly among VC-backed DTC brands that are cautious about spend, facing a cooler funding environment and trying to show attractive CAC/LTV and extend their runway- growth at any cost is not an option in this funding environment.  
-Alternative, most cost effective channels like SMS and emails will see a bump but are getting competitive.

3) Innovation in Retail and Ecommerce: 
-Innovation in retail tech and ecommerce tech is critical to drive sales - expect more Metaverse, social shopping and experiential retail tech
-Brands and Retailers are being pushed to cost cut so will look at all their SAAS partnerships and cut the ones that can't prove ROI

4) Different segments are feeling the pinch differently:
-Brands under $100 are faring better- bigger purchase items there are in a tougher environment- for example beauty is doing well, while luxury fashion/accessories and household goods are tougher.  
-People overbought over last year in certain categories like electronics, computers and home- so this year it’s more about little gifts. Grocery- moving back to brick and mortar- a post pandemic trend that is benefiting large retailers like Walmart who are able to attract users with value grocery items. 
-Lower end consumers will upgrade to accessible luxury but keep it under $100, Truly high end consumers don't feel recessionary pressures so you're seeing LVMH doing ok

While others may brag about how many #NYTechWeek events they attended, I took a different approach: "only" 13 events last week 😉.

After years of doing tech weeks, here's what I've learned:
=> Pick your lane before the week starts. I focused on LP/GP meetings, speaking engagements and specific verticals. Everything else was a no- zero FOMO.
=> Curated beats comprehensive. The best conversations happened at small format events: a VC omakase, a wine night, an emerging manager dinner. A 15 person breakfast where everyone is relevant beats a 500 person mixer where you collect badges.
=> Go where your people are. I prioritized events with people I genuinely enjoy. Energy compounds and obligation drains.
=> Protect your time: make sure you still have time to do actual work. 
=> Quality follow up is the real ROI: real conversations means a follow up list I can actually work through vs. a stack of business cards going nowhere (but I am still underperforming here). 

Highlights from the week: Consumer Goods Coffee Mixer with Sumeet Shah, Pitch & Drink with P2S.vc, Margs Before Meetings with Collab Fund x J2 Ventures Christine Keung, PEF Breakfast Jeremy Cai Connor Tomkies, Founding GPs VC Omakase with Unicorner, Partner2Partner VC Breakfast with AlphaPartner, Emerging Manager + LP Leadership Dinner with Venture Forward and Maryam Haque, the GP/LP Emerging Manager Panel with New York Ventures Jennifer Tegan Bryanna S., Ghost Angels Max Rivera Commerce Capital Summit with Equal Ventures, Chelsea Zhang Rick Zullo Andrei Rebrov 🚀 Kait Stephens, GP & Partner Wine Night with Bulletpitch+ Charlie Stephens, and The Vibe 2.0 with nvp capital Knicks Viewing Party Ariel Serber.

Yesterday, I joined a panel hosted by Venture Forward and New York Ventures on what it actually takes to raise a fund in this market. "Inside the Capital Stack: Emerging Managers & LPs" at #NYTechWeek put emerging managers and LPs in the same room to talk openly about how the raise has changed and how to position for the long game.

A few things that stuck with me:
- Brian Hollins (Collide Capital) on building a proof-of-concept fund in business school and how that shaped the way he builds the firm today. Appreciated how intentional his process of firm building was from the early days. 
- Brian and I kept coming back to LP empathy. LPs are overwhelmed, stretched, and under real pressure right now, and the managers who keep that in mind tend to build the stronger relationships. Brian gave a sharp read on whether an LP is genuinely interested or just being polite. Good signs: they bring more people into the next meeting, spend time in your data room (Dropbox DocSend to track) and start asking about timing. Warning sign: you keep meeting the same single person and the circle never widens.
- Nate Loewentheil (Commonweal Ventures) had the best one-liner of the morning- "I take no's highly personally"  and that candidness broke through. Mike Ma asked how we handle LP rejections, a thoughtful reminder that every investor feels them and the importance of managing your energy. Resilience is a big part of the game (my favorite story Herman Goihman “you can’t say you love being an eskimo but hate the cold”)  

Being an emerging manager is a marathon, which is why rooms like this matter. Thank you to Bryanna S. (excellent moderating!), Maryam Haque, Jennifer Tegan (you were missed) and Polsinelli for hosting. The support for fund managers in this city is real.

In all the GTM noise about AI accelerating outbound, AI writing your emails, AI doing your prospecting, etc. we're skipping past the most important customer in the funnel: the one who already knows they need a solution and is actively searching for it. That buyer has always been the highest-converting in the pipeline. And while everyone's been busy automating cold outreach, that buyer quietly migrated to a new channel: AI search. 

Organic traffic from Google is declining up to 30% year over year. At the same time, referral traffic from AI tools like ChatGPT and Gemini has 3x in the past year, and those visitors are converting at significantly higher rates than traditional organic. Shopify just shared the data and it's striking:

🔹 AI-referred shoppers convert at nearly 50% higher rates than organic search 🔹 14% higher average order values 
🔹 55% of AI-referred sessions land on a product detail page (vs 20% for organic) 
🔹 AI-referred orders grew nearly 13x YoY in Q1 2026

What's driving it is "journey compression." Think about the buying lifecycle: research, consideration, purchase, loyalty => in a traditional search journey a buyer does 3 to 5 searches across 8 to 12 pages over multiple days. In an AI conversation, that entire research phase collapses into a single chat. By the time they click through to your site, the model has already done the consideration work. They land ready to buy.

Translation: AI search isn't replacing your homepage funnel. It's replacing the consideration phase you never had visibility or control over. And I'd venture to say it's even more marked in B2B sales. 

This is exactly why we backed AthenaHQ at Red Bike Capital. Showing up as the answer in ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini and Claude is different than ranking on Google, and the brands (B2B or B2C) that build for it now will compound an advantage the latecomers can't close. We saw this with mobile in 2012 and social commerce in 2019. They're building more than a way to manage mentions, it's an entire infrastructure.   

If you're a founder still treating AI mentions as a vanity metric, this is your nudge. The high-intent buyer is already in the chat. The only question is whether your brand is in the answer.

About
Leura is a former founder and experienced investor who previously founded and bootstrapped two startups as CEO. Her last startup, Laurel & Wolf raised $30M from top investors CRV and Benchmark and scaled to serve tens of thousands of customers across the US.  From 2015 to present, Leura has invested in dozens of early-stage companies and actively advises founding teams on product, operations, customer growth, design, hiring and product market fit. Her current investing focus includes future of work, future of commerce, hardware/robotics and AI. Leura is also passionate about diversity in venture and actively supports diverse founders from non-traditional markets. Leura was named a Forbes 30 under 30 and was Goldman Sachs “100 most Intriguing entrepreneurs”.

Proud to see MATERIAL in The Wall Street Journal today.

The insight is simple but profound: the next leap in battery performance won't just come from new chemistry, it'll come from rethinking the form factor entirely.

MATERIAL 3D-prints batteries, allowing power sources to be designed around the structure of a device, not the other way around. That means filling hollow frames, conforming to wearable bodies, eliminating the dead space that conventional cylindrical and pouch cells force on every designer.

The result: more range, more payload capacity, virtually no mass penalty.

The US Air Force is already a big champion of MATERIAL. And we are, too.

You can't code your way out of the physical world.

That's the hard truth at the center of building real-world robotics, and it's exactly what Paige Craig, Partner at Outlander VC and Zach Rash Co-Founder & CEO at Coco unpacked on the latest episode of The Pair Program.

Paige put it plainly: you've got brilliant AI engineers working next to the guy turning a wrench on a broken wheel and a broken antenna, right next to the 3D printer. And figuring out how all of that comes together? That's the actual job.

Most founders underestimate it.

Because in the early days, humans are the glue. They fill the gaps between hardware that isn't ready and software that isn't there yet. That's not a bug. It's the operating model until you earn the right to automate your way through it.

What we covered in Ep. 96 👇

→ Why real-world deployment beats perfect demos every time
→ How Coco is building operational moats through on-the-ground data
→ The economics (and brutal realities) of last-mile autonomous delivery
→ What separates robotics companies that scale from those that stall
→ Founder-market fit and why patience is a competitive advantage in hardware

Paige's line that stuck with me:

"You have to have the patience to be great."

In a world chasing quick wins and borrowed autonomy stacks, Zach took the hard path. And that's exactly the kind of bet Outlander VC looks for.

If you're building in physical AI, robotics, or autonomous systems, this one is required listening.

The best investors are often evaluating people long before they evaluate products.

In this episode of Technoventure, I spoke with Paige Craig, Founder and Managing Partner of Outlander VC, about how his experiences in the Marine Corps and intelligence community shaped his approach to leadership, risk, and venture investing.

Paige argues that founder character matters more than pitch decks, especially in chaotic environments where resilience and execution become the true differentiators.

A few themes that stood out:
1. Why exceptional founders combine confidence with humility
2. How military leadership principles translate into venture investing
3. Why AI is commoditizing software and accelerating physical AI
4. What Paige looks for when evaluating founder resilience

We also discuss:
— Early lessons from Lyft
— Why robotics and autonomy are becoming core investment areas
— The intersection of AI and life sciences
— Why most people fundamentally misunderstand risk

"Warfare is won by humans," Paige noted. That belief continues to shape how he evaluates entrepreneurs today.

We might think high paying jobs are the best, but they're not.

What we see: almost no correlation between whether people find meaning and satisfaction in their work, and what they get paid to do it.

What stands out:
🔵 Clergy and surgeons are both very fulfilled by their jobs while one makes $365K and the other, $46K 
🔵 Airline pilots earn $281K but report lower fulfillment than personal care workers making $38K 
🔵 And there are plenty of low-fulfillment roles (like parking lot attendants or gaming supervisors) across all scales of pay 

Should that be the work AI focuses on automating?

📈In the SaaS era, most value accrued to platforms that let users own their workflows. 

The further you got from being a source of record, the less value you created. Services businesses lagged even further, constrained by margins and growth ability. 

📈In the AI era? Value will barbell to the edges.

AI agents will automate large swaths of “operate” workflows—compressing the middle. That means more value flows to platforms (own) and services (outsource) with clear responsibility and better economics.

Seed is the new Series A. 

Round sizes are growing. Valuations are rising. But also, companies are maturing. 

The average Seed stage startup today is as old as the average Series A startup was 10 years ago. Same for Series B and so-on. 

Wider adoption of AI may amplify this trend. (If easier and cheaper to build software, companies will burn less doing so and may extend time between fundraises.)

What will this do to venture markets? Multi-stage dip into earlier stages? Further institutionalization at the pre-seed stage?

Neither bootstrapping nor raising venture capital guarantees success 

The chart below compares survival rates for all private companies and venture-backed companies. 

What do they have in common? It's hard to build a company and operate it for 10+ years 

What I find most interesting is that failure rates are similar over the first ~2 years of a company's lifecycle... 

- 30% of private companies went bankrupt by their second year 
- 25% of venture-backed companies went bankrupt before raising a second fund (though 20% failed to raise that second round)

...an indication that product-market-fit is hard to find regardless of financial backing

Founders are scrambling: SaaS is dead. AI-native "services-as-software" is the future. Sell outcomes, not tools. Outcome-based pricing. Multiples of 50–100x revenue instead of 5–10x. Reposition the deck and the round comes in higher.

The market signal backs the thesis. $2 trillion in SaaS market cap evaporated in the first six weeks of 2026. Atlassian -35%. Salesforce -28%. Investors are pricing in AI's ability to rebuild those products from scratch.

Jackie DiMonte at Grid Capital wrote the cleanest version of the framework that's driving the repositioning — customers OWN the solution, OUTSOURCE it, or OPERATE in the middle, with the money sitting at the extremes and the middle getting squeezed.

Here's what I'm not seeing in the conversations: most of the companies pitched as services-as-software aren't.

Harvey, EvenUp, and the rest of the vertical AI category are operate-tier by every honest structural test. The lawyer still signs the demand letter. The doctor still signs the clinical note. The AI helps. The AI does not deliver the outcome. They're priced as outsource-tier anyway, on the bet that the marketing label travels faster than the diligence does.

I just published Part 1 of a series evaluating the distinction between Operating and Outsource companies.  Part 1 introduces a 30-second test that sorts companies into the right category honestly. There's a 5-question diagnostic worksheet at the end for running the test on your own company.

If you're building, raising, or underwriting an AI company in 2026, this is the diagnostic that's going to land on you whether you run it yourself or not. Better to know your answer first.

Legal AI startups have raised more than $2 billion.
The world's highest-grossing law firm just decided to build its own instead.


We saw this coming a year ago.

If a function is a competitive differentiator, you want to own the inputs and take full responsibility for the outputs.

And if you can't take responsibility for the outputs, it's usually because the tool doing the work is a point solution. You can reference it, but you can't outsource your thinking or your work to it.

Think about an associate at a law firm. If they mess up a contract review, it's their fault.

Replace that associate with legal AI that messes up the same review. Is it their fault, or yours? Did you use the right version, give the right context?

That ambiguity matters.

Tools like Harvey and Legora are powerful, but they’re still point solutions. As the line between labor and technology blurs, we need to rethink how value gets created and captured. 

The bulk of long-term value accrues to platforms that let customers either fully own the workflow or confidently outsource it end-to-end. That can mean build or buy.

➡️Kirkland & Ellis' reported $500M commitment to build is multiples of what they spend on external AI vendors.

Point solutions are the onramp to AI. They were never going to be the final destination.… more

Founders, the VC you're pitching is most likely raising too.


PitchBook data for 2025 shows the median emerging manager spent ~18 months raising their last fund, typically closing ~3 years after the prior one. 
➡️ EMs spend 50%+ of their time actively fundraising

Carta published median company age at each round. Call a typical raise 1 month prep + 2 months of conversations + 1 month legal (drop for SAFEs). 
➡️ Founders are market 20%+ of the time

Same headline, but raising a fund and raising a round are different sports:

🟢Access. 
Rounds support one or two leads. Once those are in place, the round fills fast.
Funds are the opposite. Anchors don't need to be first and are rarely more than 25% of the fund. 

🟢Terms. 
In a round, moving early buys you pricing and positioning. Move too slow and competition runs the price up. 
In a fund, early vs. late barely moves anything. Being early gets you QSBS, but late gets you a look at the portfolio.

🟢Conviction. 
It takes 5–10 years to know how a fund is really doing, so LPs take their time getting comfortable. 
Companies throw off interim signals (revenue, talent, retention) long before then.

Of course, these are medians, and the spread is wide. Being in or out of vogue can swing the timeline hard either way.

So, who's got it worse? 
Hint: whoever’s in market now 😉… more

📉 Represent 45% of funds closed (down from 70% a decade ago)

US VC AUM has more than doubled over the last five years. There's a scenario the next five reverse it.

Pitchbook published a 2030 private markets outlook.

Three things I'm sitting with:

1️⃣ The base case outlook for VC is stagnation. That's after doubling over the last five years and tripling over the last ten. The bear case has AUM shrinking 22%. What does that do to capital-intense startups raising early stage today?

2️⃣ The bull case depends on AI doing two things: absorbing capital AND eventually returning it. The first is happening but must persist. The second is unproven but given major IPOs on the horizon, soon to be answered.

3️⃣ AUM growth is mega-fund driven, especially in recent years. To keep growing, we need more mega funds or bigger ones. It won't come from small funds… in 2025 the16z fundraise alone equaled all emerging funds closed combined.

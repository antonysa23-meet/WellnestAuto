I think we've hit peak on closed models.

Models will keep going better, but I think we're at the peak of reason to chase the newest closed model. This is because we are at a convergence point of three things happening:

1. We are at the top of the S-curve for many tasks already, you don't need a Mythos/Fable level model for most tasks. Opus is fine.

2. Open source models like GLM-5.2 are showing performance at the level of Opus; they are now a possibility of being a daily driver for many tasks.

3. We're getting squeezed by the foundation AI model companies; they are going public this year and need to show revenue growth.

When Fable went away, I switched back to Opus for the tasks I'm doing - and somehow, it didn't feel like a huge difference. While Fable would try much harder to get a task done, many of the tasks my agents are taking on are much simpler - following up on sales, fixing bugs, improving a feature, organizing our issues - these are all not as challenging as trying to red-team attack a production system. 

The latest jump over the last model felt incremental on a day to day basis. It is definitely more powerful, but I'm not sure if I'd pay a multiple more to use it.

Meanwhile, GLM 5.2 crossed a threshold two weeks ago. Open weights aren't a benchmark curiosity anymore, and we are seeing people running them as a daily driver. While it's not Mythos-class yet, it's close enough that the model choice decision changes.

When the gap shrinks, the premium on pricing has to be earned task by task. The frontier might still be worth it for the hardest jobs. But if most of your agent traffic runs fine on something cheaper, that will be the option most companies will start gravitating towards.

This pressure is going to grow because the big labs need public-market margins: and if customers get more price-sensitive, open weights will keep gaining market share. You also get sovereignty on the intelligence model, and no one can take that model away from you.

So if you're figuring out how to agentify your business, one of the things to do now is to set yourself up for this multi-AI future. Critically, you want your agent's memory to be something you own, and also the governance and controls to what data the agent can access via MCPs. … more

⏰ 10/45 is the new rule of software development ⏰

Software engineers will write hundreds of lines of code in 10 minutes but spend 45 minutes to review it. This is the 10/45 rule of AI code. 

The problem with AI-generated code isn't just token costs. Many treat it like free code, but there’s no free lunch. The labor is simply shifting from writing to verification and context management. The 10/45 rule highlights the invisible costs now eroding productivity:

🤝 The Labor Tax of Trust: 45% of developers say debugging AI-generated code takes longer than writing it themselves. Trust has dropped because a system that’s fast but unreliable shifts the time spent from creation to fixing and validating.
📝 The Labor Tax of Intent: The richest artifact—the prompt and generation session—disappears the moment the code is committed, leaving engineers with no context for future architectural decisions.
📚 The Labor Tax of Context Drift: Teams are creating files like AGENTS.md to guide agents, but keeping these files in sync with the actual codebase requires manual maintenance and discipline, turning the context layer into a liability.
The foundation of software development needs to be rebuilt to account for this new reality. There is a massive window for new engineering tools built for the age of AI. We are actively looking to back founders in the four core areas that will define the new dev stack:

1. Prompt and session capture as a platform primitive
2. Code review rebuilt for agent-generated diffs
3. Context management at the repo level
4. Community and contribution infrastructure

If you’re building to rebuild the dev stack, we’d love to meet you.

When I started Eisen, a lot of smart people told me escheatment sounded like a small market. States are sitting on $70 billion of people's retirement savings because of it.

Every bank, brokerage, and exchange in the country is legally required to find owners of dormant accounts and return their money before the state takes it. These aren't abandoned accounts. They're retirement savings that someone hasn't logged into recently, or brokerage holdings a family never found after someone passed away. Millions of dormant accounts every year, and every institution in the country still manages this process on spreadsheets.

Last year, states rewrote the dormancy rules for crypto and the problem got bigger by an order of magnitude - bringing millions of accounts at the largest exchanges in the world into regulatory scope overnight.

We've spent two years building the platform to solve every one of these use cases, and have signed 40+ institutions including Adyen, AllianceBernstein, Binance.US, and BitGo. We just raised our Series A. It's time to grow.

I'm hiring our Founding Marketer to define the next stage of our growth. Looking for someone who's been a senior B2B marketer through a scale phase, ideally in fintech or compliance.

Here's what you'd own: 
- The full growth function for a company expanding from one compliance vertical into a multi-vertical platform. 
- The go-to-market playbook for financial institutions, crypto exchanges, and eventually insurance and telecom. 
- How this category gets defined and who defines it.
- The marketing org you build from scratch, and the budget that comes with it.

This is demand gen for a market where the demand exists by law. We need someone who can take what's working and turn it into the growth story this market hasn't seen yet.

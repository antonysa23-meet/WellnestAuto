About
Katelin is Founding Partner at Alexis Ohanian's early-stage venture capital firm, 776. With over $1B of raised capital, the firm is dedicated to reshaping venture capital by empowering visionary entrepreneurs to tackle humanity’s biggest challenges while driving exceptional returns. As an investor, Katelin is interested in solutions that push the boundaries of human potential and resilience—advancing health, exploring new frontiers, driving sustainability, fostering creativity, and enriching the human condition.

Having spent her early career at Pixar Animation Studios, Katelin brings over 20 years of operational experience in building and scaling teams, products, and cultures to the world of venture. As a former senior executive at influential startups such as Klout and Reddit, Katelin has a unique perspective on the intersection of people and technology. Leveraging her skills and credentials in human resources, business operations, and culture building, she is committed to helping evolve the next generation of entrepreneurs and the broader venture ecosystem.

Featured
Reshared by Katelin Holloway

Two lessons have shaped how I think about venture investing.

First: Founders rarely fail because they lack vision. They fail because their systems can’t keep up with their ambition.

Second: You can’t scale a product faster than you can scale trust.

Both lessons came from operating long before I ever wrote a venture check.

Every journey has its transformative beats. Early in my career, I was a public school teacher. Later I spent five formative years at Pixar, where I saw firsthand how intentional culture design — feedback loops, creative trust, psychological safety — protected excellence. After that came the turnaround at Reddit, where our job was not simply to scale the company, but to restore trust across a global community and organization under pressure.

Across all of those experiences, one pattern repeated itself: most crises inside companies aren’t technical. They’re human systems breaking under growth.

The same dynamic shows up in venture-backed companies every day. Founders move incredibly fast... often faster than their teams, processes, or incentives can support. When those systems lag behind ambition, friction appears. Decision-making slows. Trust erodes. Execution suffers.

That’s why the work of supporting founders often looks like empathy from the outside. In reality, it’s much closer to risk management.

At Seven Seven Six 7️⃣7️⃣6️⃣, Alexis and I built the firm with that philosophy in mind. Early-stage investing looks like finance from the outside. In practice it’s a lot of time spent helping people and organizations figure out how to grow without breaking.

I can't tell you how much I enjoyed this conversation with Ted Seides on the Capital Allocators podcast about the experiences that shaped that perspective, and how we try to apply those lessons across our portfolio today.

For anyone interested in the intersection of venture, human systems, and long-term company building, I hope you find something of value in here. Thanks for hosting me, Ted!


Human Side of Venture Investing at 776 - Capital Allocators with Ted Seides

542,000 industrial robots were installed in factories last year. Nearly half of all the new ones in the US went into auto plants. But here's the wild part: almost **none** of those machines were built here.

Most of us think of AI as something that lives on our screens, builds our software, makes our lives more efficient. But the version that's going to reshape the most jobs, in the most communities, is the one showing up on factory floors. Robotics, machine vision, the systems that move material and inspect quality. And right now the supply chain for Physical AI runs largely through China.

I keep coming back to the people side of this. When the machines that build our cars (and eventually our planes and a lot of our defense systems) are designed and made somewhere else, we don't just lose manufacturing margin, we lose the muscle memory of knowing how to make things... and we lose the jobs that come with it.

This is one of the areas I'm deeply curious about right now. Not because robots are cool (though they are completely and undeniably badass), but because whoever builds the tools that build everything else gets a very influential say in what gets made, where, and by whom.

If you're working on this, I want to talk and would like to meet the IRL cast of Wall-E, please!… more

Huge congrats to Interlune on securing a NASA SBIR Phase III award! 👏

This is the part of the innovation cycle that matters most: when breakthrough R&D gets the support to become real, deployable infrastructure. In this case, technology designed to measure and extract resources directly from lunar regolith, including helium-3 and hydrogen.

You cannot build a real lunar economy if every critical resource has to be shipped from Earth. Learning how to use what already exists on the Moon is what turns exploration into permanence.

It’s exciting to see NASA continue leaning into partnerships with venture-backed companies that are willing to take on the hard engineering challenges required to scale these systems. The intersection of public mission and private execution is where some of the most important progress happens.

This is how we move from exploration to infrastructure, gang. So proud of the Interlune team for making it happen!… more

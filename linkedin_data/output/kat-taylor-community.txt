In December, Beneficial State Foundation submitted a public comment to the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau urging it to preserve fair lending frameworks that are proven to responsibly expand credit access. Unfortunately, the Bureau recently finalized new rules that, among other things, eliminate disparate impact analysis, undercut Special Purpose Credit Programs, and limit data collection that could help identify which small business borrowers are being denied financing. 

For anyone working inside a bank, credit union, or CDFI, here's why this matters.

Disparate impact analysis is a diagnostic tool. It helps lenders catch the moments when a facially neutral policy — a minimum loan size, a rigid credit score cutoff, a thin-file rule — quietly screens out borrowers who are likely to repay. Through our Underwriting for Racial Justice Lender Pilot, I've watched 17 mission-aligned lenders use exactly this kind of analysis to find qualified borrowers their old models were missing, creating better lending practices for their institutions.

Similarly, Special Purpose Credit Programs have allowed lenders to address documented disparities and provide additional underwriting flexibility for eligible applicants. The new rule effectively eliminates these programs by creating an unattainable evidentiary standard.

The Bureau's rationale is that fifty years of fair lending laws have closed credit gaps, but Federal Reserve data tells a different story. Black and Hispanic mortgage applicants are still rejected at rates nearly twice those of white applicants with similar financial profiles, and pay higher rates when approved — even controlling for income, credit score, and down payment size. People of color are also rejected at higher rates for small business loans, and the new rule about Section 1071 will make it harder to systemically track (and correct) those patterns.

Our allies at Rise Economy – along with the National Fair Housing Alliance and two private companies that work on fair lending compliance – have filed a lawsuit to block CFPB from enacting the new rule related to the Equal Credit Opportunity Act. 

Meanwhile, here's where we are. The federal floor just got lower. But community-focused banking doesn't depend on regulators. We can continue to do that from within.

If you’re a banking leader and want to talk through how to maintain rigorous fair lending practices in this environment, our door is open.

This is, indeed, a pivotal moment for the future of school meals. Tens of millions of children across the country depend on healthy school meals as a dependable source of nutrition.

Programs that provide grants for kitchen infrastructure, staff training, and funding for farm-to-school initiatives are vital components of school nutrition. As Nancy Brown, CEO for the American Heart Association, writes in this article: “Schools are uniquely positioned to make healthy choices familiar and appealing to children.” The Trump administration’s budget request to cut such programs threatens the effectiveness of school meal programs in combating the affordability crisis, preventing future health consequences, and improving student learning outcomes.

California is choosing a different path. Last year’s state budget included full funding for the School Meals for All program and funding for the complementary Kitchen Infrastructure and Training program, which supports school nutrition professionals with training and equipment to serve more freshly prepared, locally sourced school meals. 

Our 225-strong coalition of partners that make up #SchoolMealsforAll, continues to fight every day to protect school meals funding in order to deliver health and opportunity for every child. Without adequate funding, schools struggle to deliver on their promise to students and families.

About
As Managing Partner at NewBuild Venture Capital since 2022, I invest in early-stage enterprise and supply chain software companies. Leveraging expertise in early-stage investment, finance, and supply chain thought leadership, NewBuild focuses on seed and Series A funding for innovative solutions in areas such as supply chain infrastructure, edge computing, AI, and warehouse automation.  

With over 20 years of experience in early-stage venture investing, my professional journey is deeply rooted in empowering startups to achieve impactful growth. My work reflects a commitment to fostering innovation, investing in resilient founders, sponsoring successful women, and advancing technologies that enhance operational efficiency and productivity.

Before starting NewBuild, I was a Partner at NEA, NGP Capital and Intel Capital, investing in enterprise and deeptech companies.

Outside of my work as a venture investor, I am deeply involved in alumni giving to IIT Madras and am a board member of the MIT Club of Northern California.

I recently had the pleasure of speaking with Rohini Chakravarthy, Managing Partner at NewBuild Venture Capital, for a Zero100 webinar. We were discussing enterprise AI, but we spent very little time talking about models or vendors.
 
Instead, we talked about the familiar “build, buy, or outsource” dilemma, and how AI has completely changed the math.
 
Rohini offers a fascinating vantage point. While many of us see AI through the lens of running today's operations, she spends her time looking several years ahead, working with the founders building what enterprise technology may become.

It reinforced a theme that keeps coming up in my conversations with leaders.
 
A few reflections from our chat:
 
🔹 First, AI is changing the unit of value. Historically we bought software to support work. Increasingly we're buying capabilities that perform work. The conversation shifts from "Which application do we need?" to "Which capabilities should we own, orchestrate or shape?"

 🔹 Second, different parts of the technology stack are commoditising at different rates.. AI is changing the economics of build versus buy because every layer of the stack is evolving differently. Competitive advantage won't come from owning everything. It'll come from recognising which capabilities are becoming commodities and which remain strategic.
 
🔹 Finally, while much of the AI conversation still focuses on models and vendors, the real story is the convergence of AI with robotics and connected infrastructure. It’s closing the gap between knowing something and actually doing something.

Taken together, these aren't just technology shifts. They're changing the economics of the enterprise.

As an investor in chips and hardware for 20+ years, it has been awesome to see new optimism and capital come in to these sectors in this age of AI. But there is also a nagging worry on whether the bullish valuations we see have fundamentals attached or just Silicon Valley hype. We got to discuss this and more with entrepreneurs, experts and co-investors from across physical AI at a dinner we co-hosted with PwC last week. 

We didn't resolve the bubble question, of course, but we left with important insights to frame our investment process in the coming months:  

1) Physical AI has an expanded aperture compared to the last robotics boom in warehouses and manufacturing a decade ago. Autonomous driving has been a significant market expansion, and defense, healthcare, and consumer applications will all be market adders that can justify a new horizontal rail. Infrastructure software companies that were previously locked in by distribution and small market size MAY make sense in this new world.

2) While the open internet and open-source repositories provided training data for foundational models, data in industrial and supply chain environments are closed within enterprises. Entrepreneurs are bridging this gap by offering innovative services that deliver value today while also gathering training data for the future. 

Thank you to everyone who joined us! And to our co-hosts, Monica Nayar, Jason Burton, Romit Dey and Rohit Nayak of PwC.

“Where’s my stuff”? Is the most common question in enterprise supply chain operations. 

We have gotten used to precise tracking for consumer goods and services, but complex enterprise supply chains often lack that visibility. With datacenter buildouts, EVs and home electrification driving huge demand, this is an (acute problem and) opportunity for electrical distribution to modernize supply side operations.

National Association of Electrical Distributors (NAED) has selected the IDEA (Idea4industry) - Pull Logic joint venture to build “Where’s My Stuff?”, the electrical distribution channel’s first industry-owned supply chain visibility solution. 🎉

Today, tracking material status across the electrical channel still depends on phone calls, spreadsheets, disconnected portals, emails, and manual follow-ups. That creates delays, uncertainty, and fragmented visibility across every handoff.

Project Nexus is designed to connect the systems that manufacturers, representatives, and distributors already use to create trusted, real-time visibility into orders and shipments across the channel. 🚀

The platform is designed to:
• Synchronize order and shipment updates across authorized trading partners
• Provide visibility as orders move from confirmed to in production to shipped
• Reduce reliance on manual tracking and multiple supplier portals
• Proactively flag estimated ship date changes and potential delays
• Support role-based permissions so companies can control what information is shared

The result is a trusted, real-time source of truth for order visibility delivered within existing workflows and enterprise systems.

Proud to work alongside NAED and IDEA on an initiative focused on improving material tracking and supply chain visibility across the electrical industry.

View company: Realta Fusion
Realta Fusion

 

4d • Edited • 

🧲 🪞 We just did something no commercial #fusion company has ever done before: demonstrate direct energy conversion (DEC) of plasma kinetic #energy into electricity. 🧲 🪞

This is real #science. This is real #engineering. This is real #history.

(See links to our press release and a blog with more details on this historic achievement in the comments below.)

On June 19th, the Realta Fusion team and our collaborators at University of Wisconsin-Madison successfully drew multiple amps of current at around 100 volts from the kinetic energy of the plasma in the Wisconsin HTS Axisymmetric Mirror (WHAM) machine, enough to power several light bulbs.

#DEC has been demonstrated in national laboratory and academic settings over the years, and several private fusion companies have the integration of DEC technology on their roadmaps, but nobody in commercial fusion has ever actually demonstrated that it works on a real machine until now.

Our CEO Kieran Furlong said it best: “People have been talking a big game about DEC for years, so we just went out and did it.”

To be clear: this is a first #demonstration that DEC works on WHAM, not that we have made net electricity from fusion. That milestone is still ahead of us. That said, this is a real, working proof-of-concept that puts us closer to fusion power plants that are genuinely cost-competitive.

Huge credit to the whole Realta Fusion team and our collaborators at UW-Madison for pulling off this historic feat.

Bob Mumgaard

  • 3rd+

CEO of Commonwealth Fusion Systems

1mo • 

Fusion energy has plenty of challenges, but the first is always plasma physics. You have to control superhot fusion fuel in a way that’s agreeable to the laws of the universe. Today, in a set of five peer-reviewed papers, we’re documenting how we’re confident we’ll be able to do that with our ARC fusion power plant.

The papers, written by 58 experts from CFS and collaborating universities and research institutions, show the scientific foundation. That starts with calculations and simulations backing up our expectation that an ARC plant will produce 1.1 gigawatts of fusion power that we can convert into 400 megawatts of net electricity for the grid. And the papers address how we’ll tackle fusion challenges like heat exhaust, disruptions, and plasma stability.

The bottom line: The science is solid. There are no showstoppers. We begin with decades of tokamak knowledge, extend that with what we’ve learned already from our SPARC demonstration machine, and fine-tune it with what we’ll learn from operating SPARC. Our commercial success is contingent on this successful de-risking of the core science.

We published these papers in the Journal of Plasma Physics from Cambridge University Press through the peer-review process. That brings the independent validation of our work that’s crucial to establishing trust in our results. There’s a reason why peer review is the gold standard in the scientific community: It’s the best way to distinguish ideas from reality. In fusion, where the science and engineering are tough, peer-reviewed research helps leaders, investors, customers, and citizens make informed and unbiased decisions about who to trust and where to invest their dollars.

Congratulations to all who contributed to these papers, and thank you to the Journal of Plasma Physics for taking on their publication. Today the global fusion community can celebrate a new win in the advancement of the last energy source humanity will ever need.… more

View company: Commonwealth Fusion Systems
Commonwealth Fusion Systems

 

1mo • 

Today, CFS laid the physics foundation for our ARC fusion power plant.

With five deeply researched papers — validated by independent peer review and now published in the Journal of Plasma Physics — we’ve shown why we’re confident that we’ve nailed the scientific basics of producing copious amounts of fusion power and handling fusion’s biggest challenges.

⚛️ How we’re proving the science
Fusion, the power of the stars, is hard to bring to Earth. A power plant must control a superhot, unruly cloud of charged particles called a plasma so those particles fuse together and release energy. We’re proving our approach with our SPARC tokamak, the machine we’re building now in Massachusetts, and these new research papers show how our SPARC approach largely carries over to our ARC power plant.

The collective assessment from the papers’ 58 co-authors at CFS and collaborating institutions: This machine will work. No scientific breakthroughs are required to bring this clean, secure, abundant source of energy to the electricity grid. Their research builds on decades of tokamak research, backed up by supercomputer simulations that model the plasma down to the level of individual plasma particles.

“Designing a fusion power plant that we truly intend to build soon means that we had to ask the right questions, sharpen our tools, and focus on the top-priority information that SPARC will teach us,” said Alex Creely, Chief Engineer of ARC Conceptual Design at CFS.

⚡ Powering the grid
The new papers explain how our tokamak will use extremely strong magnets to manage our plasma, confining it at high enough temperature and density for a long enough time to generate 1.1 gigawatts (GW) of fusion power. We’ll convert that into 400 megawatts (MW) of continuous net electricity for the grid, enough to power about 280,000 average American homes.

The papers also discuss how we’ll handle major challenges. That includes handling the heat exhaust that’s a key practical constraint for tokamaks, managing disruptions that can derail power generation if not handled well, and keeping the plasma stable for top performance. Swipe through the carousel below to see some of the details.

🚀 What comes next
These results are based on a recent ARC plant design, but already we’ve begun working on improvements. The tools we developed for these papers let us integrate different aspects of fusion physics so we can rapidly try new improvements. The core science will still largely apply.

If you want to check our math, the full papers are available online. With this transparency, you don’t have to take our assertions on faith.

As Principal Scientist Jon Hillesheim put it: “Fusion progress sometimes has been slow. But now in the physics community, people are looking at us and realizing we’re really pushing fusion forward.”

View company: Commonwealth Fusion Systems
Commonwealth Fusion Systems

 

3mo • 

Commonwealth Fusion Systems announced today we’ll design and build high-temperature superconducting (HTS) magnets for Realta Fusion in a long-term strategic partnership that’ll benefit both companies’ efforts to bring this new form of clean, affordable energy to the electricity grid.

Realta, a spinoff from the University of Wisconsin-Madison, is focusing on a type of fusion technology called a magnetic mirror, a cousin to the tokamaks that CFS is building for our own fusion energy machines. A magnetic mirror fusion machine confines its fusion fuel between two sets of powerful magnets, and HTS magnets have breathed new life into the approach by enabling stronger magnetic fields and smaller devices.

CFS made the magnets for the University of Wisconsin’s WHAM experiment, an effort that yielded record magnetic fields for the technology, and the Realta partnership extends that work. We’ll design and build magnets not just for the next device on Realta's technology road map, but also its commercial-scale successor and for the power plants that follow.

Through the deal, Realta can tap into CFS’ deep, proven expertise in magnet manufacturing, design, supply chain development, service, and operation. That helps Realta’s fusion development work move swiftly and economically. And for CFS, the deal holds the potential for billions of dollars in revenue as Realta’s business matures.

CFS isn’t changing course. We’re still pursuing our own tokamak technology and plan to connect our own ARC fusion power plants to the grid in the early 2030s. But manufacturing HTS magnets for other fusion efforts is part of our mission to make the fusion energy transition happen as fast and as widely as possible. With Realta as an ally, there are more shots on goal — more chances for fusion to succeed with different technologies, different markets, and different customers.

CFS believes in a robust fusion ecosystem. This partnership helps CFS, helps Realta, helps fusion, and ultimately helps humanity.

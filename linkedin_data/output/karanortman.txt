Merchandise matters: superserving the women’s sports fan

With an estimated $4 billion total addressable women’s sports merchandise market in the US alone, there is a golden moment for organizations and athletes to professionalize and optimize their retail footprint. Nearly 80% of fans say they would purchase more if given the chance - retail is a significant growth lever.

Monarch Commerce, Inc.'s partnership with Atlanta Dream WNBA represents a critical shift toward the intentional professionalization of franchise retail. By approaching licensed merchandise with a women’s-sport-first perspective, they provide a strategic business tool for teams and athletes to capture the demand and drive revenue. 

Here is why this is a game-changer for organizations and athletes:

Closing the Monetization Gap: We aren't seeing a lack of interest; we're seeing a failure of infrastructure. 60% of active buyers walk away due to items being out of stock - solving for supply by investing in inventory is a move to capture immediate, high-margin revenue that is currently being left on the table.

Turning Identity into Brand Equity: Women’s sports fans view merchandise as a "cultural badge." Yet fans are 60% less likely to find styles that they like than men’s sports fans. Aligning product design with modern fan culture, and marketing it appropriately, is the fastest way to drive brand relevance, and leverage fans as an organic marketing engine.

Commerce as the 365-Day Stadium: 67% of women’s sports fans have bought merch, while only 49% have attended a game, making the commerce engine a primary brand entry point. Viewing merch as a sophisticated, year-round engagement platform that lives far beyond the final whistle can unlock the next surge in revenue and fan growth. 

Treating your commerce engine as a direct extension of your brand is a fundamental move for anyone building a high-performance sports business, and at Monarch we are committed to backing infrastructure that scales this industry. 

The potential of this market is ready to be unlocked by those willing to think and act differently, reach out to join us.

Last week I had the privilege of speaking at the The Global Impact Investing Network West Coast Impact Investing Forum - and the conversation that followed reminded me why this work matters.

My message was simple, but the data behind it is hard to ignore: the managers we've been calling "emerging" have consistently outperformed their established counterparts since 1997. And yet, their share of venture capital dollars has collapsed - from 46% in 2002 to just 14% in 2023. Last year, half of all VC funding went to nine firms.

That's not a market efficiently allocating capital. That's inertia dressed up as due diligence.

Here's the tension I keep coming back to: investors should be focused on finding non-consensus opportunities to drive outsized returns. And yet, many of us are applying that same conventional thinking when it comes to manager selection. We gravitate toward established names, familiar pedigrees, incumbent funds.

I made the case for retiring "emerging manager" in favor of "next-generation manager" - because language shapes behavior, and right now our language is leading us toward the familiar.

To fellow LPs: our allocation decisions are a form of market signal. When we over-index on incumbency, we reinforce it. The question isn't whether next-gen managers are ready. It's whether we are.

If you've been defaulting to established names, I'd encourage you to interrogate that instinct. The data may surprise you.… more

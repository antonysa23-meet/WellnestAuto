I've been married over 30 years. 

It's been amazing. I wouldn't trade it for the world.

BlackLake Security is run by myself, my partner Mark J., and our wives. 

The human aspects of those relationships are obviously important. 

But the biggest advice my wife has given me over three decades wasn't about vendors, security, or business strategy. 

It was very human: "Quit sweating the small stuff." I know, I know. 

There are books written about it. It's become a cliché. 

But here's the thing about clichés, they become clichés because they're true, and we still need to hear them. I've always been detail-oriented. 

If some little detail isn't like I thought it was going to be, it messes my head up. I'm really good at not micromanaging, I let my team run with what they need to run with. 

But when it comes to details? I used to hold onto all of them. Every small thing that went wrong, every minor deviation from the plan, every tiny imperfection, I carried it all. 

And it was exhausting. As I've gotten older and wiser and actually listened to her, I've learned to let go. She constantly reminds me: don't sweat all the little details and small stuff. 

Let it go. And honestly? It's really helped me. 

We've been able to scale this company because of it. Here's what I've learned: being detail-oriented is valuable. Obsessing over every detail is paralyzing. 

There's a massive difference between the two, and knowing when to care and when to let go is maybe the most important leadership skill nobody talks about. In cybersecurity, we deal with details all day long. Configurations, logs, policies, vulnerabilities, everything matters. 

But if you treat every single detail with the same level of urgency, you burn out. Your team burns out. And ironically, you miss the details that actually matter because you're drowning in the ones that don't. My wife taught me something that no business book ever could: not everything that feels important is actually important. 

And the wisdom to know the difference doesn't come from more analysis, it comes from trust. 

Trust in your team. Trust in the process. And sometimes, trust that things will work out even when they're not perfect. 

The companies that scale aren't the ones with leaders who control everything. They're the ones with leaders who know what to control and what to release. 

So here's what I'm curious about: What's the one piece of advice from your spouse or partner that changed how you lead, not through business wisdom, but through life wisdom?… more

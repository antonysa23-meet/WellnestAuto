Just returned from the The Wall Street Journal’s annual Food Forum, and I’m struck by something: in our deeply polarized political climate, the food industry may be one of the spaces where we find bipartisan consensus. 

Four themes emerged that have me thinking:

🌾 Immigration is Essential Infrastructure:
Industry leaders across the board openly acknowledged that our food system simply cannot function without immigrant labor. It is an operational reality.

⚠️ Ultra-Processed Foods Need Regulation:
There’s growing consensus that highly processed foods should come with warning labels and stronger oversight. Of course, we need good science to back these claims and not everyone agrees on what that means. But, the starting point — food is health — is a rallying cry. 

📈 Commodity Volatility is the New Normal:
Leaders are no longer assuming price stability in cocoa, coffee, and even meat. They’ll need to build business models that can withstand price fluctuation. Although one CEO said that he is in a wait and see and will not reactionarily raise prices, this issue came up in almost all discussions. 

🛡️ Food Safety as National Security:
When a single egg farmer has to cull 6 million birds due to avian flu, partisan differences become irrelevant. Food safety threats should be viewed through a national security lens — one that that unites, rather than divides.

The bigger question: Is food the industry where we can actually come together at one table, across the aisle? Unlike many policy areas, food touches everyone’s daily life in immediate, personal ways. The industry’s pragmatic needs force honest conversations, and those could bypass typical political posturing.

What do you think? Can the food system be a model for how we tackle other complex challenges together? Can we start at one, long table?

“How do we build the next Silicon Valley?” is a silly question that I get asked dozens of times per year. 

There won’t be another Silicon Valley (unless somehow SF manages to shoot its own golden goose with poor governance - and even then, it would take decades). 

BUT there are some steps that anyone interested in building their local startup ecosystems can take and they may not be as obvious as you expect:

1) Be happy when a local company gets a Series B from an SF fund.

Whoa, shouldn’t we be annoyed? We need these local champions to take local capital and stay local, right? Nope.

There aren’t really many places to raise a serious Series B/C/D these days. How many funds in Dallas could lead a $100M Series C? And that’s not to pick on Dallas - I’ve had this conversation with fund managers from Seattle to Sydney. Letting the winners go is the right call.

2) Deepen strengths rather than shoring up weaknesses

A startup ecosystem will naturally coalesce around an area’s core competency. Biotech in San Diego, Logistics in Bentonville, Government/Defense in D.C. Don’t fight that pull, lean into it. And then the area attracts nearby talent you can expand naturally.

3) Convince local rich people to be angels

This is one of the core advantages in SV. Everyone made their money in tech, so investing into young startups is sort of the expectation rather than some wild risk. Not so in communities where the capital came from other industries. Fund managers who evangelize locally to prospective angels are doing the real work.

Don’t build the next Silicon Valley. Build the first thriving startup ecosystem in hundreds of cities across the US (or indeed the world). 

Just some musing while I'm out on sabbatical. Will only be posting sporadically, apologies in advance for the lack of daily data!… more

I thoroughly enjoyed speaking at the "New Moonshots in Manufacturing" session at the World Economic Forum’s Advanced Manufacturing and Supply Chain Forum in San Francisco last week.

What I loved most about the conversation was the chance to reflect on the history of oil refining, the industry where I learned how to be a chemical engineer, and how poetically, we are now borrowing the same playbook to transform plant waste into the next generation of carbon building blocks.

This matters because today, a small number of players hold concentrated power over the carbon building blocks behind nearly every essential good. In times of war or disruption, supply chains break, prices spike, and entire industries become vulnerable. That model doesn't scale.

The billions of tons of plant waste generated each year are our opportunity to diversify that power. But unlocking potential requires transformation to make sure the molecules we make fit within existing value chains. (hi biomanufacturing 👋 )

The ideas I shared were centered on building around real market pull. It's okay if we don't make glucose today, there are other high value compounds coming out of these waste streams that can build profitable business right now. I shared three principles we follow to give us flexibility to produce and compete, which is the core power of refining:

🫛 feedstock flexibility
📈 market versatility
🏭 co-production

Rockefeller helped build modern civilization by valorizing waste streams. Hyfé is unlocking waste to help scale civilization’s next chapter. Thanks for the opportunity to share, WEF!

Confronting the Coffee Crisis

MIT Press · Jan 1, 2008

Show publication
Overview
Our morning cups of coffee connect us to a global industry and an export crisis in the tropics that is destroying livelihoods, undermining the cohesion of families and communities, and threatening ecosystems. Confronting the Coffee Crisis explores small-scale farming, the political economy of the global coffee industry, and initiatives that claim to promote more sustainable rural development in coffee-producing communities. Contributors review the historical, political, economic, and agroecological processes within today's coffee industry and analyze the severely depressed export market that faces small-scale growers in Mexico and Central America. 

The book presents a series of interdisciplinary, empirically rich case studies showing how small-scale farmers manage ecosystems and organize collectively as they seek useful collaborations with international NGOs and coffee companies to create opportunities for themselves in the coffee market. The findings demonstrate the interconnections among farmer livelihoods, biodiversity, conservation, and changing coffee markets. Additional chapters examine alternative trade practices, certification, and eco-labeling, discussing the politics and market growth of organic, shade-grown, and Fair Trade coffees. Combining interdisciplinary research with case-study analysis at scales ranging from the local to the global, Confronting the Coffee Crisis reveals the promise and the perils of efforts to create a more sustainable coffee industry.

I am excited to announce Chingona Ventures Fund II close, the largest by a sole GP Latina. Where we represent 0.1% of all venture capitalists. 

Thank you to the founders that gave us a chance and let us support you, to the LPs that believed in us early on, and to the community that continues to support us. 

Two generations ago both my grandfathers were migrant farmworkers. One generation ago my parents moved my sister and I from Mexico to the U.S. and worked minimum wage jobs to give us a better life. I am now working to change the venture capital ecosystem and build wealth in a way we never thought possible.

Today is the day. Episode 3 of The Corridor is live, and it is one of the most clarifying conversations I have had about early-stage investing.

I sat down with Samara Mejia Hernandez, founding partner of Chingona Ventures, a high-conviction pre-seed firm that writes the first check into founders the rest of the industry overlooks.

A few things from this one will stay with me for a long time.

She told the story of a woman who had been a mechanic for thirty years, built a platform to automate the entire business, had real revenue, and got passed on by every VC she pitched. 

That same week, founders with nothing but a deck and the right network raised millions. 

That contrast became the entire thesis behind Chingona.

She broke down how AI just reset the math on early-stage diligence. The margins that used to signal a great software business, 70 to 80 percent, are now 20 to 40 percent for AI-native companies. 

The old moats do not hold. Trust, expertise, and being close to the problem do.

And she said the thing every overlooked founder needs to hear: you only need one yes. 

You might get ten thousand no's. It can come from a VC, a customer, a grant, a family member. 

You do not need everyone. You need a few early believers who are with you.

For our community especially, she named something real. We are often taught to be safe, not to take risks. Women especially are taught to be perfect. But failing while trying matters more than getting it perfect. Now is the time to take the risk.

This is a show about betting on the people the world tends to overlook. Samara lives that every day.

About
Mariam Naficy is Co-Founder and CEO of Arcade, the AI-based design operating platform for connecting buyers and manufacturers.  She is also the Founder and Chairman of Minted, the marketplace of independent artists.  Mariam is a serial entrepreneur and angel investor focused on businesses where taste and technology intersect, with a particular focus on e-commerce, marketplace, and communities.  Mariam founded and ran the first beauty e-commerce site in the US, Eve.com, in 1998, selling it to LVMH in 2000.  Mariam has made early investments in Reddit, Gusto, Color, Our Place, Madison Reed, Middesk and more.

2mo • 


In July 2008, TechCrunch made it clear they thought Minted was a terrible idea. 19 years later, my "terrible idea" now makes hundreds of millions in revenue each year.

"Few people are going to buy from a stationery store they've never heard of, giving designers little reason to submit their designs in the first place."

This was Minted's only launch coverage. 
No second chance to make a first impression.

At the time, the article was devastating. I had just spent the year building Minted around the clock, stealing hours while my newborn daughter slept.

Now, it’s one of the best things that ever happened to me. It taught me two things:

1. Protect the insight that only you can see.

Cards sold online in 2008 were ugly. I wanted them to be beautiful. I believed that there were extraordinary designers in the world who had no way to reach the market, and that crowdsourcing on the Internet was the way to find them.
Further, what TechCrunch missed was that cards are inherently viral - when a woman receives a beautiful card from a friend, she’ll turn it over to see who made it. Minted’s artists would make our cards beautiful, and our customers would make us known.

When the TechCrunch article hit (and at many subsequent moments), I thought to myself, “don’t give up on this company - no-one else recognizes its potential and loves it the way you do.” So I didn’t. I decided that Minted needed to live.
I was able to block the noise out and…

2. Listen only to what your customers are saying.

Before the TechCrunch article, Minted carried hundreds of SKUs from name-brand stationery companies. By every conventional measure, these brands should have been successful: they stood for quality. And still, I generated no sales in the first 30 days.

Then, I launched our first design competition. Artists would submit their designs, people would vote, and the winning art would be sold as stationery products. In the first month, we began selling 2-3 orders per week from our very first crowdsourced collection.

It was such a tiny sign of life, but it was there.

TechCrunch thought that sourcing from independent artists wasn’t going to work, but our customers told us something different. When we sourced holiday cards from indie artists, the results were explosive. By December 2008, I had to turn off marketing because we couldn’t fulfill demand.

The critics were loud, but they were just noise. Early customer signals, even tiny ones, are the data that actually matters.

Happy 19th Birthday, Minted and to its great team and artists!… more

3mo • 


In 2008, Minted’s crowdsourcing was my side-project. I built it at night with a college student I found on “Rent-A-Coder”. 
Today, we’re a 20,000 person creative community.

Out of the $2.5M we raised in our angel round, I spent less than $100K on what I actually wanted to build. For some reason, what was calling out to me was to start a design competition that would let unknown designers submit their work, with the winners’ work sold on Minted. This was not a logical idea, and investors were understandably counseling me against it.

At the time, the tried and true model was e-commerce 1.0: take the big brand names, put them online, make it convenient. In 2008, Silicon Valley was only just starting to wrap its head around crowdsourcing.

I'd tell investors the designs could be better if we went with unknown artists. But it's very hard to quantify that to a financial investor. What does "better design" mean to someone looking at a spreadsheet?

But I couldn't let it go.
I was captivated by the idea that I could empower talent hidden all over the world.

So… I found someone in Oregon on RentACoder(.)com (who I didn’t realize at the time was only 20) to build it with me at night. After everyone at Minted went home, he and I worked on building the competition and crowdsourcing functionality. 

Talking about you, Kamran P.! (Kamran has subsequently gone on to have a very successful career as a software engineer.)

It’s interesting that what calls to you from a gut perspective is sometimes the right move as an entrepreneur, even if you can’t prove your hunch.

At Camp Minted, the power of the community felt so tangible. Artists, illustrators, and graphic designers from around the world came together to learn and share work. This year, I spoke to our community about how to navigate their careers in the age of AI.

What started as a passion project built at night is now a living, breathing community of people earning a real living through their creativity.

Thank you to all Minties for being part of our community - you are the best group of people I’ve ever had the privilege of working with.

I loved seeing you, and look forward to seeing you again next year! 🎨… more

4mo • Edited • 


Only 5 people came to the first Minted meetup. As we begin Camp Minted today in Nashville, we are now a community of 20,000. 
Nobody thought it would work.

In the early days of Minted, we faced constant doubt from others. No one really believed in what new (and sometimes self-taught) designers could do. Someone asked me early on, "who are these designers, anyway?"

The assumption was that great design only lived in art schools and famous studios. That self-taught creators couldn't compete with people who had the credentials and the résumés.

But when we put everyone in the same competition, the best work won. Every time. It didn't matter where someone went to school or whether anyone had heard of them. It showed that there were so many talented people out there who just hadn't been given a real shot.

That's what building Minted was really about for me. Building a true meritocracy for independent artists and designers. And do not fear - in the age of AI content proliferation, human designers and curation are even more important.

I have great memories and photos from meetups all over the US and around the world. Small gatherings in cities everywhere. Thank you Minted artists, for sharing your stories, your careers and the moment you realized you could actually do this full time.

Watching this community grow into 20,000 people, I still can't believe that we found each other.

So grateful for you. See you soon. 🎨… more

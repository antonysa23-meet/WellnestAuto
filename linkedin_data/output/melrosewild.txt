Love this. So important and incredibly true — a must-read for all founders.

I’ve sat in hundreds of company meetings, investor pitches and board meetings and often wondered how certain people ended up on those cap tables. I can tell they were there from founder desperation instead of alignment. 

I’ve also had to figure out how to move difficult investors out of companies. And this happens more often than you think, especially for the women I service. 

For female founders, it can feel like you don’t have that power — but in actuality, it’s the only way to raise capital and keep your power. 

Be as organized as possible before you meet investors and have a team ready to go. Yes AI can help you build alone to a certain point but investors do not bet on solo shows because if something happens to you, their investment immediately becomes a loss. 

You also must show how deeply you care for your business the way you would care for a child, because they genuinely require the same energy and commitment as a newborn to get up and running. The capital constraints around female entrepreneurs are real, and while things are changing, we still have to navigate through the noise to find the best possible allies. We still have to build with less support and smaller checks - so you better make sure you have the right people in your corner! 

So always ask questions. Get to know people before you share your baby with them.

Aligned values are critical to growing and scaling something — a business that can truly serve your market. Interview and screen your investors. Understand who they are and why they’re looking at your space. Never beg for or accept money from just anyone.

If you don’t design and control your cap table from the start, it will often end up controlling you.

Costa Rica’s Forest Carbon Paying the People Who Keep the Trees

In the mid-1980s, Costa Rica had cut down so much forest that only 21% of the country remained covered by trees.

Forests were being cleared for cattle ranching at a pace that placed Costa Rica among the world’s deforestation hotspots.

Today, the picture is completely different. Nearly 60% of the country is forest again, and in 2026 Costa Rica became the first country in the world to have its national forest carbon removals independently verified under one of the highest international standards for carbon credits.

But the most important part of the story is not the carbon credits.

It is how Costa Rica made forests economically valuable to the people living with them.

In 1996, the country passed a law that restricted forest clearing and created a system that pays landowners to keep forests standing, restore degraded land, and protect watersheds and biodiversity.

Much of the funding came from a fuel tax.

In simple terms, the people burning carbon helped pay the people protecting the forests that absorb it.

Over time, the system expanded to reach local communities, Indigenous territories, and women, directing climate finance closer to the people managing the land.

The model is not perfect. Questions remain about long-term financing and how much of the forest would have survived without payments.

But the broader lesson is hard to ignore.

Costa Rica did not rely on temporary campaigns or short-term projects. It built a financial system that rewarded conservation year after year.

What stays with me is the quiet practicality of it.

Instead of treating forest as land waiting to become something more profitable, Costa Rica made keeping it standing economically worthwhile.

Years ago, a former environment minister summed it up in one sentence:

“The pocket is the quickest way to the heart.”

Costa Rica reminds us that climate finance works best when it reaches the people making decisions on the ground.

When protecting nature creates real value, conservation stops being a sacrifice and becomes a source of income.

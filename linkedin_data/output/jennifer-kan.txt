Last week at the National Academy of Sciences, I had the honor of opening the GUIPRR–National Science Board (NSB) Joint Summit — a gathering of leaders across government, universities, philanthropy, and venture who share a common mission:

To rebuild America’s science funding system for speed, scale, and shared purpose.

In my keynote, I shared the story of our portfolio company Colossal Biosciences and explored the emergence of a new economic playbook for science, one that leverages non-traditional organizational structures and funding mechanisms to better align incentives across the ecosystem.

In a fireside conversation moderated by Kirsten "Dr. Kiki" Sanford, Professor Dashun Wang and I shared bottom-up and top-down perspectives on the past and future of science funding in America, and how we can turn learnings and ideas into actions.

Reflecting on two days of discussions at the Summit, a few ideas stood out:

1. Diversification and alignment are essential.
Our current funding system can’t keep pace with the rate of innovation. We need new mechanisms and new partnerships to make systemic change possible.

2. Public–private collaboration requires new structures and new trust.
It’s time to move beyond “toss-over-the-fence” partnerships toward true co-development models that marry public missions with private agility.

3. We need new metrics of success.
The next generation of science should reward collaboration, risk-taking, and traction. Not just patents and papers.

4. Alternative financing is no longer fringe.
Donor-advised funds, recoverable grants, science angels, and crypto-enabled micropayments are now helping fund ideas too early or too unconventional for traditional systems.

5. Trust is the currency of transformation.
The future of American science depends on rebuilding public trust, especially in regions where innovation ecosystems remain undercapitalized.

6. Youth is an untapped resource.
Young people bring fresh ideas, impatience with inertia, and freedom from the fear of impossibility. We need real mechanisms to co-create the future of science with them.

Whether you’re a scientist, funder, or policymaker, we each play a role in shaping the future of how science gets funded.

From your perspective, what changes would make the biggest difference?

There’s a new model emerging in philanthropy for funding bold science—one that in some ways resembles early-stage venture capital—that I'm excited about.

It's called thesis-driven philanthropic funds.

Unlike traditional foundations or fragmented grant making, these funds:

- Are led by domain experts, not generalists
- Pursue bold, time-bound missions, not open-ended causes
- Pool capital from philanthropists who act as the philanthropic equivalent to limited partners 

Why does this matter?
Because like great startups, great science and technology projects that have the most potential for outsized returns often die in the valley between “obvious to experts” and “obvious to institutions". Thesis-driven philanthropic funds operate in that valley where they back high-leverage work before it's consensus. They move fast and enable a different kind of capital deployment: more targeted than traditional philanthropy, more flexible than government grants, and higher risk tolerance than venture capital.

Investing in the bioindustrial revolution 

3w • Edited • 


Biology is starting to look a lot like software did 20 years ago. Not because biology is becoming simple, but because the cost curves underneath it are bending fast enough to make entirely new categories possible.

That's the real pattern behind most breakout companies: they didn't win by being a little better or a little cheaper. They won by defining a new unit of work that only became viable once the technology caught up.

Salesforce did it for enterprise software.
Stripe did it for online payments.
Uber did it for ride-sharing.

Biology is now entering that same phase. Sequencing is cheaper. DNA synthesis and editing are cheaper. Lab infrastructure can be rented instead of built. Robots run experiments around the clock. AI is moving more of the work in silico before anything reaches the bench.

That combination changes what small teams can do. Ideas that needed $50M and 5 years a decade ago can now be explored by a few founders with a fraction of the capital.

When that happens, the best companies aren't the ones that copy a category. They're the ones that create one.

Disruptive technologies tend to improve faster than users' needs grow, so to outsiders the most interesting biology companies often look like "toys" at first:

▫️ Proteins for precision mining → Alta Resource Technologies
▫️ DNA preservation → Cache DNA
▫️ De-extinction → Colossal Biosciences
▫️ Digitalizing the sense of smell → General Sense
▫️ Self-driving labs → Trilobio

Easy to dismiss. Until they can't be ignored.

The next category kings in biotech will come from teams building against these new cost curves, not around old market definitions. That's where we believe the venture-scale opportunity is.

Who's building a category creator that few people understand today?
If that's you, I'd like to hear from you!

Accelerating the bio-industrial revolution

3mo • 

Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA) is building computers from brain organoids, and the response deadline is Tuesday.

I spoke with Michael Koeris about this yesterday.  The program is called O-CIRCUIT. The goal: construct biological processing units that can perform AI training and inference at the edge with power consumption measured in milliwatt-hours per day. Not kilowatts. Milliwatt-hours.

The benchmark they're chasing comes from nature: A fruit fly brain: 140,000 neurons, 6 mWh/day.

Current edge AI chips us orders of magnitude more power for comparable simple tasks. The gap between biological and silicon efficiency isn’t small, it's enormous. 

The technical approach combines neural, glial, and immune cells into structured organoids that can learn, adapt, and process information. Phase 1 builds a biological processing unit that plays dynamic games. Phase 2 integrates a biological olfactory sensor into a drone navigation system. 
Biological compute powering autonomous systems in the field.

This matters beyond defense. If you can build a computing substrate from biology that runs on microwatts, you've opened an entirely new design space for embedded intelligence. Sensors that think. Materials that respond. Infrastructure that adapts. 

Most of the conversation about biology as a platform technology focuses on manufacturing: making molecules, materials, chemicals. That framing is correct but maybe not big enough. Biology doesn't just make things. It processes information. It learns. 

DARPA is now funding the bridge between those two capabilities.The bioindustrial economy and the AI economy are converging faster than most people realize. The companies and researchers working at that intersection are building something we don't have a name for yet.

If you’re building in this space I want to talk to you and Juniper wants to fund you. DMs open!

3mo • Edited • 


Most people talk about methane like it’s purely a climate liability. I think it’s one of the most underpriced feedstocks in industrial tech, and an exciting venture opportunity.

For the last decade, the dominant framing has been abatement: detect it, capture it, flare it, regulate it away. Important work, but only part of the equation.

Every year, ~570M tonnes of methane are released from landfills, livestock, oil and gas infrastructure, and wastewater. A lot of it escapes because the sources are too distributed, too contaminated, or too small for conventional infrastructure to process.

The venture opportunity is non-obvious but straightforward:

1) Feedstock flips from liability to advantage  
Methane is cheap, often free. In some cases, you’re effectively paid to take it because the alternative is penalties, compliance costs, or reputational risk.

2) Demand already exists at enormous scale  
You’re selling into huge markets with recession-proof demand such as animal feed ($180B+), fertilizers ($190B+), plastics ($400B+), and a long tail of commodity and specialty chemicals.

3) Tailwinds are stacking, not fading  
Policy and regulation are converging: the Global Methane Pledge, EPA rules, and bipartisan U.S. momentum behind domestic biomanufacturing.

Breakthroughs at the intersection of biology x engineering x AI are riding the tailwinds and economic benefits of methane as a feedstock to create massive value. 

Right now, I’m most excited about:

➡️ Methane to high-margin product creation 
➡️ Bioreactor and bioprocess design purpose-built for gas fermentation and messy, variable methane streams
➡️ Cell-free biomanufacturing pathways that use methane or methane-derived intermediates as inputs

Accelerating the bio-industrial revolution

3mo • Edited • 

Making DNA is still one of the slowest, most expensive steps in all of biology and DARPA just launched a program to bypass chemical synthesis entirely.

The Generative Optogenetics (GO) program aims to build molecular machines that synthesize DNA and RNA inside living cells, directed by light.

What that means in practice:
- Current DNA synthesis: chemical, bench-top, days to weeks per order, error-prone above 10,000 base pairs. 
- GO's approach: encode genetic information in optical signals, transduce it into nucleotide sequences in vivo — no reagents, no clean rooms. 

Biosecurity and regulatory working groups already convened. DARPA is thinking about deployment, not just discovery
DNA synthesis is the picks-and-shovels layer of the entire synthetic biology economy. Every engineered microbe, every fermentation-derived product, every bio-based material starts with someone ordering custom DNA. The bottleneck is real and this program is going after it directly.

If you're working on programmable biology, molecular machines, or biosystems engineering this is worth your attention AND we (the Juniper team) would love to meet you. The program is in early stages, working groups are still being convened, and DARPA is actively looking for performers.

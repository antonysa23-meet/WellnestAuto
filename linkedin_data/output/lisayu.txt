AI is a 5-layer cake powered by:
1. Energy
2. Chips
3. Infrastructure
4. Models
5. Applications

First, you need energy to power the machines that run AI. Then you need the chips to process the data and workloads. Infrastructure is the land, power, cooling, construction, networking, and systems that orchestrate thousands of processors. These are AI factories. AI models understand all kinds of information. LLMs (Large Language Models) took the world by storm. Now world models and models built for robotics & autonomous systems are being built out. Finally applications are at the top of this cake (i.e. industrial robots, drug discovery platforms, AI agents, co-pilots. self-driving cars, autonomous robots are AI applications).

At The Tech Capital InfraAI Global Summit in Athens, Greece. I had the opportunity to chair the panel: "How Are GPU Designs Evolving and Can Data Centers Keep Up?" We discussed the rapid evolution of GPU architectures and the pressure it puts on conventional data center design as well as how it impacts the other layers of the AI cake. 

Thank you to our panelists Wolfgang Neubert, Tomasz Buk, and Dimitris Elliniadis for being part of the discussion. The whole video can be found here: https://lnkd.in/gqFSWyPT

Thank you David Zhou for having me on your podcast Superclusters to share from GP’s point of view!

On this episode, we discussed how it’s all about the density of the NPS you have amongst the best talent and founders!

As an emerging manager, I’ve always made it a priority to go out of my way to help founders, even though I should probably be spending more time talking to LPs…I can’t help it since I’m a former founder myself!
 
The highest level of signal is from founder references (even more so if they didn’t take money from you and you’re still stop of mind). Ask any founder about 99VC! 

Also, as a fund manager, are you building something the market needs or wants?

2 years ago, I started building out the Physical AI thesis long before it was coined “Physical AI”. I wanted to invest in the hard things…the hardware and vertical software with deep industry expertise.

While funds were investing in early AI bets and GPT wrappers, I held fast and paused on investing in solutions that can easily be replicated with coding agents.

I quietly invested personal capital in long term bets and built out a team that shared my vision…that the next era of AI is the intersection of artificial intelligence and the physical world.

Arriving at a clearly differentiated strategy took years of patience and perseverance but this year truly affirmed Physical AI as the next era of tech growth, that focusing on founders first was not done in vain, and that sticking with conviction was the right move.

Thank you for those who supported me from the beginning…you know who you are!… more

“It’s all about the density of the NPS you have amongst the best talent. Of course, if they have a good experience with you, they’re more likely to refer others they think highly of to you. And that’s the reason why it becomes a leading indicator and therefore, a self-fulfilling prophecy as to who rises to the top.”

Tis the final episode in this season of Superclusters and I can't think of a better person to end it with than my good friend Charlotte Zhang at Inatai Foundation!

We talk Charlotte's pet peeves, how not all first rounds are created equal now, what happens when you have 30% DPI in the first 3 years, and so on. And I couldn't have done it without my fellows questioners, Lisa Yu, Mahesh Ramakrishnan, and Helena Gagern, who are all building fascinating investment theses.

Some of my fav moments:

🚨 The highest level of signal you can get from a founder reference is if they talk up an investor they like but never took money from. There's a natural incentive to talk about the investors who backed you, but for someone who isn't one to make that shortlist means somehow that GP is still staying top of mind.

⚖️ “Investment strategies are simply financial products serving the market of what founders and management teams in businesses need.” As a VC, are you building something the market needs? Wants? Or none of the above?

“Because incumbent brands create access flywheels, the most important thing for an emerging manager is having a clearly differentiated strategy."

🪙 If you have an "exceptionalism" or opportunistic bucket, one of the best things you can do is to communicate what kinds of companies might fit in that bucket to your LPs early on, so they know what to expect. For instance, we're still looking at writing first checks, but if it's a frontier lab at $400M post, this'll be in the separate bucket.

All that and more.

The second day in Athens dove deep into the forces reshaping AI infrastructure beyond just compute. From fibre to GPUs, the discussions highlighted how performance, latency, and design are driving a new era of operational strategy.

The Hidden Constraint: Fibre, Subsea, and the Latency Economics of AI
In an invite-only roundtable, industry leaders explored how connectivity, both fibre and subsea networks is becoming a defining factor for latency, resilience, and the geographic distribution of AI workloads.

Inside the AI Factory: DSX and the Future of Scalable Intelligence
Vladimir Prodanovic, ATD, CDCAP, CDCDP, CDCEP, CDCMP, CDCSP (NVIDIA) delivered a keynote on AI factory architectures, examining power density, cooling, orchestration, and utilisation. 

How Are GPU Designs Evolving and Can Data Centres Keep Up?
Chaired by Lisa Yu, this panel addressed the rapid evolution of GPU architectures and the pressure it puts on conventional data centre design.
Panelists included Tomasz Buk, Dr. Wolfgang Neubert and Dimitris Elliniadis.

The discussion revealed a fundamental tension: infrastructure cycles are slower than hardware innovation, forcing creative approaches to cooling, power density, and refresh strategies if data centres are to keep pace with the next generation of AI hardware.

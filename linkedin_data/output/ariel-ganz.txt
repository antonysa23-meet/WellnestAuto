About
As the Buddha said, Life is suffering, we get old, we get sick, and we die. However there is a cause to suffering, especially physical suffering, and there is a way out.While medicine has made leaps and bounds in the past century, we have not yet solved major problems areas in medicine. Many human beings are still suffering with diseases that will one day be detected early and precisely treated. We still treat people as averages rather than as individuals.As a Founder and General Partner at Arben Ventures, I invest in the future of health, investing in the future I hope to see, until it exists, in partnership with Ben Rolnik.We invest across precision medicine, early detection, cancer, brain health, women's health, mental health, and robotics and lab automation, investing at the intersection of science, medicine, and AI.My PhD in Molecular Nutrition at Cornell with Dr. Marie Caudill and Dr. Patrick Stover focused on Precision Nutrition, Chemistry, and Biochemistry, particularly looking at how genetic variation impacts individual nutrient needs. At Stanford Genetics, i worked as a postdoctoral fellow with Dr. Michael Snyder, applying multi-omics to understand the intersection between mental health and physical health, particularly in the context of depression, perceived safety, and lipid and energy metabolism. This broader shift in our understanding of the mind as a fundamental part of physical health comes at a critical time, as the World Health Organization predicts that mental health will be the leading cause of disease by 2030.As a human being experiencing illness, it became clear to me that medicine today is focused on sickness, not on optimizing health. Many small pains that patients experience are overlooked, even though they are real, because we don't yet have the data to understand them. I'm excited about personalized and resilience-driven approaches to medicine that treat individuals, optimize health, and detect small deviations early to improve health for human being on the planet.  Abganz@stanford.edu

Some exciting news: Elemind’s closed-loop neuromodulation technology now works in an earbud.

We've demonstrated Elemind's closed-loop stimulation through an in-ear form factor, matching our headband's stimulation phase accuracy. 

This is important because sleep was only the first use case for Elemind, and while a headband form factor is perfectly fine for sleeping, it's not something people would want to wear during the day. 

For daytime brain states (attention, stress, focus, cognitive performance), our technology needs to live in something people already wear all day.
Earbuds are that form factor.

This is not yet for sale. It’s a working prototype.

But we’re very excited to bring this to select partners in the audio and consumer electronics space.

1w • 

She was 27. An engineer at Airbus, designing satellites.

In the five months before she died, she contacted her GP practice more than 20 times. She saw six different doctors. Her cancer was never caught in time.

Meet Jessica Brady. Her family's courage is changing the NHS.

Jess fell ill in the summer of 2020. Unexplained weight loss. Night sweats. Chronic fatigue. A persistent cough. Enlarged lymph nodes. She kept showing up. She kept trying.

Her instincts were right. Her persistence was right. The system let her down.

In November 2020, she was finally diagnosed with stage 4 cancer. She died three weeks later.

Her mother Andrea, put it simply: "It was hard for Jess to advocate for herself." That's exactly what her family set out to fix.

Because of their campaigning, the NHS has introduced Jess's Rule.

If a patient presents three times with the same or worsening symptoms, GPs are now urged to reflect, review and rethink. To see the patient face-to-face. To question assumptions about age and demographics. To refer onward.

It's a real, systemic change, won by a family who turned grief into action.

And it matters most for the people the system overlooks. Research suggests that younger patients and people from ethnic minority backgrounds often wait longer for a serious diagnosis. Jess's Rule exists to push back against exactly that.

So here's what to know and share. If you've seen a GP three times for the same or worsening symptoms, you can name Jess's Rule and ask for a face-to-face review and a genuine reconsideration of your case.

"I've been here three times" is no longer just a statement of exhaustion. It's a prompt for action.

Jess kept showing up. Her family carried it forward. Now the rule exists. Use it.… more

"You're 22. Why would anyone trust you to build and commercialize a medical device?"

I've been asked this, in different words, by investors, clinicians, and doctors twice or three times my age.

Here is the honest answer.

Before Morph Labs, I spent my late teens obtaining prosthetic limbs for people who could not afford them. More than 150 of them. Some waited decades for a prosthetic, and some didn't even know the concept of prosthetics existed.

I may not have been in a lab reading about the problem, but I was in the room when someone tried a limb for the first time. And I was in the room months later when they quietly admitted they had stopped using it.

That second room is the one almost nobody outside of the industry ever sees.

I used to think this field moved slowly out of caution. I didn't wait for a PhD or for permission, because the people across from me did not have time for me to collect credentials. Their problem was already here. My urgency had to match theirs.

Age only tells you how long someone has been near a field. It says nothing about how close they have been to the person that field exists to serve.

I started young because I was already close.
I'm still here for the same reason.

Definitely optimistic.… more

About
I'm a technology investor and product visionary. As a technology investor, I co-founded Mighty Capital, the VC firm that leverages the Product Alpha Effect, a data-backed framework for outperformance. Honored on the Kauffman Top 30 Index and Power100, invested in pioneering companies like Amplitude (NASDAQ: AMPL), Netskope (NASDAQ:NTSK) and Groq. 

As a product visionary, my contributions earned me the reputation of “genius at making products people love” during the cloud/mobile era, when I built products that billions of people use at Meta and Siebel Systems, won industry awards and nominations from the Wall Street Journal and the Emmys, and wrote an award-winning bestseller on what makes a great product. 

My home is in San Francisco. I love my husband and two cats -and bright colors, especially green. I live by three core values:
- Respect - Honoring diverse perspectives and contributions.
- Grit - Viewing challenges as opportunities for growth.
- Adaptability - Embracing change as a catalyst for progress. 

Raised in an entrepreneurial family in Paris, I earned a master’s in electrical engineering from Centrale-Supelec before moving to Silicon Valley during the dot-com era. With an MBA from Stanford, I spent the first decade of my career as an entrepreneur and product manager. My award-winning bestseller on what makes a great product led to the founding of Products Count, a nonprofit that helps everyone build great products and inspires 1 in 3 product managers globally. I have spoken about the product innovation and investing on platforms like Harvard Business Review, CNBC’s Closing Bell and NPR’s Tech Nation. My journey into venture capital began through the doors opened by this product community, leading me to invest in pioneering companies like Groq, Angle and Canela. I frequently speak about investing trends on global stages like TechCrunch, FII, Collision, TheNextWeb and Fortune's Most Powerful Women Summit.

For my formal bio and headshot, visit mighty.capital/for-the-media.

My prediction after interviewing 1,500+ CPOs: the product manager role will be obsolete by 2030. The 5th annual CPO Insights Report we released today is our most consequential yet.

Here’s what’s happening: building software used to be expensive, slow, and resource-intensive. It’s not anymore. A team of 12 Product Builders (part PM, part engineer, part designer) can now ship 12 products. A year ago, that same team shipped one.

The ripple effects are everywhere. Product Builder headcount is up 10x. Traditional PM roles are down 30% across industries and 70% in SaaS. Speed to market has jumped to the #1 CPO challenge in a single year. And 1 in 3 CPOs now owns the AI M&A budget.

What excites me most is what’s emerging at the frontier: a new archetype we’re calling the Chief Product Investor. These are CPOs who think like portfolio managers: allocating capital not just headcount, managing risks instead of roadmaps, and scaling what works before the financial metrics catch up.

It’s the 5th year I see our data-driven predictions materialize. I couldn’t be more energized about what comes next.

TY Renee Niemi Jayesh Patel Products That Count and Mighty Capital teams for your hard work and passion.

When we first backed Groq 5 years ago, the signals we were hearing from our 600,000+ product leaders pointed to a contrarian conclusion: inference would be the bottleneck, and the winners would be the teams who could ship real performance in production.

2026 is the year the Product Alpha Effect™ goes mainstream: product becomes the primary driver of value creation.

Every year, we synthesize tens of thousands of signals from the 600,000+ product leaders who engage with Products That Count and ask: what’s the state of the product conversation?

Here’s what shifts:
- Boards rely less on lagging sales metrics and more on product signals: activation, usage depth, workflow pull-through, agent performance.
- CPO scope expands into GTM + product marketing, data + analytics, measured on outcomes (revenue, productivity, profit), not output.
- Talent is the constraint: AI-fluent, domain-deep, full-stack PMs win.
- Security becomes product-by-design as trust becomes the moat.
- Product Builders rise: product + design + engineering blur to crush time-to-market.

TY to my brilliant colleagues Hoda Mehr Renee Niemi Michael Paris Jayesh Patel for your contribution to this article.

AI biases used to be a big topic of conversation a couple of years ago, and recently it hasn't been. So the question I have is, how do you handle AI biases today?

Many of us notice it in our interactions with LLMs. But what do you do about it?

There are two ways you can handle that bias. One is you can push back in the conversation you're having with your AI thought partner. You can say, I disagree, I think you're biased because of this, please reframe your perspective. I've experimented with that and found it works really well.

Another way is instead of using information from the web through your LLM, you can create a project that only pulls data from a dataset you own and control. So you can do it in conversation through simple pushback, or you can do it by design through projects.

There's been a lot of reactions to my post on AI exhaustion and AI fatigue. And what's interesting is how I actually think about what AI does to the product development process.

AI doesn't lead to more products. It leads to killing more bad ideas.
Because you can experiment and launch so much faster, you can test on a much greater scale. More experimentation at the top of the innovation funnel, but a lot less actual product at the bottom. 

And because AI allows for so much faster development and iteration, when you put a product out in market you can put a competitive, cannibalizing version out pretty fast too.

So the best product teams in the age of AI aren't adding more products. They're cannibalizing their own, constantly.… more

After publishing our fifth annual CPO Insights Report there have been some discussions that I want to clarify, because I think a couple of things got misunderstood.

What our survey of 1,500 Chief Product Officers actually said is that the role of product manager is expanding into product builder, incorporating not just product management but also design and engineering, and that the productivity of product builders is significantly greater than that of product managers, engineers, and designers working separately.

What the report did not say is that the role of product manager is going away.
The title is changing. The responsibilities are expanding. But everything a product manager does is actually more important in the AI era than before, not less. Product managers bring judgment, and they bring the ability to translate technology into business value. Those things don't go away with AI, they become more critical.

What is the impact of our fifth annual CPO Insights Report on entrepreneurship, founders, and early stage investing?

First, we used to think innovation in the AI platform shift was going to come from AI researchers. Three years ago that was true. The model, the optimization, the technology itself was the differentiation. That's no longer the case. LLMs have become commodities.

The next hero entrepreneur of this AI era is someone with product judgment. Someone who can build a product with real differentiation and defensibility despite an AI platform shift that levels the playing field. That is the product builder. So if you're an investor looking to pick winning founders, you want to be looking at product builders, not researchers.

Second, the product alpha effect is becoming a reality. If you want to generate outsized returns, product innovation is the key driver of investment performance. And being able to understand product signals, process them at scale and at speed, is going to be the only way to produce those returns.… more

About
Pocket Sun is the Co-founder and Managing Partner of SoGal Ventures, a pioneering early-stage venture capital firm with $55M+ AUM, backing world-class women and diverse founders beyond Silicon Valley.

Since 2015, Pocket and her co-founder Elizabeth Galbut have invested in 150+ startups, identified five unicorns at inception, and delivered top-quartile returns to over 200 diverse LPs. SoGal is known for spotting what the world needs before it becomes obvious—often backing founders who go on to create entirely new categories in AI, digital health, care, sustainability, and culture-defining tech.

At the heart of Pocket’s investment philosophy is a belief in compassionate futurists: visionary entrepreneurs who build with empathy, science, and precision to transform how we live, work, and stay healthy.

Pocket's work has earned recognition on the cover of Forbes Asia, features in Fortune, CNBC, BBC and Bloomberg, and accolades such as Entrepreneur Magazine’s 100 Most Powerful Women, VC Journal Rising Stars, and LinkedIn Top Voices. 

Pocket has been featured in the award-winning documentary Show Her The Money, appeared as a guest judge on Entrepreneur Media’s Elevator Pitch, hosted Start-Up Season 5 across 26 Asian markets, contributed to 5 business books, and engaged over one million listeners via her Chinese business podcast. Driven by a commitment to feminism, queer communities, and activism, Pocket Sun continues to shape the future of inclusive, high-impact investing.

I have complicated feelings about AI. Ingeborg Investments new report, "Building Enduring Careers in the Age of AI," put words to something I've felt but couldn't name: this shift isn't landing on all of us the same way.

Three lines that stuck with me:

→ "As skills are commoditized, network access becomes the new moat." And it's the moat with the steepest gender gap. When everyone can do the work, who you know quietly decides who gets to rise.

→ "Code × everything becomes the default in the AI era." This one made me smile. Women are far more likely to study CS when it's paired with another field — at Northeastern, our share of CS majors jumped from 18% to 39% the moment it became interdisciplinary. The future is being built in exactly the way more of us want to build.

→ "When AI does the work, people must speak for it — and women have historically been penalized for self-promotion." Here's the data that stings: women who self-promote in hiring are rated ~22% less likeable and less hireable than men with identical qualifications. So just as claiming your work becomes the whole game, we're penalized for playing it.

But women-owned businesses are growing at 2x the U.S. average. We hold 59% of AI governance roles. The infrastructure for this entire era is being built inside the functions we already lead.

I don't want us to spend the next decade retrofitting a system that was never designed with us in mind. I want us to build it right the first time.

So help me with the uncomfortable question: if the AI era rewards whoever speaks loudest about their work — and women get punished for doing exactly that — are we about to automate the gender gap into permanence, or finally code our way out of it?

Functional medicine has been talked about for years as the future of proactive healthcare — but for too long, it has been too expensive, too fragmented, and too inaccessible for most people.

That is what makes this milestone from Parsley Health so important.

Parsley is now the first functional medicine provider to accept insurance nationwide, bringing physician-led, root-cause care in-network for 150M Americans across major health plans. This is a huge step toward making preventative, personalized care part of the mainstream healthcare system — not just a luxury for those who can afford to pay out of pocket.

Chronic disease drives the majority of healthcare spending in the U.S., yet our system is still largely built around managing symptoms after people are already sick. Parsley’s model flips that: deeper diagnostics, longitudinal care, and a focus on understanding the underlying drivers of health.

SoGal Ventures has backed the team that is continuing to push the category forward. Huge congrats to Robin Berzin MD Berzin and the entire Parsley Health team on this milestone.

This is what healthcare should look like: more accessible, more proactive, and built around actually helping people get better.

The biggest lever for improving outcomes in foster care might be earlier mental health care.

In this episode of the Expanding Access, Michelle Turner, founder & CEO of Here Now Health, talks about the systemic gaps in mental healthcare for children in the foster care system.

The SoGal Ventures portfolio company founders' perspective comes from firsthand experience as a foster parent. What she saw was a system where instability often compounds trauma: frequent placement changes, high case manager turnover, and a heavy reliance on crisis intervention instead of prevention.

The result is a cycle where foster youth disproportionately drive Medicaid spending through emergency and acute care, rather than receiving consistent mental health support that could stabilize outcomes earlier.

Here Now Health is working to change that model through trauma-informed virtual therapy designed specifically for foster youth—care that follows the child regardless of placement, supports caregivers, and focuses on prevention instead of crisis response.

If you care about the intersection of mental health, policy, and child welfare, this conversation is worth a listen.

Compute is the layer of AI that almost no one talks about — until it becomes your biggest line item.

In Episode #142 of the NF Twins podcast, Carmen Li, Founder & CEO of Silicon Data and Compute Exchange, breaks down why GPUs are becoming the new oil — and why the compute market remains widely mispriced.

From hyperscalers vs. neo-clouds to NVIDIA’s real competition, token pricing, latency, GPU arbitrage, and the silent margin erosion many AI startups underestimate — this is the infrastructure conversation founders need to be having. Carmen also shares her journey from Bloomberg to running two companies simultaneously, her no-nonsense leadership style, and why transparency will define the next phase of AI infrastructure.

SoGal Ventures had the opportunity to back Silicon Data and see firsthand how disciplined thinking around compute can become a real strategic advantage.
If you’re building in AI, scaling aggressively, or trying not to let compute costs quietly erode margins — this one is worth a listen.

About
Michelle Detwiler is a Co-Founder and Managing Partner at Biospring Partners, a dedicated healthcare growth equity firm investing in pharma services- mainly outsourced complex manufacturing businesses and clinical trial services.

Prior to Biospring, Michelle was a Managing Director at General Atlantic where she launched their first life sciences investment platform. Prior to General Atlantic, Michelle was Co-Founder and General Partner of Longwood Fund, a leading healthcare investment firm based in Boston.  

Prior to Longwood, Michelle was the Senior Vice President of the CEEDD at GlaxoSmithKline’s (NASDAQ:GSK) leading global business development and M&A. 

Michelle holds a Bachelor of Medicine and Bachelor of Surgery from the University of Oxford Medical School and a Doctor of Philosophy in Human Physiology from the University of Oxford.

Michelle serves on the following:
- Board of Trustees, Xavier University of Louisiana 
- Trustee Advisory Board, Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center
- Board of Directors, Women’s Foundation of Boston, focused on serving women and girls in need 
- Co-Founding Member of the Bowman Fund, Brasenose College Boat Club, Oxford University

"The key question is not 'Do you have capacity?' It's how will this capability reduce risk, shorten decision cycles, and improve the probability of technical and regulatory success for my molecule?"

ADCs & AOCs are quickly expanding beyond oncology into rare disease and neuromuscular disorders. But that growth is colliding with a familiar problem: molecules that look great at bench scale don't always hold up under manufacturing, analytical, and regulatory scrutiny.

In BioProcess International, our VP of Regulatory Strategy, Jeffrey C. Mocny, Ph.D., and VP of ADCs & Bioconjugates, Nicolas Camper, break down where the gaps can show up and how to close them early:
→ Precision conjugation strategies like glycan remodeling can be hard to control once they scale
 → AOCs add complexity from oligonucleotide conformational diversity that current analytics can't fully characterize
 → A clear QTPP gives teams a practical reference point before locking in a conjugation route
 → Control strategies should target known risk points, not problems discovered downstream

Their core message is that drug developers should build CMC into the molecule from day one. Start with a clear quality target product profile, stress-test which conjugation routes are actually manufacturable, and design the control strategy around known risk points rather than discovering them downstream.

Definitely worth a read for anyone steering a bioconjugate program toward the clinic: https://lnkd.in/gDp5M9x9

A common misconception in cell therapy is that manufacturing challenges can be solved later, once the biology is validated. In reality, late-stage manufacturability fixes are often the most expensive.

For iPSC-derived therapies, early decisions around cell line development, platform design, process strategy, traceability, and GMP readiness are not secondary technical details. They shape the development path from the start, with direct implications for scalability, timelines, and translation.

The conversation in our industry is rightly focused on innovation. But innovation without a credible path to reproducible manufacturing is not enough.

The real advantage lies not only in creating promising cell therapy programs, but in building them on foundations that can support translation, scale, and long-term viability.

Inhibition of sustained hypoxic vasoconstriction by Y‐27632 in isolated intrapulmonary arteries and perfused lung of the rat

British Journal of Pharmacology · Jan 29, 2009

Show publication
Abstract
We have examined the effects of Y‐27632, a specific inhibitor of Rho‐activated kinases (ROCK I and ROCK II) upon sustained hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction (HPV) in both rat isolated small intrapulmonary arteries (IPA) and perfused rat lungs in situ. Y‐27632 (100 nM–3 μM) was found to cause a concentration‐dependent inhibition of acute sustained HPV in rat IPA. Application of Y‐27632 (10–600 nM) in perfused rat lungs caused no change in basal perfusion pressure, but was found to inhibit HPV in a concentration‐dependent manner, resulting in complete ablation of the pressor response to hypoxia at a concentration of 600 nM. Furthermore, addition of Y‐27632 at any point during hypoxia caused a reversal of HPV in perfused rat lungs. These results suggest that activation of Rho‐associated kinase may be a pivotal step in the generation of sustained HPV.… more

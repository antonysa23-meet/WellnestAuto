From an investment perspective, fragrance remains one of beauty's most attractive sectors—strong growth, high margins, and powerful discovery dynamics. One theme I kept coming back to: fragrance as a platform, not a product. It's our view, that the most interesting companies aren't simply selling perfume. We also discussed why strategics acquire vs. build. Large consumer companies can formulate fragrance, manufacture it, and distribute it globally. What they're often buying is something much harder to create internally: community, brand affinity, and cultural relevance.

The Weekly Business Review "WBR" is one of the most underused habits in a business — not because people don’t know about it, but because it rarely gets run the way it should.

Part 3 of our AI Framework focuses on the WBR — and why it becomes a completely different tool once AI is doing all the underlying work.

A few things that matter:

→ It forces a single source of truth. Same numbers, same definitions, same view of the business. No side conversations based on different cuts of data.

→ It shortens the feedback loop. A miss in week one is fixable. A miss you only see at the end of the quarter is SUPER expensive. The WBR closes that gap.

→ It scales. Doesn’t matter if you’re at $1M or $100M — the discipline holds. If anything, it becomes more important as the business gets more complex.

→ AI changes the effort curve. Data gets pulled automatically. Variances get flagged. The meeting shifts from “what happened?” to “what do we do about it?”

If it’s working, the output is simple: clear actions, clean reporting, and something you could hand to a board without reworking it.

Most teams spend too much time reviewing what already happened. The WBR is how you stay current enough to actually do something about it.

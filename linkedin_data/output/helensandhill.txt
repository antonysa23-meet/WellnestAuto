2d • 

The most important computer of the next century will not be built by whoever demos the most qubits this year. It will be built by whoever figures out how to make a million of them on infrastructure the world already knows how to manufacture. That is the problem Sebastian Weidt has organized his career around, and it is the bet behind Universal Quantum, the company he co-founded with Sussex professor Winfried Hensinger after the two published, in 2017, the first practical blueprint for a large-scale trapped-ion quantum computer.

The trapped ion is widely regarded as the highest-quality qubit there is. The open question has always been how can you scale from a handful of qubits to a million.

UQ's answer is built on two unusual bets:
1. Modularity: Rather than chasing one ever-larger chip, they build standard silicon modules and connect them with ultrafast electric-field links, sidestepping the wiring problem that chokes monolithic designs. 
2. Control: Most trapped-ion machines steer their qubits with finely aligned lasers, the most mature and highest-performing approach today. UQ instead uses microwaves and magnetic field gradients. Microwave control has historically traded some gate speed for a system that does not get harder to wire as you add qubits, and on fidelity it has now largely caught up with lasers. Crucially, the chips are made with standard silicon fabrication (instead of spending billions to create a custom fab line).

1w • 

A single chart from SpaceX's S-1 explains why the space economy is at an inflection point.

For 50 years, the cost to put a kilogram into orbit barely moved. Whether you launched in 1965 or 2005, you were paying somewhere in the tens of thousands of dollars per kg. The Space Shuttle (the supposed reusability breakthrough) landed around $64,000/kg. The line was flat for half a century.

Then it broke.

The first Falcon 9 in 2010 brought launch cost to roughly $2,700/kg — about 85% below the historical average of $18,500. Falcon Heavy pushed it to ~$1,400/kg, a ~92% reduction. 

Here's the part most people miss about why.

A rocket has two stages. Stage 1 is the giant booster that does the heavy lifting off the pad. Stage 2 carries the payload the rest of the way to orbit. Historically, both were thrown away after a single flight — the equivalent of scrapping a 747 after one transatlantic trip.

Falcon 9 solved half of that. The booster now flies, lands, and flies again — SpaceX flew 165 Falcon missions in 2025, the vast majority on already-flown boosters. That alone is a huge cost reduction.

But the booster is only part of the cost. The real prize is making both stages reusable — recovering the entire rocket, refurbishing it fast, and flying it again like an airplane. That's the bet behind Starship: full reusability, rapid turnaround, multiple flights from the same hardware.

The logic is simple. When you stop discarding your most expensive asset on every trip, the dominant cost stops being the rocket and starts being the fuel. 

That cost reduction is the launch-pad to reaching space infrastructure such as data centers in space.… more

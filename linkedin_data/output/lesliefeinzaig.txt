About
Leslie Feinzaig is the Founder and General Partner at Graham & Walker, an early stage venture fund backing pre-consensus founders and ideas that break the mold, whose mission is to change the face of public markets by backing a new guard of founders and CEOs. Prior to launching the fund, Ms. Feinzaig was a 2x startup exec with 8- and 10- figure exits who started her career under the tutelage of HBS Professor Clayton Christensen, author of the seminal theory of disruptive innovation. 

In 2017 Ms. Feinzaig founded the Female Founders Alliance, the largest community of its kind in America, which serves as Graham & Walker's proprietary deal flow machine. In 2024, she launched VCs for Harris, the bipartisan viral pledge that united 850 VCs, including a who's who of Silicon Valley according to NPR. 

Ms. Feinzaig is a regular contributor at Fortune Magazine and Fast Company, and publishes the popular Substack, “Venture with Leslie”. 

Born and raised in Costa Rica, Ms. Feinzaig moved to America with a full scholarship to Harvard Business School. She has been since named one of Forbes Magazine's Most Powerful Women from Central America, Worth Magazine’s Worthy 100, Puget Sound Business Journal 40 Under 40 and Women of Influence Awards, and Seattle Magazine's Most Influential People. She lives in Seattle with her husband and two young daughters.

Andreessen Horowitz is not a venture capital fund.

And it’s not just Andreessen. It’s all the big funds.

They started out as VC. They operate funds that invest in private early stage companies. But they haven’t been VC funds for a very long time.

That’s not a knock on their success or influence - both of which are massive.

But these aren’t VCs anymore. They’re what I call Consensus Capital: big finance with a Sand Hill Road address.

To continue calling them venture capital is both disingenuous and damaging to how we understand our industry.

I vote we stop.

Why do I care so much about this? 

For LPs: Because when LPs write checks into these funds, they should know what they’re actually buying. True venture capital is about messy, early, conviction-driven bets on non-consensus founders. Consensus Capital is about indexing into the obvious - paying up for access to a narrow band of “consensus” founders and making money off the beta. Those are two entirely different products.

For founders: Because the label shapes their expectations. Consensus Capital flows to a very particular pedigree of founders - those from a handful of schools, startups, or AI labs who are highly discoverable (sometimes literally by AI agents). If that’s them, capital will be abundant and easy. If it’s not, they need to know there are still alpha-seeking VCs out there, the ones who look for outstanding non-consensus builders like Marc and Ben did 25 years ago.

For the ecosystem: All the VC commentators (myself included) are tripping over ourselves about what this all means. My B-school classmate Rob Go wrote about VC’s existential crisis. Sapphire’s Elizabeth "Beezer" Clarkson says venture is broken. Carta data guru Peter Walker talks about the bifurcation of venture capital. Eric Newcomer describes it as a break between the “haves and the have nots”. Calling it all “venture capital” distorts the data. Mega-rounds and mega-funds skew every benchmark. Split them up, and the ecosystem gets better data, better benchmarks, and a truer picture of what’s really happening out there. 

Consensus Capital and Venture Capital are two different investing strategies (that can very much co-exist!). If we treat them like it, everyone would be better off. More in my substack in the comments.

It’s hard to remember now, but there was a time when the term “girlboss” was aspirational. We all know what happened next - it went from aspirational to toxic within a decade. The women themselves went from feminist trailblazers to feminist traitors - all because their management style did not fit the girlboss brand.

Today, we call that same management style "Founder Mode." You may not like it, but it's extremely popular and encouraged. 

2 years ago I wrote a viral article about the women who lost their jobs for doing their jobs. Today, I wrote about their comebacks, what that means for the rest of us, and why it's so important to allow these fallen leaders to get a second chance.

Because you can't launch and scale a company without the freedom to make mistakes and be imperfect.

And because it's time we stopped branding female leaders, and trapping them in the confines of perfection.

Until then, we’ll keep losing brilliant entrepreneurs to the impossible task of being perfect symbols instead of imperfect, adaptable leaders.

I applied to an emerging manager program last week.

I found out about it on Monday. I thought the deadline was Friday. Turns out, it was Tuesday. So I wrote the application on a cross-country flight, wedged in a middle seat between my two daughters. 

On hour 4 of the 6 hour flight, I realized they asked for a pitch video. And so I recorded it, right then and there. 

I've watched the video a few times since, half proud, half horrified. I think you can tell this is after a week on the road and five days of "vacation kids" with no childcare. I didn't intend to show my kids in the video, but of course they kept interrupting and ended up making a cameo (the little one was thirsty. Again.)

It’s very, very far from perfect. But it's real. It's how I - and millions of moms - make it work in this season of life. 

In the video, I talk about what we’re building at Graham & Walker: a fund that backs the founders Silicon Valley too often overlooks. I share the path that brought me here - from Costa Rica to Harvard to tech exec to founder to VC. And I talk about why this work matters to me not just professionally, but personally.

They’ll either love it or hate it. But it’s done. And I’m proud of it. 

(Plus, it’s true what they say - done is better than perfect).

Today, I'm proud to unveil the #vcsforkamala pledge.

As of this morning, 110 VCs representing ~$150 Billion in assets under management have signed.

Over the past few weeks, like so many of you, I’ve watched countless venture capital power players announce their support of Donald Trump. Many of them are VCs I respect and admire. And I’m not so much surprised about seeing VCs express conservative viewpoints - some of which I share.

But this particular election - there are much more existential issues at stake.

And so last Tuesday I went online, bought a URL, reserved a social handle, and wrote a 3 paragraph pledge in support of Vice President Harris. On Thursday night, I sent it to a dozen or so friends who volunteered to help. By Friday morning, Mark Cuban and Reid Hoffman had signed. A dozen more joined in the hours that followed, big enough names that there was no turning back.

As of right now, 110 VCs representing about $150 Billion in assets under management have signed the pledge. I believe there are at least a hundred more, just from our outreach, that would sign the pledge but are prevented from doing so by their firms’ internal policies.

The names on this list are not just well known democrats - they are moderates and independents, too. Each person’s reasons for signing are their own, and they vary. What we share is a commitment to safeguarding American democracy, and a desire to engage with each other and with elected leaders on the best path forward on the issues that we care about.

In the last five days I have asked myself, over and over, why I am doing this. There are so many VCs out there with far more power, money, and fame than me. So many that have far more to gain and far less to lose by taking a stand.

Here’s my why.

I’m doing this because I have kids. I’m doing this because they are girls. I’m doing this because to have economic prosperity, we need institutional stability. I’m doing this for the thousands of founders who are watching our industry aghast. And I’m doing this because Silicon Valley power players that have lost touch with the rest of us, don’t speak for all of us.

I am in awe of what a small group of volunteers accomplished in the last 5 days. Thank you so much to all of you, those who need to remain anonymous, and to everyone who was brave enough (and able to) sign the pledge.

Check out the full list of signatories, and sign the pledge, on www.vcsforkamala.org

And join us.

#vcsforkamala #vcsfordemocracy

The first time Costa Rica qualified for the World Cup was Italy 1990. It was a HUGE deal. That was the year I first saw grown men cry and learned to curse in Italian. On match days, the streets emptied out. Schools closed. Workplaces didn't close, but nobody showed up anyway. And nobody got in trouble. When Costa Rica played, all eyes were glued to the TV. When we scored, you could feel the ground shake in our neighborhood. You could hear the neighbors scream. 

My eyes well up even writing this. It's one of the best, most formative memories of my life. 

Growing up in a tiny country that barely registered on any world stage made every moment like this feel other-wordly. It was my first lesson in the power of being an underdog and the unifying power of mission. A humble group of men who days before were running drills in a run down public soccer pitch shined on the world stage and carried the hopes and dreams of an entire nation. 

Most of you reading this are sitting somewhere in North America, maybe you'll watch a match or two, maybe you're complaining about traffic in your host cities around match day, maybe you're reading about FIFA's unabashed money-grab. Meanwhile, for millions of people around the world, the next 6 weeks are the stuff dreams are made of. 

Costa Rica didn't qualify this year, but I'm celebrating anyway, and thinking of the little boys and girls around the world who are about to get their first lesson in possibility.

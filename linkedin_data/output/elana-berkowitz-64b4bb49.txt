At our post-AGM dinner a few weeks ago, we asked a roomful of founders, LPs, and favorite operators for one book they’d recommend for summer. There was technology, of course, but also art, cities, Beatles, and lots of reflection on how to build a meaningful life. 

Just a sampling of the recommendations for your beach tote this holiday weekend:

ON TIME AND WHAT IT'S FOR: Dealer’s Choice so I'll start with my own: Abraham Joshua Heschel's The Sabbath, which reframes time itself as a "palace" — a thing whose meaning comes from its very finite supply. Caroline Hyde brought From Strength to Strength by Arthur C. Brooks, on the quiet art of the second half of life. And Kristen Naiman recommended David Whyte's Consolations.

ON THE PEOPLE WE'RE TETHERED TO: Nirban Singh, MPH brought Valarie Kaur's See No Stranger, and John Buttrick offered John & Paul: A Love Story in Songs by Ian Leslie, casting Lennon and McCartney as one of the great creative marriages, with all the devotion and friction that implies. Noorain Khan recommended Lena Dunham's Famesick, on celebrity, illness and creative collaboration. 

ON THE MACHINES: Brian Nguyen makes the case for Ted Chiang's Exhalation, still some of the most humane writing about technology I know. Kate Twist recommended Ray Kurzweil's The Singularity Is Nearer. And for the operatic version, Paige Jernigan brought The Optimist, Keach Hagey's account of Sam Altman and OpenAI.

ON BUILDING CITIES, POWER AND COMPANIES: My other pick was The Gods of New York, Jonathan Mahler's portrait of the city between 1986 and 1990: egotists and idealists building the greatest city in the world. Courtney Leimkuhler countered with Kingmaker, Sonia Purnell's astonishing (and pretty spicy) life of Pamela Harriman. And the one book that came up twice was Dirtbag Billionaire (thank you Ross Dakin and John Buttrick) on how Yvon Chouinard built Patagonia and then gave it away.

ON ART AND BEING HUMAN: We slipped this into our thank-you note after the AGM cocktails, so consider it the house pick: Your Brain on Art covers the new science of neuroaesthetics — how making something for as little as forty-five minutes measurably lowers cortisol, and how a single art experience a month can lengthen your life.

Yesterday we co-hosted a breakfast with our friends Reach Capital and Ashley Mayer from Coalition with Robert Armstrong (FT markets editor, men’s fashion columnist, and originator of the “TACO Trade" term, which he fears may end up being the first line of his obituary). 

A few things that stuck with me:

1. Markets aren’t telling a single, coherent story

Oil markets suggest a potential Iran conflict would “blow over.”
Options markets are getting more nervous—investors are buying insurance.
And bond markets are behaving strangely.

In a geopolitical crisis, sovereign bonds usually rise as a flight to safety. Instead, they’re being sold off which raises a deeper question:

If investors don’t trust governments as the ultimate backstop…what replaces it?

2. AI: the story may not hang together (yet)

Rob was skeptical that meaningful productivity gains from AI are actually showing up in the data today. The data is noisy and slow to reflect structural change.

So when we say “AI is driving productivity gains,” what are we actually seeing?

Some of it may simply be post-COVID labor reshuffling—people moving into better-fitting roles.

More importantly, he pushed on whether the broader narrative is economically coherent.

We often hear that AI will drive massive productivity gains and sustained unemployment. Historically, those two dynamics don’t coexist without a clear mechanism breaking the link between them. Right now, that mechanism is often…hand-waved.

3. The economy looks strong—until you look closer

The U.S. remains exceptionally good at absorbing shocks.

But ~20% of Americans are economically on the edge.

Lower-wage workers saw the biggest gains during COVID. That has reversed.

What’s emerging instead is “delinquency churn”:
people making just enough payments to avoid default, but not enough to stay current—carefully timing 30/60/90 day payments to manage cash flow.

Subprime portfolios haven’t cracked yet—not because people are stable, but because they’re juggling.

4. A couple of lighter (but telling) notes

“Straight men are dressing better for business.”

And Rob uses prediction markets like Kalshi every day—but wonders whether broader adoption will make them more accurate…or less.

Feels like a metaphor for this moment:
more data, more participation—unclear if that actually leads to more signal.

If you want to join us for a future breakfast, ping me or Ashley Mayer

Last week I spent an afternoon nerding out with leaders of the NYC’s Department of Transportation and the MTA, including CEO Janno Lieber via Tech:NYC's Grid Fellowship. We were mostly inside Grand Central, which is 113 years old, 13 stories deep, the most complex node in a system moving ~6.5 million people every day.

What stuck with me wasn’t a single “innovation,” but the scale and constraint of the system itself. New York street space may be the scarcest resource in the world. Our streets absorb buses, bikes,  deliveries, construction, emergency vehicles—everything—so even small changes have real second- and third-order effects.

One theme that kept coming up: government has its own version of the valley of death. Congestion pricing and the 14th Street Busway both survived lawsuits, public backlash, and political risk before they launched and then public opinion flipped almost overnight once the benefits were felt. Startups die in the valley of death when they run out of capital or conviction before product–market fit. Public systems die when leaders reverse course before outcomes can be felt.

The current MTA leadership seemed deeply customer-centric—obsessed with UX, consumer incentives, and brand building. (Yes, fixing grime and “mystery dripping water” in stations was explicitly described as hurting the brand. And, yes, New Yorkers, that station was the East Broadway F stop.)

Innovation here is slow and unglamorous but consequential: AI for monitoring and planning, ops-driven pilots that actually escape purgatory, century-scale infrastructure bets, geothermal cooling experiments in subway stations that can’t be air-conditioned etc.

It was a reminder that some of the hardest innovation work isn’t greenfield—it’s shipping inside live, legacy systems, in public, where users never log off and won’t accept a half-working MVP.

Also: more than 2,500 retired NYC subway cars have been sunk into the Atlantic to form artificial reefs. Entirely unnecessary information. But delightful. As always, I love you forever New York City.

Everyone feels the world speeding up. The real skill now is staying grounded enough to build what lasts even as everything else moves. Anchor ends my trilogy by looking at what keeps us steady while the world accelerates around us.

Synthetic media doubles yearly. Tools store everything but remember nothing. Memory, Perceive, and Protect are becoming the human infrastructure we forgot to build — the layers that preserve context, meaning, and identity when speed outpaces understanding.

What began as a tech framework ended up helping me understand this season of my life. Not every chapter stays, but their imprint does. Some connections matter enough to leave space for whatever future form they take, even as you move forward. And sometimes the past returns simply to show what’s changed.

Earlier this week Aubrie Pagano and Isabelle Brothers and I gathered artists, LPs, GPs and founders for an evening of AI and creativity, reimagining what human imagination can look like in an algorithmic age.

To take it back to freshman year, I’ve been thinking a lot about what Walter Benjamin called the "aura" of art — that feeling of time, space, place and uniqueness that’s lost when technology makes everything infinitely reproducible. And, of course, there is so much AI slop. AI-generated web content has grown over 8,000 % in the past two years.  (And Martin Luther King Jr via Sora selling car insurance?!) The internet is getting faster and emptier at the same time.

And yet, many creative revolutions started with panic. The camera didn’t kill painting though it did transform it. Electronic amplification didn’t kill music.  (Though my dad would beg to differ.) 

Our speakers pushed us to imagine what widening the aperture of possibility could look like across fields as varied as death, fine fragrance development, country music, and artist compensation on the blockchain.

Charlie H. showed us how to spot the subtle “ghost sounds” that give away AI-generated music — tiny digital artifacts our ears can still detect even as the models get frighteningly good (learn more via his excellent pod Switched on Pop).

Sean Raspet from Patina brought blind smell tests of new scents he developed via his AI platform that maps the olfactory system and allows him to create net new fragrance molecules. Guests caught notes of weird florals, the bottom of the ocean, and, memorably, “my grandmother’s attic.”

The speakers reminded us that in a world where anyone can make anything, taste, authenticity and trust are the new scarcity. There was a lot of food for thought – and a lot of duck prosciutto as actual food. And a good amount of belief that the next generation of iconic companies won’t just make back office work faster, they’ll make human creativity more abundant, varied and vibrant. 

Grateful to friends who attended and shared their work. Dave Krugman, Stacy London (both fabulous presenters), John Gannon, Anna Mason from Ingeborg, Lisa Marrone from Pivotal Ventures, Adina Tecklu, Karin Klein, Laura Chau, Matthew Hartman, Caroline Cline, Nabiha Syed, Freya Zaheer, Align Impact, Fiduciary Trust International, Jordan Crook, Jenny Fielding, Todd Bailey, Luca Ranaldi, Aashay Sanghvi, Tia Breakley, Sarah Smith, Lisa Jacobs Blau, Carly Ayres and many more. It’s been a busy M&A month at Springbank and we’re thrilled to share that two of our favorite companies are joining forces. Last week, Little Otter announced its merger with Hazel Health creating the most comprehensive, tech-enabled pediatric mental health platform in the country.

Together, they now reach over 20 million children and families across 20 states and 6,200 schools!

What makes this so powerful? Each company has been tackling the youth mental health crisis from a different—and equally essential—perspective. Hazel built the leading infrastructure for school-based care. Little Otter built the gold standard clinical model and product set for for at-home triage, therapy and parental support. Now, they’re uniting to create something that’s long been missing: a continuous, coordinated ecosystem of mental health support for families across home, school, and community.

At Springbank, we invest in the infrastructure of modern life: how we work, live, and care. And few needs are more urgent than this one. Our systems weren’t built for “the anxious generation.” Families are overwhelmed. Schools are under-resourced. Clinicians are stretched thin. More than 70% of counties don't have a child psychiatrist. 

What’s needed isn’t more point solutions. It’s integration of evidence-based care that meets families where they are—and stays with them through the ups and downs.

We’ve been proud to back Rebecca Egger, Egger MD, and the Little Otter team from day one as they built the best-in-class platform for early childhood mental health. This next chapter, scaling in partnership with school districts, pediatricians, and families nationwide, is just the beginning.

Yesterday, as the school year kicked off, All Raise invited me and the fabulous Beth Ferreira (Serena Ventures) Allison Baum Gates (SemperVirens Venture Capital) Jennifer Kwan Mandelbaum (Halogen Ventures) and Rachel Mayes (Carta) to talk about parenting and venture. We were joined by 50 investors and operators chatting about designing smart systems around who and what matters to you at work and at home. 

A few of my quick takeaways: 
• Stack the nights. I only miss 2 bedtimes max during the week and I make them count. Sometimes I'll do 3 events or meetings per evening. Be intentional about which rooms you choose. 

• Run an energy audit. What non-work tasks have the largest delta between time it actually takes vs how draining/annoying you find it? Ruthlessly outsource the bandwidth/energy killers. (For me: laundry forever.)

• Treat leave like a product launch. Owners, comms cadences, SLAs, decision rights — clarity is a gift to your team. See link in comments for a guide Springbank and AllRaise wrote on this topic for founders.

• Outcomes > optics. I stopped apologizing for constraints and started tracking results. 

I don't believe in "mom guilt." It is simply wasted energy. It steals focus. Guilt is a tax on productivity (and joy) — and my taxes are already plenty high … 

If you’re a founder or fund rethinking how you support caregivers, let’s trade playbooks — we’ve built templates, guides, and the occasional bit of wisdom here at Springbank.… more

I am so sad to hear of the passing of Andrew Kassoy, a co-founder of the B corp movement. A successful PE investor and entrepreneur, he believed in business as a key to thriving markets and thriving communities. I had the privilege of working at Etsy when it was a B corp. And today, I'm a (perhaps over?) consumer of lots of other B corps like Warby Parker, Patagonia, Bombas, Nestlé Nespresso SA —proof that mission‑driven companies can thrive in competitive markets. 

B corps have their skeptics and their very real drawbacks to be sure but Andrew started an important conversation worth having — one that led directly to the creation of the Public Benefit Corporation legal structure. Anthropic is a PBC and Open AI is navigating the process of becoming one. 

See the lovely video below. As my friend Abby Falik ✨ put it, "Watching this brought me back to the heart of the heart: leadership is about love -- and the courage to align the precious hours and days and years of our lives with our convictions." 

Let's make those hours and days and years count. May Andrew's memory be a blessing. And a million hugs to his wonderful wife Margot Brandenburg

I will never forget the day a couple years ago when my friend Patrick Coughlin told me about what had happened to his mom.

I’ve known Patrick since 2012. For all the years I've known him he's been ahead of the curve on cybersecurity. An evangelist, an educator. This particular story from Patrick shook me. And, more importantly, it shook him.

Here's the short version: Patrick's mom answered the phone and heard what sounded exactly like his sister, screaming for help. Then a man came on the line: “we’ve got your daughter.” He demanded money. None of it was real. The voice was AI, the kidnapping invented, the whole thing generated and dialed at scale. And, like so many others, Patrick's mom very nearly paid anyway. Fortunately, she knew to verify. 

That experience sent Patrick down a two-year rabbit hole into how AI has quietly turned digital fraud into the fastest growing crime in the country. It became a book, and it is out now: “Dark Side of the Boom: How Scammers Hijacked the AI Revolution.” 

While the rest of us debate whether AI will save us or doom us, scammers already have the most powerful impersonation tool they have ever dreamed of. As Patrick puts it, there are now only two kinds of people: those who have been scammed, and those who are about to be. 

And Patrick is no armchair observer here. He has spent his career in cybersecurity, founding TruSTAR, selling it to Splunk, and running a massive team at Cisco. He even worked in counterterrorism for the U.S. government. Fifteen years defending governments and the Fortune 500. 

This book is what happened when he turned all of that expertise toward protecting the rest of us. Read it. 

Then send it to the people you love, starting with your parents. 

Congrats, Patrick. It is a great read, and a needed one.… more

Sean Neville and Matt Venables spent close to a decade building one of the most important pieces of financial infrastructure of our generation.

Together at Circle, they helped invent USDC. Sean architected the protocol. Matt led engineering and helped drive the launch. They built a dollar that actually behaves like a dollar on the internet.

So, when we learned what they wanted to build next at Catena, we paid close attention.

Here is the short version.

AI agents are about to become real economic actors. Not just assistants helping humans work faster. Systems that hold funds, execute payments, manage workflows, and operate on behalf of businesses around the clock.

But the financial system we have today assumes a human on the other end of every transaction. Someone logging in. Approving a payment. Authenticating an account.

That assumption breaks completely at machine speed.

We’ve been thinking a lot at Acrew about the convergence of two massive shifts: the dollar becoming internet-native via stablecoins, and the internet itself becoming agent-native via AI.

Catena is building the financial institution for that world.

A banking platform for agents. A control plane for the humans and businesses behind them. Verified identity, programmable payments, governance, compliance, and oversight designed from the ground up for AI-native transactions.

Very few teams could credibly attempt something this ambitious. Sean and Matt are two of the only people on the planet who deeply understand both stablecoin infrastructure and financial regulation at the level this requires.

Today, we’re proud to share that Acrew Capital co-led Catena's $30M Series A alongside Chris Dixon at a16z crypto. Along with the new capital, Catena also announced today that it has filed its application for an OCC National Trust Bank charter.

Sean, Matt, and team, honored to be in it with you.

Earlier this month, I took my nearly six-year-old daughter Bayley to the Peruvian Andes to see what her grandmother has been building for 18 years.

My mom, Martha Dudenhoeffer, founded a nonprofit called Maki International in 2008 in Ayacucho, one of the most remote and historically traumatized regions of Peru. "Maki" means "hands" in Quechua. The organization works across a handful of rural Andean communities, investing in education and entrepreneurship so families can build futures on their own terms.

On my last trip 8 years ago, I visited a school in Occollo, a subsistence farming village 11,000 feet high in the Andes, and the kids were visibly malnourished. Lethargic. Thin. Too anemic to focus in class. This time, kids from that same village ran up to greet Bayley. Energetic, engaged, curious. Their latest blood tests showed zero cases of anemia. Zero. Nutrition was never the endpoint. Daily meals, nutrition education for parents, and basic public health were the foundation that makes education, and everything after it, possible.

In a neighboring village, Bayley met Valentina, one of several women whose families have built small guinea pig farms with Maki's support. Bayley held the baby guinea pigs for as long as they'd let her. We did not explain what they were raised for. A few years ago, that business didn't exist here. Today these families are earning real income from it, and planning for something bigger. There's real demand for guinea pig supply at scale, and Maki is helping the town of Vinchos build a cooperative of farms to fuel their economy.

Bayley saw nearly 900 women across remote mountain villages earning independent income as embroiderers, artisans whose work now ships internationally. Women who, for generations, had lived only off subsistence farming.

And Bayley experienced, inside a women's prison, how her grandmother is the source of hope for women serving long sentences for nonviolent crimes tied to poverty. Women who have found in Maki a workshop community, income, dignity, and something to hold onto until release.

Most organizations chase scale. My mom chose depth. A handful of communities, decades of presence, real relationships. It's slower and quieter, and it's also why it works. Bayley may not have all the words for it yet, but she felt it. The hugs. The tears. The way everything shifted when Martita walked into a room. At one point she looked up at me and said, "Martita is the hero for all of these people."

The most meaningful impact isn't built in a moment. It's the product of showing up, year after year, for people the rest of the world tends to overlook.

If you know me, it's probably not surprising that my mom is an entrepreneur who has spent decades empowering other entrepreneurs. I cannot claim to have her depth of impact, but I will continue to draw inspiration from it.

So proud of you, Mom.

Nobody wants to talk about chasing invoices.

It's among the unsexiest parts of running a company. Past-due invoices, awkward follow-ups with customers who owe you money, reminder emails nobody opens. And threading the needle on how to get paid without churning your customer in the process. Every B2B company has the problem. The business doesn't run without effective invoice collection. And yet optimizing account receivables is often an afterthought because there's no glamour in it.

This is why I'm a little obsessed with what George Kurdin and Joe Zhou are doing at Monk.

Today Acrew Capital co-led Monk's Series A alongside our friends Nikhil Basu Trivedi and Mike Smith at Footwork, with our other friend Sheel Mohnot at Better Tomorrow Ventures. Monk is rebuilding accounts receivable from the ground up on frontier models, with AI agents doing the heavy lifting so revenue and GTM teams can focus on the distinctly human parts of their jobs.

The first time George walked me through the product, he didn't pitch. He took me through the actual mess: the slow-payers, the relationships that need a soft touch vs. a harder nudge, the nuance of tone and personalization in conversion. Founders who know the problem that deeply are rare. Teams that have demonstrated execution like Joe and George are rarer. And rarer still: the level of enthusiastic customer love that we heard from every customer with whom we spoke about Monk was palpable and inspiring. 

George and Joe, than you for inviting us on this journey with you.

Every ad impression, every recommendation algorithm, every retargeting pixel — all of it monetizes the space where human intent is unclear.

Agents collapse that space to zero.

An agent arrives with a mandate. Get me X. Budget Y. Constraints Z.

- No attention to capture. 
- No desire to manufacture. 
- No conversion funnel to optimize.

That's a different customer. And it needs a different internet.

- The attention economy monetizes the path to intent.
- The intention economy monetizes the *fulfillment of intent.*

At Tempo been building the protocol for that second economy — the Machine Payments Protocol, with Stripe, supported by Visa and others. Open source, designed for IETF standardization.

I've never really thought of myself as a person in finance.

Growing up, I did not imagine a career in financial services. I was in awe of people in finance for the way they seemed to have calculators in their brains and could make sense of the world in data. But I always considered myself more of a creative problem solver, someone who liked to help people bring their ideas to life. 

And when I think about about my career, I think of supporting founders and of the startup ecosystem - and to a certain extent of my own founder journey in starting Acrew Capital.

So being included in Barron's 2026 list of Most Influential Women Finance surprised me a bit. Until I thought about the founders that I've had the great privilege of working with and the remarkable impact they've had in reshaping financial services as an industry. As always, this is about them.

And in the spirit of this Barron's recognition, I'm especially thinking about the amazing women reshaping financial services with whom I'm so lucky to work. People like Jennifer Kuperman at Chime, Alexandra Mysoor at Alix, Claire Tomkins at Future Family, and Sima Gandhi...among many others.

What excites me most right now is how quickly the financial services landscape is changing. AI is reimagining financial services, from automating the services layer of finance to reshaping the infrastructure that underpins the industry. It will be fascinating to see how the financial system evolves and how lists like this reflect that change over the coming years.

Honored to be included alongside so many remarkable people.

And thank you to Reshma Kapadia for the thoughtful feature.… more

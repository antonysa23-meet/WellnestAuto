I'm excited to release my latest 'Labs to Launch' Substack series profiling Dr. Luis Sentis, faculty member at The University of Texas at Austin and a repeat entrepreneur, Part II -- an introduction to his latest startup, AIVE! 

The AI-Driven Drone Revolution is here—and it’s moving faster than most people realize.

We’re entering a world where drones are no longer remotely piloted tools, but fully autonomous, agentic systems capable of making decisions, navigating without GPS, and executing complex missions end-to-end.

The implications are massive:
• A projected $5T market for AI-powered geolocalization
 • Drone navigation systems growing 31.4% CAGR through 2029
 • Explosive demand across defense, disaster response, agriculture, and infrastructure

At the center of this shift is a critical problem:
 How do drones operate in GPS-denied environments—reliably, precisely, and at speed?

That’s exactly what Dr. Luis Sentis is solving with his newest venture, AIVE.
After building one of the leading humanoid robotics companies (Apptronik), Luis is now focused on what may be an even larger opportunity:
 creating the “AI brains” for autonomous drones.

AIVE’s breakthrough is deceptively simple—and incredibly hard to execute:
Enabling drones to move at high speed while continuously observing, interpreting, and mapping the world in real time—with precise geolocation, even in smoke, fog, or complete GPS denial. Think:
 • Border security at global scale
 • Autonomous disaster recovery in chaotic environments
 • Next-gen defense systems operating without reliance on GNSS

This is not just a drone company. It’s foundational infrastructure for the autonomous future.

Last night we had a great time co-hosting a panel on Capital & National Security: Investing in Defense and Dual-Use Innovation with the wonderful team at Orrick (Anik Guha, Neel Lilani, James (Jim) Jensen, Naomi Yankellow).

I also feel very fortunate to have shared the stage with such a thoughtful group of defense investors: Shahin Farshchi, Aidan Madigan-Curtis, Chris Moran, and Morgan Mahlock, who have collectively helped shape how venture capital engages with national security today.

My top takeaways: 

→ Defense investing has fundamentally shifted over the past decade
Geopolitics played a role, but we also discussed other key drivers including expansion of venture-scale outcomes, technical talent entering the sector, increased government openness to startups, and rebranding of defense innovation as a frontier technology category.

→ Power law vs innovation outliers remains an active debate
Should investors concentrate capital into likely category leaders, or continue backing technical outliers that could reshape the landscape? I appreciated that our panelists shared perspectives on both.

→ Technology progress is strong, but adoption remains a key challenge.
While pathways like DIU, AFWERX, and NavalX have improved startup access, the hardest challenge remains navigating the transition from pilots to programs of record and aligning with multi-year budget cycles.

→ Defense supply chains remain globally dependent
Many critical components are still sourced internationally, creating strategic vulnerabilities. This continues to be an area where we are actively looking for startups building enabling technologies that can compete on both cost and productivity.

Grateful for the discussion and everyone helping push this ecosystem forward. The strong turnout and engagement were a great reminder of the momentum building in this space.… more

Tim Bucher

  • 3rd+

CEO at Agtonomy | ex-Apple | ex-Microsoft | ex-Dell | Tech Entrepreneur and Farmer

3mo • 

This past week at CONEXPO-CON/AGG, I had the opportunity to speak on the “quiet benefits” of autonomy in industrial markets such as construction and agriculture.

After years of running autonomous Agtonomy-Enabled equipment commercially with real customers, what’s surprised me most is how quickly the secondary benefits show up when machines can perform repetitive tasks the same way every time with the utmost CONSISTENCY, under operator supervision acting as the “conductor” of a fleet of vehicles.

One benefit is fuel consumption dropping with autonomous solutions because there’s less idle time and fewer wasted passes. Operators spend less time exposed to dust, heat, vibration, and hazardous terrain which is yet another significant “quiet benefit.” I’ve spent long days in heavy equipment myself for many decades, so I know firsthand what that exposure feels like.

Training also changes. Novice team members can ramp faster because autonomous systems reinforce best practices - like “lane assist” in the automotive world. Now novice operators can get real work done in the field while training vs. spending hours/days/weeks in simulators. Equipment wear is also reduced as computers can run them more smoothly than manual operators which, in turn, reduces maintenance costs. Work quality becomes more consistent from operator to operator, shift to shift.

Labor savings may open the door, but CONSISTENCY is what creates the multiplier effect.

Autonomous machines are no longer theoretical. These machines are operating with real customers today, and the lessons are starting to translate across multiple industrial markets yielding more benefits we had thought when we began on this digital transformation journey of these amazing industrial markets.

It was great to be part of a session with leaders sharing REAL-world experiences shaping where construction technology is heading next. Thanks to Association of Equipment Manufacturers (AEM) with special shout-out to John Somers and Matthew Abeles for organizing the discussion, and to fellow speakers Ty Findley, Nick Espinosa, Colin Guinn, Zulqernain (ZULQ) Malik, Bharanidharan Rajakumar, and Vivin Hegde for the great conversations.

The full session is available here: https://lnkd.in/gkFWjahW. I join the panel around 48:43.

4mo • Edited • 


Last week I had the pleasure of speaking to a University of Southern California Engineering class on Disruptive Technologies led by Dr. Chrysostomos L Max Nikias (Past President of USC)  and Dr. Alice Liu.

I shared how we think about Industrial Automation 2.0 at Grit—AI transforming the physical world through embodied AI, robotics, and the compute stack behind them—and what it really takes to move breakthrough research from university labs into durable, revenue-generating companies.

We discussed:
·      The two brutal “valleys of death” in Deep Tech
·      Why hardware startups fail (and how to avoid it)
·      The shift from Automation 1.0 to AI-driven, adaptable robotics
·      Why ROI, focus, and integration matter more than hype

I was also joined by Dr. Clinton Smith, Co-Founder, CEO of RIOS Intelligent Machines, who brought factory-floor reality to the conversation with hard-earned go-to-market lessons.

My core message: Industrial automation and vertical robotics are creating some of the most meaningful technical careers of this generation. It’s still early—and the opportunity is enormous.

Grateful to Dr. Liu for her invitation to speak and to the USC students for the thoughtful discussion.

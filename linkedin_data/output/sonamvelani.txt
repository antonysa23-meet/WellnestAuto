About
I’ve always been fascinated by the machinery of cities - how infrastructure, technology, and policy shape the way we live. This passion led me to launch Streetlife Ventures, where we invest in startups at the intersection of cities and climate. We back entrepreneurs building the systems that will make urban life more sustainable, equitable, and resilient.

Over the course of my career, I’ve worked across the public and private sectors - Goldman Sachs, the World Bank, Zipline, and the NYC Mayor’s Office - to design and finance multi-billion-dollar investments in climate infrastructure, affordable housing, and inclusive economic growth. I specialize in bridging capital, policy, and technology to scale what works, with deep experience in structuring complex deals, leading public-private partnerships, and operating across global markets. 

My interest in urban development is grounded in my lived experience. I grew up in Chicago in a working-class family, and I understand first-hand the complicated ways that inadequate housing, limited transportation options, and environmental risks create structural barriers to upward mobility. The first in my family to go to college, I attended Harvard University on a full scholarship and studied Government, Economics, and Visual & Environmental Studies. Since then, I’ve made it my mission to tackle systemic inequities head on.

My work has taken me from financing the largest transit agency in the U.S., to deploying drones to deliver medicine in Africa, to building the first open-source infrastructure database in Asia. Whether it’s investing in carbon-neutral tech or reimagining how cities serve people, I’m focused on aligning innovation with financial returns and long-term impact.

I’m a strong believer in paying it forward and love being active in my community. I co-founded the Climate Tech Cities community, serve on the board of Scale for Climate Tech, and the selection committees of Activate, The Clean Fight, and Venture for Climate Tech. I'm also passionate about closing the gender gap and serve on the board of Women.NYC, volunteer with the Girl Scouts, and was honored with the President’s Volunteer Service Award.

Outside of work, you’ll find me Citibiking, museum hopping, watching reruns of the West Wing, and getting lost in new cities and new conversations - 70 countries, thousands of people, and still counting on both fronts!

Please send a message when connecting and stay in touch! www.sonamvelani.com

CTO @ Senpilot

2w • 

Con Edison is paying New York City renters cash to plug a microwave-sized battery into their window AC unit. Just a box, a wall outlet, and an app that handles everything automatically.

The battery charges overnight when grid demand is low, then runs the AC automatically during peak hours so the apartment stays cool without pulling from a grid that's already getting crushed by everyone else's AC running simultaneously. Participants get roughly a full July electric bill back in cash rebates.

I've been watching the virtual power plant conversation closely and it almost always assumes rooftop solar and homeownership. California's VPP program is over 200 megawatts, genuinely impressive, and almost entirely inaccessible to renters. New York City is 65% renters. The places with the worst grid stress during heat events are also the places where every distributed energy solution has historically required a deed to participate.

The program is growing from 200 kilowatts last year to 2 megawatts this summer, which is still tiny. But the model is what's interesting, because for the first time someone is building demand response around how most urban Americans actually live rather than assuming they own a house with a south-facing roof.… more

🙋🏻‍♀️ As we wrap up Women’s History Month, here’s to inspiring the next generation!!! So fun to be back on campus at Harvard University sharing my $0.02 of wisdom at the Girls Into VC conference at my alma mater. Sitting at the Science Center, looking out at a lecture hall full of young women in the same seats I sat in 20 (!) years ago, trying to figure out their own paths. I can tell you Gen Z is way more on top of it than us geriatric millennials ever were! 😅

Back then, I had no idea what venture capital was. I didn’t understand how the industry worked, and it didn’t feel like something that was “for me.” The numbers reflected that: In the 2000s, women made up 5% of partners at VC firms, and most firms had no women in decision-making roles at all. You only needed one hand to count the number of women GPs.

Fast forward and Girls Into VC brought together 400+ young women to fill the Science Center - investors, founders, and future leaders committed to expanding access to venture capital. These young women aren’t trying to figure out if they belong - they are there to run funds and build the next billion dollar companies! 🚀 A huge shoutout to Isabella Mandis and Sarah Phillips for building a global community of over 8,000 members spanning 60 countries and 6 continents (you go girls!). 🏃‍♀️

Today, women represent 15-20% of decision-makers in VC. Organizations like All Raise, PEWIN - Private Equity Women Investor Network, Global Women in VC, and Girls Into VC have helped build a real pipeline. We are still far from parity, but materially different from where things started.

It’s one of many reasons why Laura Fox and I started Streetlife Ventures in the first place - to move the needle on who gets funded, what gets built, and who gets to be in the room when those decisions are made. Or as Shirley Chisholm put it: “If you don’t find a seat at the table, bring a folding chair.” 🪑

And needless to say, we all stand on the shoulders of the women who came before us. The same weekend in Cambridge, Harvard Faculty of Arts and Sciences Dean Hopi Hoekstra brought together generations of alumnae for the Harvard Women’s Philanthropy Initiative Summit, focused on ensuring that future generations of women have more access, more support, and more of a shot as they take on the world.

Cities have always been humanity’s greatest engines of opportunity, invention, and community. Home to more than half of humanity today, the world’s cities will add another 2.5 billion people by 2050. Cities are critical to our past, present, and future.

The rapid growth of cities in an ever-warming world is the biggest challenge we face. From coastal floods in New York, to red skies in San Francisco, to unbearable heat waves in Mumbai, climate change is no longer theoretical. It’s now impacting the places we call home.

As we adapt to and mitigate the consequences of this changing reality, our cities are at the forefront of the race against climate change. Approximately 75% of the urban infrastructure of 2050 is yet to be built, representing a $5 trillion market opportunity. How we design our built environment - our buildings, transportation systems, waste management processes, food chains, power grids, and public spaces - will have a profound influence on the livability of our communities.

At Streetlife Ventures, we are the ultimate optimists and are excited to work with founders and policymakers to build the future we want to live in.

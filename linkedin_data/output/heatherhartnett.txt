About
Heather Hartnett is a General Partner and CEO of Human Ventures, where she and her team have created one of New York’s premiere startup studios, incubators and early stage venture funds. Prior to founding Human, Heather’s venture capital career spanned across various roles at firms including Lightspeed Venture Partners, CityLight Capital, and Claremont Creek Ventures. Previously, Heather led the development and operations at the David Lynch Foundation, where she worked with some of the country's largest family offices and investment institutions. She's a graduate of the Kauffman Fellowship Program and serves on the board of Transact Global, Trilantic Capital Partners Founder’s Council, AARP’s Innovation Council, and Summit Impact.

Heather is active in the community, serving on the leadership board of tech: nyc and Transact Global. She has been listed as one of The 50 Most Influential Women in America by Hearst Digital Media Publications, is a frequent speaker at TechCrunch Disrupt, and is a regular contributor to Forbes, where she covers a range of venture capital topics.

The First Annual Human Attention Summit just wrapped and it's hard to describe how incredible it felt.

It all started with Joe Marchese, who is genuinely one of the most brilliant minds when it comes to capturing and holding human attention. People kept saying the whole event felt like being in Joe's living room or even inside his head. Warm, electric, full of people who are both incredibly smart and incredibly real. That's Joe's superpower, and it's the premise we built Human Ventures on.

The theme of the summit itself was pretty simple. The most valuable thing in the world right now isn't data or distribution. It's Attention. We gathered 185+ carefully curated Humans who are instrumental to shaping what that means for the future.

Matthew Prince on who really controls the internet. Arthur Sadoun and Takeshi Numoto on creativity and commerce. Esther Perel on relationships and connection in the age of AI. Ari Kastrati, Neil Vogel, Carolyn Everson, and Reed Rayman on where capital and culture meet in the experience economy. Dan Nathan, Ed Elson and Sonali Basak about the impact on financial markets. Filmmaker, Darren Aronofsky and Frank Cooper III on why storytelling might be the last truly human act... and the list goes on.

But the programming was only part of it!

What makes a summit worth showing up for is the quality of the Humans in the room. Real conversations, around topics that actually matter, with just the right amount of fun woven through (thanks mentalist Max Major). Game changing art with Angelo Sotira and Layer. Salon dinners the night before at some of New York's best restaurants. Sadelle's bagels with Stuzzi Hot Sauce and Raf's pastries between sessions. Truly the only summit I've ever been to that had a custom cocktail program throughout (thanks to Jeff Bell, Meredith Erickson, Alison Roman, Richard Betts and Tequila Komos and Doladira).

"Good hospitality is how you create the conditions for good conversation. Get that right and everything else follows."

Big thanks to the incredible Humans, Jessica W. and Danielle McLaughlin for creating a new type of event that just raised the bar yet again.

See you next year.

Reshared by Heather Hartnett

Had a great conversation with Zoe van den Bol on the #DrivingAlpha podcast about what we've been building at Human Ventures. Some takeaways from our conversation:

1. "Human Alpha" is the real differentiator
I've always been surprised that the name "Human" wasn't taken when we started Human.vc. This whole industry is based on people—not just technology. I call it "picking stock in people."

2. Why we're long New York
The network mindset here is unmatched. Every customer, every major company, people at all stages—all within reach. New York founders intuitively understand the power of connection.

3. Meet founders where the needs are
Ten years ago, founders needed a place to build together. So we started a startup studio. As companies succeeded, the fund came naturally. We've evolved by listening.

4. Relationships > transactions
Barry Diller once said: "You're only as good as your latest response." It's not about how many people you know—it's about whether they actually want to respond when you reach out.

5. What I'm betting on
Tech is finally catching up to help us build better relationships. Natural language processing, understanding networks, surfacing meaningful connections—this is where it gets interesting. (Check out Goodword and stay tuned for human.ai 👀)

Hot take: Email will be dead in 5 years (or as we know it today). The only things that cut through are warm intros and real relationships.

My advice? Go to and host more in-person events (right, Joe Marchese and Danielle McLaughlin?). The premium on real human interaction is going up.

Thanks Zoe for the thoughtful conversation 🙏

Good food, good humans, good connections. That's what last night was all about.

Last night we hosted a tasting for Stuzzi Hot Sauce – the newest brand from Casa Komos Brands Group – and it exceeded every expectation.

Stuzzi's co-founder Carla Rza Betts, explained that it is hot sauce made the right way: just three ingredients, made in Italy, zero pretense. In Italian,  stuzzicare means "to stimulate, to excite, to tease," and that's exactly what it does.

We paired Stuzzi with wood-fired pizza, oysters, softshell crab sliders, and fried chicken at Chef Ryan Hardy's Pasquale Jones in Soho, NYC, where he let us take over the whole restaurant.

What struck me most was watching the room. When you create something genuinely exceptional, the right people find their way to your table. Industry leaders, food enthusiasts, CPG Investors, and discerning palates – brands that refuse to compromise on quality will always find their tribe. 

Sometimes the best validation doesn't come from what you say about your product, but from who chooses to shut down their restaurant to celebrate it with you!

I went to LA last week to give a talk at the Milken Institute Global Conference about how loneliness has become a greater mortality risk than smoking or obesity, and how the next wave of longevity investing has to include the infrastructure of human connection, not just biotech.

Then I spent four days living the thesis of the talk in real time. Investor dinners with my favorite GPs from LA, SF, and NYC. A unique dinner on the future of human connection with Whitney Wolfe Herd and Dr. Arthur Brooks. Arguably the best party of the week hosted by Annie Luchsinger and Alex Pall on the roof deck at Dantes. A lunch I co-hosted with the brilliant Kathryn O'Connor Gardner with 14 extraordinary women who, by the end of the meal, had become real friends rather than just contacts.

Time in between with long-time friends like Thomas McLeod and Danielle Mantich, who introduced me to new friends like Molly Burnett. And of course quality time with my long-time business partner Joe Marchese, which is its own kind of medicine.

The research keeps telling us what some of us already know... that connection isn't a soft skill or a nice-to-have. It's a physiological need with measurable effects on how long and how well we live. 

My talk argued that connection is a venture strategy. The week reminded me it's also a happiness strategy and that you can't outsource work to a screen and expect it to be as productive or fulfilling. IRL is the new alpha.

Grateful to everyone who showed up in person and made the week what it was.

2mo • 

Deloitte just cut its paid parental leave in half. A lot of CFOs are about to learn that this was the most expensive "savings" decision they ever made.

The US is already the only developed country without federal paid parental leave. 1/4 of American women are back at work within 2 weeks of giving birth. 1/3 leave the workforce entirely when they don't have the time and support they need, and many stay out for 5 years or more. 

That's the baseline we're starting from. And now two of the country's most prominent employers are making it worse.

Deloitte is taking their paid parental leave program from 16 weeks down to 8 for their internal support roles. They're getting rid of a $50,000 reimbursement that covered adoption, surrogacy, and IVF. They're cutting PTO by up to 10 days and ending pension accruals. 

At Zoom, birthing parents went from getting 22–24 weeks of leave down to 18, and non-birthing parents went from 16 weeks down to 10. 

All of this while Deloitte's US business grew 8% last year to $35.7 billion in revenue. Zoom's full-year revenue crossed $4.6 billion with operating cash flow growing almost 22%. Neither of these companies is cutting benefits because they can't afford them. They're cutting because the labor market has shifted, and they've decided they can get away with it.

We should call this what it is: it's a rollback of parental support, and the cost is going to land hardest on mothers.

If Deloitte and Zoom are the first to make decisions like this, they won't be the last and every company that follows is going to push more parents into choosing between their careers and their families, at a moment when daycare already costs more than rent in most major cities.

Cutting parental leave doesn't just take something away from parents. It tells them exactly where they stand.… more

Media is a relationship business. And relationships still get built in person, which I think our industry continues to underestimate and undervalue.

Last week I had the good fortune (see what I did there?) to attend Meredith Klein's legendary "Friday Media Lunch", co-hosted by Amanda Coffee (Coffee Communications), and featuring Fortune's Allie Garfinkle. It was a great reminder of why IRL still matters.

Allie said it herself. Her best interviews, like Brad Lightcap (OpenAI) during Code Red and Natasha Lyonne, came from being in the room at Fortune Brainstorm. Not cold outreach but by showing up.

Of course, one of these things was not like the others. I was the only VC at the table and that's usually how I like it. The most valuable relationships I've built are the ones that cross industry lines. Comms leaders see patterns VCs miss, and vice versa.

Huge respect to Meredith and Amanda for what they continue to do for this industry. These rooms don't happen by accident, and the quality of comms industy is better for the work they put in to convene people.

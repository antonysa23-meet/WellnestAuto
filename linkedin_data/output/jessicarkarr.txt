About
*** Please send pitches via the form on coyote.ventures website, I DO NOT read pitches on LinkedIn or via InMail ***

Founder & Managing Director of Coyote Ventures, investing in tech-enabled solutions that drive access to healthcare for overlooked populations, including in women's health. Former biochemist, early Impossible Foods employee (#12), and impact-focused investor. Open water swimmer, certified yoga instructor, Pom mom.

Coyote backs companies that integrate into real-world healthcare ecosystems, demonstrate measurable ROI and patient impact, and are built by founders with deep domain expertise and lived experience. Our investments span digital health, healthcare services, consumer and diagnostics.

My path has been shaped by science, product-building, and impact-driven investing. I’m a former biochemist with an MS in Biochemistry from UC San Diego, where my research in the Dorrestein Lab developed novel mass spectrometry techniques to study interspecies communication in the microbiome. I went on to work at Impossible Foods, where I spent over six years in R&D and product development helping bring the Impossible Burger from idea to public launch. I completed an MBA focused on impact and systems thinking from Presidio Graduate School. I’ve advised early-stage startups across food, health, and wellness, and helped build the strategy and operations for an impact-focused VC fund. 

I also convene the Health Equity Innovators Summit: a curated forum for innovators, funders, healthcare leaders, and policy leaders shaping the future of equitable care. Learn more and attend: https://www.healthequityinnovators.com/

Founders: submit deals at https://www.coyote.ventures/

Open to thoughtful conversations with founders and leaders working on meaningful systems change!

Why do so many conferences, summits, retreats miss the mark on facilitating collaborations amongst attendees?

On one end of the spectrum, most events are too content heavy.

They pack an agenda full of fancy CEOs who understand industry problems at a high level but aren't in the weeds with the solutions. They make statements like "healthcare costs are too high" without going into what can be done to solve it.

The women's health echo chamber will continuously repeat the same statistics - "women were excluded from clinical trials until 1993" - but we've been hearing these talking points for years. They emphasize to an already bought-in audience why they should care - a completely missed opportunity.

On the other end of the spectrum, some retreat-focused events are ungrounded and fail to meet people where their at - especially people who already have deep personal development trajectories. 

I work hard to produce mindful events that I wish already existed. Where we integrate thoughtful, multi-disciplinary speakers with experiences for all participants to connect on a deeper level. Where we recognize intersectionality in improving the health for women and underserved populations towards a healthier future for all. Where we can move past the "why" and collaborate on innovations. 

And it's working - real collaborations are already in motion from our last gathering!
-An entire new startup has formed from two participants who met at the gathering
-One speaker is now an advisor to a company who attended
-Coyote Ventures hired a Venture Partner who attended 

We didn't anticipate planning two events this year, but we want to keep the juices flowing. Join us for our upcoming Health Equity Innovators Fall Gathering and meet this horse & puppy - link in the comments!

The roundtable sessions at this year's Health Equity Innovators Summit were where a lot of the most honest conversations happened - this was by design.

We ran seven simultaneous sessions covering the policy landscape, early-stage capital for Medicaid-focused digital health, fintech as a driver of health outcomes, the build/buy/partner decision in AI healthtech, community co-design, a branding bootcamp, and what it actually takes to move from pilot to health system partnership. Each one was led by someone doing the work, not just observing it, including Shruti Kothari of Luminarity Solutions, Tahira Dosani of Resilience VC, Yichen I. F. of Acumen America, Kathryn Heffernan of UPMC Enterprises, Emily F Peters of Common Unbold, Priyanka Vaidya of Kiara Health, and Mary Katica of IDEO.org.

What came out of these conversations may look different for each person who participated, but a few things surfaced across multiple sessions. Founders who understand the policy cycle and use it as a strategic lever are in a fundamentally different position than those who react to it after the fact. The tension between proving impact and building sustainable unit economics in Medicaid is real, and it deserves more honest conversation in the investment community than it typically gets. And community co-design is not a methodology you layer on at the end. It changes who has authorship over the solutions being built, and that matters.

One of the sessions I keep coming back to from this year's Health Equity Innovators Summit was the conversation between Dr. Sarita Mohanty, MD, MPH, MBA, President and CEO of The SCAN Foundation and Dr. Pooja Mittal, Chief Health Equity Officer.

They led a session on what it actually means to design health innovations with communities genuinely at the center, as co-authors of the solutions being built for them. What grounded the conversation was a lifespan approach to care, rooted in the idea that the health of an individual is inextricably linked to their family, their social environment, and the systems around them. They shared how they collaborated on a digital hub for community health workers that takes local impact and scales it into something that changes the ecosystem, not just the individual.

The moment that landed hardest was when they named something the field doesn't like to admit: pilotitis. Too many promising interventions never escape small-scale demonstration mode. We keep proving things work and then failing to build the structural conditions that let them grow. The SCAN Foundation is pushing for a universal long-term services and supports benefit that would provide home care for all, regardless of asset level, which is a structural answer to a structural problem.

Sarita and Pooja are doing the kind of work that deserves serious attention because it operates at the policy and systems level rather than the product level. Grateful they brought it into the room.

Mika Eddy still has the spreadsheet.

The spreadsheet breaks down the $100,000 billed for her own pregnancy and delivery, with $20,000 billed to her directly out of pocket. She was a director at UnitedHealthcare - she understood every billing code, and still couldn't escape it. 

That gap is the founding philosophy of Malama Health. We invested before they had clinical outcomes data. We didn't need it because we had Mika.

Malama announced a $9.2M seed round earlier this year - Coyote Ventures is proud to continue to support Malama alongside Acumen America, Wisdom Ventures, and Capital F. The outcomes data exists now: 45% fewer preterm births, 19% fewer NICU admissions, a Tufts RCT, and an NIH grant. Medicaid-first was never a constraint: it was the thesis.

We were honored to have Uché Blackstock, MD open this year's Health Equity Innovators Summit in Tiburon.

Dr. Blackstock is a physician, the founder of Advancing Health Equity, and the author of Legacy: A Black Physician Reckons with Racism in Medicine. Her book, a New York Times Bestseller, is an unflinching personal and professional reckoning with racism in a system that she has dedicated her career to changing from the inside.

At the summit, she spoke about what she has witnessed firsthand: the specific and disproportionate toll COVID-19 took on Black and brown communities, the way data lags put these patients at a compounding disadvantage over time, and what it actually takes for healthcare organizations to transform how they lead and deliver care to overlooked populations. She was direct, she was generous with her story, and she invited others in the room to share theirs.

Health equity work attracts people who care deeply. But caring isn't enough without the kind of clear-eyed, evidence-backed critique that Dr. Blackstock brings. She challenged the room in a way that felt necessary, and the conversation was better for it.

If you haven't read Legacy, start there. And if you're building in this space, her organization Advancing Health Equity is worth following closely.

The room Jessica Karr built this week was something special.

Big enough to hold real diversity. Clinicians, investors, policymakers, founders, all bringing different mental models into the same room. And small enough that you actually met everyone. And the caliber of people? Every single person in that room could have been a speaker.

The Health Equity Innovators Summit was one of those rare gatherings where the conversations in between sessions are just as sharp as the ones on stage. Shoutout to Jessica Karr and the Coyote Ventures team for creating and holding that space. 💙

Here's what stayed with me:

𝗧𝗵𝗲 𝗻𝗮𝗿𝗿𝗮𝘁𝗶𝘃𝗲 𝗽𝗿𝗼𝗯𝗹𝗲𝗺 𝗶𝘀 𝘀𝘁𝗶𝗹𝗹 𝗮 𝗽𝗿𝗼𝗯𝗹𝗲𝗺, 𝗮𝗻𝗱 𝗶𝘁'𝘀 𝗹𝗲𝗮𝘃𝗶𝗻𝗴 𝗽𝗲𝗼𝗽𝗹𝗲 𝗯𝗲𝗵𝗶𝗻𝗱. Too much othering. Too many point solutions layered onto fundamentally under-resourced systems. Sarita Mohanty, MD, MPH, MBA named it directly: "the forgotten middle population." The people whose needs sit just outside every coverage bucket. We have to think more upstream.

𝗦𝗰𝗮𝗹𝗶𝗻𝗴 𝗿𝗲𝗾𝘂𝗶𝗿𝗲𝘀 𝗽𝘂𝗯𝗹𝗶𝗰 𝗮𝗻𝗱 𝗽𝗿𝗶𝘃𝗮𝘁𝗲 𝗱𝗼𝗹𝗹𝗮𝗿𝘀 𝘄𝗼𝗿𝗸𝗶𝗻𝗴 𝘁𝗼𝗴𝗲𝘁𝗵𝗲𝗿. The The SCAN Foundation and Health Net ‘s Pear Suite Partnership is a model worth studying. Community health workers as a benefit, not a nice-to-have. With HR-1 cuts to Medicaid looming, the case for equity has to be made on moral AND economic grounds simultaneously. 

𝗙𝗼𝗹𝗹𝗼𝘄 𝘁𝗵𝗲 𝗽𝗼𝗹𝗶𝗰𝘆. I led a roundtable on what health plan and system policy agendas actually mean for health equity and digital health scale. They determine whether solutions get to scale at all. The room had a lot to say.

𝗧𝗵𝗲 𝗹𝗮𝗻𝗱𝘀𝗰𝗮𝗽𝗲 𝗶𝘀 𝘀𝗵𝗶𝗳𝘁𝗶𝗻𝗴, 𝗳𝗮𝘀𝘁. Alana Troutt, Taylor Priestley , Kapil Dhingra, MD, MBA, FACEP and Shika Pappoe MD, MPH, MBA unpacked what that actually means on the ground: for payers, providers, and the communities caught in between. The systems are changing whether we're ready or not. The question is whether we're shaping the change or chasing it.

𝗔𝗻𝗱 Uché Blackstock, MD 𝗹𝗮𝗻𝗱𝗲𝗱 𝘁𝗵𝗶𝘀: "Health is not just a cost. It's investable."

That reframe matters more than ever right now.

Somehow the most iconic photo from the summit is Angie. Pooja Mittal and Sarita Mohanty, MD, MPH, MBA’s panel in full swing behind her, looking like she also had thoughts on the forgotten middle population. 🐾

One of the best parts of the whole thing was looking around the room and seeing so many people I've been in the equity trenches with for years. Catching up with old friends who are still showing up, still building. That part never gets old.

Grateful to have been in the room.

Integrating '-omics' and natural product discovery platforms to investigate metabolic exchange in microbiomes.

Current Opinions in Microbiology · Feb 1, 2011

Show publication
The microbiome is an abundance of microorganisms within a host (e.g. human microbiome). These microorganisms produce small molecules and metabolites that have been shown to affect and dictate the physiology of an individual. Functional knowledge of these molecules, often produced for communication or defense, will reveal the interplay between microbes and host in health and disease. The vast diversity in structure and function of microbiome-associated small molecules necessitate tools that will utilize multiple '-omics' strategies to understand the interactions within the human microbiome. This review discusses the importance of these investigations and the integration of current '-omics' technologies with tools established in natural product discovery in order to identify and characterize uncharacterized small molecules in the effort towards diagnostic modeling of the human microbiome.… more

A Hinge Region cis-Proline in Ribonuclease A Acts as a Conformational Gatekeeper for C-Terminal Domain Swapping.

Journal of Molecular Biology · May 13, 2010

Show publication
Domain swapping, the process in which a structural unit is exchanged between monomers to create a dimer containing two versions of the monomeric fold, is believed to be an important mechanism for oligomerization and the formation of amyloid fibrils. Structural studies have implicated proline as an important residue for domain swapping due to its increased frequency in hinge regions preceding swapped arms. We hypothesized that prolines unique ability to populate both cis and trans peptide bond conformations may allow proline to act as a conformational gatekeeper, regulating interconversion between monomer and domain-swapped dimer forms. The hinge region of RNase A contains a proline at residue 114 that adopts a cis conformation in the monomer and extends to a trans conformation in the C-terminal domain-swapped dimer. Substitution of P114 with residues that strongly prefer a trans peptide bond (Ala, Gly) results in significant population of the C-terminal domain-swapped dimer under near-physiological conditions (pH 8.0, 37°C). This is in stark contrast to dimerization of wild-type RNase A, which requires incubation under extreme conditions such as lyophilization from acetic acid or elevated temperature. In addition, we observe similar results when cis-P114 is mutated to glycine in a homologous RNase, human pancreatic RNase 1. Our results suggest isomerization at P114 may facilitate population of a partially unfolded intermediate or alternative structure competent for domain swapping, and provide support for a more general role for proline isomerization as a conformational gatekeeper in domain swapping and oligomerization.… more

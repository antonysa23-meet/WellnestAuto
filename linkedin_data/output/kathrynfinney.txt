About
Entrepreneurship is my vocation. 

Over two decades ago, I started transforming how bold ideas become breakthrough companies. Founded one of the first women-led online media companies in 2002 and scaled it to exit. FIRST: influencer to have a major book deal, female influencer on national morning show, one of the First Black woman to sell a startup.

digitalundivided: Founded this groundbreaking organization. Built a 40+ person team for the groundbreaking social enterprise, generating seven-figure annual revenue. Created the "#ProjectDiane" research that literally changed the face of Venture Capital. The report became the gold standard for diversity data in venture capital.

Genius Guild: Launched a multi-million dollar investment fund focused on investing in high growth startups led by underrepresented entrepreneurs and emerging fund managers, deploying capital where traditional VCs/LPs wouldn't look. Portfolio includes: Backpack Health, Health in Her Hue, Refashiond VC, Capitalized VC. 

BUILD Universe: Created an AI-powered platform that takes people from zero to launched business in minutes. Making entrepreneurship accessible to entrepreneurs who don't fit the typical founder profile.

Post

Five months ago I started writing about a thesis I keep coming back to: Everybody is now an Entrepreneur. Entepreneurship, right or wrong, is now not an option, it's becoming THE option. 

Now, I've never been one to push people towards #entrepreneurship as it's probably the hardest career path one can choose. However, we're living in the time of mega-layoffs and mass reductions in the sectors where the jobs aren't coming back. 

Yes, there's jobs in other sectors, especially highly skilled "blue collar" jobs in construction, electrical work, etc.  ➡️ But how many mid-career (insert university here) grads working at (insert fortune 500 company) are going to be able to make the transition to these jobs? 

My thesis, everybody's now an entrepreneur, comes from a reading of the actual labor market data. The structural shift from employment to self-directed work has been running for three years, and the numbers underneath it are not subtle.

I am naming the five kinds of entrepreneur I see playing out across that data The full essay (and the quiz that maps you to your Prototype) is in my Substack (link in comments).:

The Forced Founder. You did not pick the timeline. The layoff did.

The Founding Team Member. You joined someone else's build. You think like an owner without being one.

The Solopreneur. You are the product. One person, one offer, full sovereignty.

The Intrapreneur. You are inside a company building something they did not budget for and did not plan to need. Founder-level pressure on employee-level pay.

The Freedom Founder. You chose this deliberately. Not running from something. Running toward something. For autonomy, creativity, and life design. 

Comment your Prototype below. Or comment the one you are between. I am reading every reply.

Here’s the truth most won’t tell you about entrepreneurship:
🚀 Hustle can build companies.
💔 But it can also destroy the person who dreamed them up.

The real flex? Building a life you don’t have to recover from.

In September 2023, I was keynoting major conferences, running my own venture fund, being a super mom…
and driving myself straight into the ER. 🚑

Blood pressure: 190/120. Hypertensive Crisis
Diagnosis: Unsustainable.
Prognosis from my doctor: “You’ll be dead in two years if you keep going like this.”

I had spent a lifetime building & funding companies, movements, and ecosystems. But I hadn’t built the one thing that mattered most:

Myself.

So I made a decision that terrified me more than any investor pitch or startup launch:
👉🏾 I paused.
🔹 I canceled everything non-essential.
🔹 I rested — for the first time…EVER.
🔹 I focused on my health, my family, and my joy.
🔹 I learned to surf, ran 5k races, lifted heavyweights, invested in new ideas, rebuilt my life.

And by Jan 2025, something wild happened:

I didn’t just recover. I became the strongest, happiest, and most creative version of myself — at 49 years old. 🎯

The damn thing I built… was me.

If you get the chance — or are forced — to pause, take it.
You don’t just owe it to yourself.
You owe it to everything you’re trying to build.
#Entrepreneurship #FounderLife #Leadership #Resilience #RestAsRebellion #WellnessJourney

When Dave Chappelle got his first Comedy Central deal, he told his father, expecting a celebration. His father took a breath and said: name your price now.

A few years later, Dave walked away from a fifty million dollar deal.

⭐️ He could walk away because the line was already drawn. Most founders get tested first and try to draw the line in the middle of the test. By then it's too late. The pressure is on, the people in the room are watching, and you make the decision the moment wants instead of the one you'd actually want.

In Build the Damn Thing I write that you'll be tested in three places: your bank account, your personal life, and your mental health. They're not three problems. They're three places the same problem shows up, where the work asks you for something you shouldn't give.

⭐️ So name the line now, in all three, before you're tested in any. Pull your values. For each pressure, write one specific line, specific enough that it holds under pressure. Then write the walk-away sentence: I am willing to walk away from X for Y. Put it where you'll see it on a day you don't feel strong enough to draw it from scratch.

The decision is already made when the moment arrives.

If you don't name your values, someone else will name them for you. Your first investor will. Your first big client will. So will the first hard quarter. You will not like what they pick.

⭐️ Personal core values aren't a wall poster or an HR exercise. They're a North Star. They tell you who to hire, who to take money from, and when to walk away from a deal that would change your life.

A few years ago, several million dollars of funding came across my desk with conditions that compromised the integrity of the organization and my own. The money was real. The partner was respected. I sat with it for three weeks. Then I walked away (slowly, because it was game-changing money). Less than two years later, the same kind of money came back, without the conditions.

The walk-away wasn't inspiration. It was recognition. The line was already drawn, in a note on my phone, in language I'd spent years sharpening. When the test came, all I had to do was point at it.

That's what naming your values gives you: the decision is already made when the moment arrives. You spend the pressure of the moment executing the decision, not making it.

➡️ Here's the start of the exercise. Open a blank page and write twenty actions and attributes you most respect in people you've actually worked with — not what they have, what they do. Comes prepared. Tells the truth. Pays on time. Don't edit. Just write twenty. Then cluster them into three or four themes, and name each theme in one word. That's your first draft.

Mine are Integrity, Empathy, Clarity, and Fearlessness. The full five-step version, and how each one has been doing the work for years, is in the newsletter. Link in the comments

⭐️ Lesson learned: AI can do it. But AI doesn't know it.

I know how to build products. I've built and scaled companies, raised real money, written books, influenced a generation of diverse founders. And I still made the oldest mistake there is- trusting someone (or my case something) else to build my vision #buildthedamnthing

The QA didn't work. The code kept breaking. Every week I was bleeding cash into something that wasn't taking shape. So I sat down and reread my own book.

➡️ In Build the Damn Thing, I tell the story of the dry cleaners my family bought while we were grieving my father. We hired very expensive consultants, including the former CEO of IKEA, who charged us over six figures to essentially tell us nothing. Note: CEOs of major corporations rarely know how to actual build a business from zero. 

If you don't know what you need, no consultant (or AI) will know either, and they will gladly charge you for the not-knowing.

Twenty years later I was running the same play on my own platform, with AI instead of an over priced former CEO. So I stopped. Took the breath. Closed the laptop, opened a blank doc, and rebuilt the framework myself. Slowly. Like someone who had done it before. Because I had.

➡️ Here's the takeaway, and it applies to you the same way it applied to me. Before you bring in any outside help — a developer, a consultant, an AI tool — ask one question: have I done enough of this myself to know what I'm asking for?

If the answer is no, sit with it. Open the blank doc. Get clear first. The clarity is what you're actually paying for when you hire. If you have it, every dollar works for you. If you don't, every dollar pays someone else to figure out what you don't know,  the most expensive line item in any startup.

There's always a gap between the idea in your head and the idea that exists in the world and AI is starting to close that gap.

➡️ The build-your-own-app era is real. Most people are stalling at the idea-in-my-head stage anyway. Not because the technology is hard, but because articulating clearly is hard.

My Substack essay this week walks through three AI exercises you can do this weekend. 

The summary is below. Full essay is at kathrynfinney.com/insights has the actual prompts.

Exercise 1: Prompt-to-PRD. Turn your idea into a one-page product spec. AI does the structuring; you do the thinking.

Exercise 2: Prompt-to-customer-interview-script. Generate the 8 to 10 questions you should be asking five potential customers before you build anything.

Exercise 3: Prompt-to-landing-page. Hero, subheadline, three benefits, one CTA. Test demand before you write code.

The build-your-own-app era does not require you to write code. It requires you to articulate clearly. AI handles the rest.

If you have been carrying an idea around for two years, five years, fifteen years, this is the weekend to drag it out of your head.
